# Rural Development 101

> *Rural development is the process of improving the quality of life and economic well-being of people living in relatively isolated and sparsely populated areas.*

---

## The Big Picture

While cities cover only a tiny fraction of the Earth's land, **rural areas** cover almost everything else. 
- These are the forests, pastures, and agricultural fields that feed the cities. 
- Without rural food production, urban life would be impossible.

To understand rural space, geographers look at **how farmers choose to use their land**. 
- Why do we grow delicate vegetables close to cities, while cattle ranches are located hundreds of miles away in the middle of nowhere? 
- How does the transition from farming to manufacturing change rural communities?

This article introduces agricultural systems, Von Thünen's spatial model of land use, and the Green Revolution.

---

## Agricultural Systems: Intensive vs. Extensive

Geographers classify farming based on the amount of labor and capital invested relative to the land area:

- **Intensive Agriculture:** Farmers must expend a relatively large amount of effort or capital to produce the maximum yield from a small piece of land. (e.g., wet rice farming in Asia, or commercial market gardening). Located closer to populations.
- **Extensive Agriculture:** Farming that uses relatively small amounts of labor and capital relative to the large area of land being farmed. (e.g., cattle ranching, or large-scale wheat farming). Located far from populations.

## Von Thünen's Land-Use Model

In 1826, Johann Heinrich von Thünen noticed a spatial pattern in how crops were arranged around a city. He created a model showing that **land use is determined by transportation costs to the market**:

```
                       ┌─────────────────────────┐
                       │   VON THÜNEN'S RINGS    │
                       └────────────┬────────────┘
                                    │
         ┌──────────────┬───────────┴───────────┬──────────────┐
         ▼              ▼                       ▼              ▼
┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐
│   CITY NODE    ││    RING 1      ││    RING 2      ││    RING 3      │
│  The Central   ││ Dairy & Market ││ Forestry / Wood││ Grains / Crops │
│    Market      ││ Gardening      ││ (Heavy to      ││ (Light weight, │
│                ││ (Spoils fast)  ││  transport)    ││  easy to ship) │
└────────────────┘└────────────────┘└────────────────┘└────────────────┘
```
*(Beyond Ring 3: **Ring 4 (Animal Grazing)**—cattle require vast land and can walk themselves to the market, minimizing transport costs).*

- **The Principle:** Land closest to the city is the most expensive. Therefore, farmers must use it for intensive, high-profit products that spoil quickly (like milk or fresh berries). Land far away is cheap, making it ideal for extensive grain farming or grazing.

## The Green Revolution: Boosting Yields

In the mid-20th century, the **Green Revolution** transformed global agriculture:
- **What it was:** The development of high-yield seeds (especially wheat and rice), chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and advanced irrigation systems.
- **The Impact:** It prevented worldwide famine, allowing food production to keep pace with the exploding global population. (See [Population101](./Population101.md)).
- **The Cost:** It led to environmental issues, including chemical runoff polluting water, soil erosion, and increased debt for small farmers who could not afford the expensive seeds and fertilizers.

---

## Key Terms

- **Rural Area:** A sparsely populated region outside cities and towns.
- **Intensive Agriculture:** Farming using high labor/capital on small plots.
- **Extensive Agriculture:** Farming using low labor/capital on huge tracts of land.
- **Von Thünen Model:** Model explaining agricultural rings based on transport costs.
- **Green Revolution:** Mid-20th-century agricultural technology boom.
- **Bid-Rent Theory:** Theory explaining that land price drops as you move away from the city center.
