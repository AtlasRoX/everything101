# Climate Migration 101

> *Climate migration is the movement of people who are forced to leave their home region due to sudden or progressive changes in their local environment that adversely affect their lives or living conditions.*

---

## The Big Picture

If a hurricane destroys a coastal town, residents are displaced temporarily. But what happens if the sea level rises permanently, submerging the town under water?
- What happens if years of severe drought turn fertile farmland into a desert, making it impossible to grow food or find drinking water?

This is **Climate Migration** (or environmental displacement).

Unlike historic migrations (which were driven primarily by economic opportunities), climate migration is driven by **ecological survival**. 

As global temperatures rise, changing weather patterns, rising oceans, and desertification are rendering entire regions of the Earth uninhabitable, forcing millions of people to relocate.

This article introduces the triggers of climate migration, the most vulnerable geographical zones, and the legal gap in international refugee law.

---

## Triggers of Environmental Displacement

Climate migration is caused by two types of environmental events:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │    CLIMATE TRIGGERS     │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                            ┌──────────────────┐
│  SUDDEN-ONSET    │                                            │  SLOW-ONSET      │
│  (Rapid Events)  │                                            │ (Gradual Decay)  │
│ - Hurricanes     │                                            │ - Sea-level rise │
│ - Flash floods   │                                            │ - Desertification│
│ - Mudslides      │                                            │ - Soil erosion   │
│ - Wildfires      │                                            │ - Water scarcity │
└──────────────────┘                                            └──────────────────┘
```

- **Sudden-Onset Hazards:** Disasters that strike without warning. They cause rapid, massive displacement, but residents can often return home to rebuild once the event ends.
- **Slow-Onset Hazards:** Gradual, environmental degradation that takes place over decades. Once the environment crosses an ecological threshold (e.g., soil becomes too salty to grow food), the displacement is permanent.

## Vulnerable Zones: The Geography of Risk

Some regions face disproportionately high risks of climate displacement:

- **Low-Lying Island Nations:** Countries like Tuvalu, Kiribati, and the Maldives sit just a few feet above sea level. They face complete submersion by the end of the century.
- **River Deltas:** Low-lying farming regions like Bangladesh's Ganges delta face a combination of rising seas, monsoon floods, and salt-water intrusion that ruins crops.
- **Drylands (The Sahel):** The semi-arid band below the Sahara Desert in Africa faces severe desertification and drought, ruining cattle farming and forcing rural populations to migrate to overcrowded cities.

---

## The Legal Gap: Who is a "Climate Refugee"?

Although the term "climate refugee" is used in the media, it has **no legal definition in international law**:

> **The UN 1951 Refugee Convention:** Defines a refugee as someone fleeing persecution based on race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social group, or political opinion.

Because the convention does not include **environmental destruction** as a ground for seeking asylum, people fleeing climate change have no automatic legal right to claim refugee status in another country. They are often classified as voluntary economic migrants, leaving them vulnerable to deportation.

---

## Key Terms

- **Climate Migration:** Relocation forced by environmental degradation.
- **Sudden-Onset Hazard:** A rapid environmental disaster (e.g., floods).
- **Slow-Onset Hazard:** Gradual environmental decay (e.g., sea-level rise).
- **Desertification:** The process by which fertile land becomes desert.
- **Climate Refugee:** An informal term for environmental migrants lacking legal refugee status.
