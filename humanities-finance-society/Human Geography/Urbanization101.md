# Urbanization 101

> *Urbanization is the process by which large numbers of people become permanently concentrated in relatively small areas, forming cities.*

---

## The Big Picture

In 1800, only **3%** of the world's population lived in cities.
By 1950, it was **30%**.
Today, over **56%** of humanity lives in urban areas — and that number is projected to reach **68%** by 2050.

For the first time in human history, we have become a predominantly urban species. We are living through the greatest voluntary human migration in history — from fields to cities — and it is accelerating.

**Urbanization** is one of the most powerful geographical shifts of our age. It transforms how people work, how they consume resources, and how they experience identity and community. It lifts millions out of rural poverty by concentrating economic opportunity. But rapid urban growth also creates immense challenges: traffic gridlock, air pollution, housing crises, and the sprawling informal settlements — slums, favelas, squatter camps — that house a third of the world's urban population.

---

## A Brief History of Global Urbanization

| Era | % Urban | What Happened |
|---|---|---|
| **1800** | ~3% | Mostly pre-industrial; London and Beijing were the largest cities (~1M) |
| **1900** | ~15% | Industrial revolution pulls workers to factory cities; urbanization in Europe and North America |
| **1950** | ~30% | Post-WWII suburban expansion in developed nations; urbanization spreads to developing world |
| **2008** | ~50% | Humanity crosses the 50% urban threshold for the first time — a historic milestone |
| **2024** | ~57% | Growth overwhelmingly in Africa and South Asia |
| **2050 (projected)** | ~68% | ~2.5 billion more urban residents expected, almost entirely in the Global South |

## The Urban Transition in the Global South

The urbanization of the 20th century was led by Europe and North America. The urbanization of the 21st century is led by Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

### Key Differences
- **Scale:** African and Asian cities are absorbing migrants at rates that 19th-century European cities — which faced severe health crises even at much smaller scales — never experienced.
- **Informality:** Much of this growth is happening in **informal settlements** — areas where housing is built without planning permission, land title, or guaranteed public services.
- **Youth:** The urban populations of Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia are overwhelmingly young, creating both an economic opportunity (large working-age population) and a challenge (millions of young people needing jobs).

---

## Megacities vs. Metacities

As cities swell, they reach unprecedented scales. Geographers classify:

- **Megacities:** Urban agglomerations with **10 million or more** people. (e.g., New York, Cairo, Kinshasa).
- **Metacities:** Urban agglomerations with **20 million or more** people. (e.g., Delhi, São Paulo, Tokyo).
- **Tokyo's Scale:** Tokyo's metropolitan area contains over 37 million people — more than the entire population of Canada, in one contiguous urban zone.

By 2035, there are projected to be over **40 megacities** globally — most of them in Asia and Africa.

## The Suburbs and Urban Sprawl

In wealthy developed nations (especially the US), post-WWII urbanization took a distinctive form — outward expansion rather than densification:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │    THE SPATIAL SHIFT    │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                            ┌──────────────────┐
│   CITY CENTER    │                                            │   THE SUBURBS    │
│ High density     │                                            │ Low density      │
│ Mixed use        │                                            │ Single-use zoning│
│ Walkable         │                                            │ Auto-dependent   │
└──────────────────┘                                            └──────────────────┘
```

**Suburbanization** — driven by affordable mortgages (GI Bill), highway construction, cheap gasoline, and cultural preference for private home ownership — emptied many American city centers from the 1950s onward.

**Urban Sprawl:** The unrestricted, low-density spread of housing and commercial development across rural land. Its consequences:
- **Car dependency:** With no walkable density, a car becomes a necessity, not a luxury. Americans with cars spend ~50 minutes daily driving, on average.
- **Loss of farmland:** US sprawl consumed 11 million acres of agricultural land between 1992 and 2012.
- **Climate impact:** Sprawl generates significantly higher carbon emissions per person than dense urban living (more driving, more energy to heat/cool single-family homes).
- **Fiscal impact:** Low-density development is expensive to service — roads, pipes, electrical lines, and emergency services must cover vast distances for relatively few people.

## Urban Challenges

### Informal Settlements (Slums / Favelas / Squatter Camps)
When cities grow faster than formal housing supply, people build informal housing wherever they can — on steep hillsides, flood-prone land, along railway corridors, under bridges.

- **Global Scale:** Approximately **1 billion people** live in informal urban settlements worldwide — about one in eight humans.
- **Characteristics:** No legal land title; limited or no piped water, sewerage, or electricity; structures often built from salvaged materials.
- **The Paradox:** Slums are often vibrant economic and social communities — not simply "zones of failure." They represent the entrepreneurial energy of millions of rural migrants who cannot yet afford formal housing but are building futures in the city.

Notable examples: **Dharavi** in Mumbai (India), **Kibera** in Nairobi (Kenya), **Rocinha** in Rio de Janeiro (Brazil), **Orangi Town** in Karachi (Pakistan).

### Gentrification
The process of converting a lower-income urban neighborhood into a higher-income one, typically driven by real estate investment and the influx of wealthier residents.

**The Pattern:**
1. Artists, musicians, and creatives move into cheap neighborhoods, attracted by low rents and cultural energy.
2. Their presence increases the neighborhood's cultural cachet.
3. Developers follow, renovating buildings and raising rents.
4. Original low-income residents and small businesses are displaced by rising costs.
5. The neighborhood becomes an expensive, sanitized version of its former self.

**The Controversy:** Gentrification improves physical infrastructure and property values but systematically displaces vulnerable long-term residents. It is simultaneously an engine of urban renewal and a mechanism of spatial injustice.

## Sustainable Urbanism: Smart Growth and the 15-Minute City

To combat sprawl, pollution, and inequity, urban planners advocate for **sustainable urbanism**:

### Smart Growth Principles
- **Walkability:** Design neighborhoods where daily needs — grocery stores, schools, parks, cafes — are within a 15-minute walk or bike ride.
- **Mixed-Use Development:** Combine residential, commercial, and green spaces in the same neighborhood or building.
- **Public Transit First:** Prioritize subways, trams, bus rapid transit, and cycling infrastructure over personal automobiles.
- **Infill Development:** Build new housing within already-developed areas rather than expanding into undeveloped land.
- **Density:** Concentrate population density to reduce per-capita land consumption and infrastructure costs.

### The 15-Minute City
Popularized by Paris Mayor Anne Hidalgo, the **15-Minute City** concept proposes that every resident should be able to access work, schools, healthcare, shopping, and leisure within 15 minutes of their home by foot or bicycle — eliminating the need for long commutes and reducing car dependence.
- Paris has invested heavily in bike lanes and pedestrianizing major roads.
- Melbourne, Barcelona, and Singapore have adopted similar frameworks.

## Case Study: Curitiba, Brazil — A Model of Urban Planning

**The Context:** Curitiba, the capital of Paraná state in southern Brazil, was growing rapidly in the 1960s — just like hundreds of other Brazilian cities. Most Brazilian cities responded by building highways for cars. Curitiba chose a radically different path.

**The Innovation:** In the 1970s, under Mayor Jaime Lerner, Curitiba developed the world's first **Bus Rapid Transit (BRT)** system — a network of dedicated bus lanes with station-platform boarding (like a metro, but cheaper), connecting the city along five structural axes radiating from the city center.

**The Design Philosophy:**
- High-density development was *only* permitted along the BRT corridors — incentivizing walkable, transit-oriented neighborhoods.
- Low-density areas were protected from overdevelopment.
- The city invested in extensive public parks, which also served as flood control.
- An innovative "Green Exchange" allowed poor residents to trade bags of sorted recyclable waste for bus tokens and food.

**The Results:**
- Curitiba has one of Brazil's highest urban quality-of-life rankings.
- Its citizens use far less fuel per capita than other Brazilian cities of comparable size.
- The BRT model has been replicated in over **200 cities worldwide** — from Bogotá's TransMilenio to Istanbul's Metrobüs.
- It was accomplished at a fraction of the cost of metro construction.

**The Lesson:** Curitiba demonstrates that intelligent, integrated urban design — not necessarily massive budgets — can produce dramatically better urban outcomes. The city's success lies in aligning land use with transportation: designing urban space so that transit is not just an option, but the most convenient choice.

## Key Terms

- **Urbanization:** The process of population concentration into cities.
- **Megacity:** An urban agglomeration with over 10 million residents.
- **Metacity:** An urban agglomeration with over 20 million residents.
- **Suburban Sprawl:** Low-density, automobile-dependent outward city expansion.
- **Informal Settlement (Slum):** Urban housing built without formal land titles or public service connections.
- **Gentrification:** The displacement of lower-income residents by rising property values driven by wealthier newcomers.
- **Smart Growth:** Urban planning principles emphasizing density, mixed use, and transit.
- **15-Minute City:** Urban design concept ensuring all daily needs are reachable within 15 minutes on foot or bicycle.
- **Bus Rapid Transit (BRT):** A high-quality, high-capacity bus system using dedicated lanes and platform boarding.
