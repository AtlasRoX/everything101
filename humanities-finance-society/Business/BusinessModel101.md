# Business Model 101

> *A business model is a conceptual framework that explains how a company creates, delivers, and captures value.*

---

## The Big Picture

Imagine you want to start a company that rents out bicycles. 
- Do you buy a fleet of bikes and charge people by the hour? 
- Do you charge a monthly membership fee for unlimited rides? 
- Or do you give the bikes away for free and sell advertising space on the wheels?

Each of these options is a different **business model**. 

A business model is not just a product; it is the entire system of how a business functions. It defines who your customers are, how you reach them, what your operations cost, and how you actually make money. A great product with a bad business model will fail, while a mediocre product with a brilliant business model can conquer markets.

This article introduces the Business Model Canvas and explores common business models used in the modern economy.

---

## The Tool: The Business Model Canvas

Developed by Alexander Osterwalder, the **Business Model Canvas** is a simple, visual template that breaks a business down into nine core building blocks. Economists group these blocks into four pillars:

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│               THE BUSINESS MODEL CANVAS                │
├───────────────┬─────────────────────────┬──────────────┤
│  THE INFRASTRUCTURE  │     THE OFFERING        │  THE CUSTOMER│
│ - Key Partners       │   - Value Proposition   │- Cust. Rel.  │
│ - Key Activities     │     (What problem do    │- Channels    │
│ - Key Resources      │      you solve?)        │- Cust. Seg.  │
├───────────────┴────────┬────────────────┴──────────────┤
│       THE FINANCE      │ - Cost Structure (What it costs)              │
│       PILLAR           │ - Revenue Streams (How you charge)            │
└────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

1. **Value Proposition (The Core):** What unique value do you deliver? Why should a customer choose you over a competitor?
2. **Customer Segments (The Who):** Who are your target customers? Are you serving a niche market or a mass market?
3. **Channels (The How):** How do you deliver your value proposition to customers? (e.g., website, retail stores, app).
4. **Customer Relationships:** How do you interact with customers? (e.g., self-service, personal assistance, automated emails).
5. **Revenue Streams (The Money In):** How does the business earn cash?
6. **Key Resources:** What assets (intellectual property, offices, machines) are needed to make the model work?
7. **Key Activities:** What vital tasks must the company perform? (e.g., software development, supply chain logistics).
8. **Key Partners:** Who are the external suppliers or partners that help you run operations?
9. **Cost Structure (The Money Out):** What are the major costs of running this business model?

## Four Common Modern Business Models

Depending on how a company arranges these nine blocks, they build different models:

### 1. Transactional (Direct Sales)
The classic model: you manufacture a product and sell it directly to a customer for a one-time price.
- **Example:** You buy a coffee from Starbucks or an iPhone from Apple.
- **Key Focus:** Maximizing sales volume and managing inventory.

### 2. Subscription (Recurring Revenue)
Instead of buying a product once, customers pay a recurring fee (weekly, monthly, or annually) for ongoing access to a service.
- **Example:** Netflix, Spotify, or a local gym.
- **Key Focus:** Customer retention (keeping churn rates low) and predictable cash flow.

### 3. Freemium (Free + Premium)
You offer a basic version of your product for free to attract a large user base, but charge a fee for advanced features or to remove ads.
- **Example:** Dropbox, LinkedIn, or Spotify.
- **Key Focus:** High conversion rates (converting free users into paying subscribers).

### 4. Marketplace (Platform)
The company does not own any inventory or provide the service directly. Instead, they build a digital platform that connects independent buyers and sellers, taking a fee or commission from each transaction.
- **Example:** Airbnb, Uber, or eBay.
- **Key Focus:** Solving the "chicken-and-egg" problem (attracting enough buyers to make it attractive for sellers, and vice versa).

## Key Terms

- **Business Model:** The blueprint of how a company creates and captures value.
- **Value Proposition:** The promise of value to be delivered and a customer's belief that they will receive value.
- **Customer Segment:** A group of people or organizations sharing similar characteristics that a business targets.
- **Revenue Stream:** A source of revenue for a company.
- **Churn Rate:** The percentage of subscribers who cancel their subscription in a given time period.
- **Freemium:** A business model that blends free and premium tiers of service.
