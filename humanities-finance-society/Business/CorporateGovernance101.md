# Corporate Governance 101

> *Corporate governance is the system of rules, practices, and processes by which a firm is directed and controlled, balancing the interests of a company's many stakeholders.*

---

## The Big Picture

If you own a small bakery, you run it yourself. You decide what to bake, what to charge, and how much to pay yourself. 

But what if you own shares of a massive public corporation like Microsoft or Apple? You cannot walk into the office and tell employees what to do. You are an owner, but you do not run the company. Instead, you delegate management to a professional CEO.

How do you, the owner, ensure that the CEO is working hard to make the company successful, rather than spending company money on private jets and expensive parties?

This is the challenge of **corporate governance**. It is the set of rules, checks, and balances that aligns the incentives of managers with the owners and ensures accountability.

This article introduces the principal-agent problem, the role of the board of directors, and the debate between shareholder and stakeholder capitalism.

---

## The Core Challenge: The Principal-Agent Problem

At the heart of corporate governance is a classic conflict known as the **Principal-Agent Problem**:

```
                       ┌─────────────────────────┐
                       │   PRINCIPAL-AGENT SPLIT │
                       └────────────┬────────────┘
                                    │
         ┌──────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                     ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                  ┌──────────────────┐
│  THE PRINCIPAL   │                                  │    THE AGENT     │
│ (Shareholder/    │                                  │     (CEO/        │
│  Owner)          │                                  │   Management)    │
└────────┬─────────┘                                  └────────┬─────────┘
         │                                                     │
         ▼                                                     ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                  ┌──────────────────┐
│ Wants long-term  │                                  │ Wants high pay,  │
│ stock growth &   │                                  │ power, status, & │
│ dividends        │                                  │ short-term gains │
└──────────────────┘                                  └────────────────┘
```

- **The Principal (The Owner):** Wants the company's value to grow over the long run.
- **The Agent (The Manager):** Runs the company day-to-day. They may want to maximize their own salary, job security, and personal prestige, even if it hurts the owners.
- **Information Asymmetry:** The managers know much more about the company's true health than the owners do, making it easy for managers to hide poor decisions or manipulate financial reports.

---

## The Board of Directors: The Watchdogs

To solve the principal-agent problem, shareholders elect a **Board of Directors** to act as their watchdogs:

- **The Role:** The board represents the owners. It hires and fires the CEO, approves major investments, audits financial statements, and decides how much to pay top executives.
- **Board Structure:**
  - **Inside Directors:** Board members who are also employees of the company (e.g., the CEO or CFO).
  - **Independent (Outside) Directors:** Board members who have no employment or financial ties to the company, providing unbiased oversight.
- **Executive Compensation:** To align incentives, boards often pay CEOs in **stock options** rather than cash. If the company's stock price rises, the CEO gets rich alongside the shareholders.

## Shareholder vs. Stakeholder Capitalism

How should a company define its ultimate goal? There are two competing philosophies:

### 1. Shareholder Theory (Milton Friedman)
The sole social responsibility of a business is to maximize profit for its shareholders (owners).
- **The Rationale:** Shareholders took the financial risk to build the company. If managers spend company money on social causes that don't make a profit, they are essentially stealing from the owners. The market will naturally regulate behavior.

### 2. Stakeholder Theory (Edward Freeman)
A corporation is responsible to *everyone* affected by its decisions, not just owners.
- **The Stakeholders:** Includes employees, customers, suppliers, local communities, and the environment.
- **The Rationale:** A company cannot succeed in the long run if it poisons its local community, exploits its workers, or cheats its suppliers. Business ethics and sustainability are vital for survival.

---

## Key Terms

- **Corporate Governance:** The system of rules and checks directing a corporation.
- **Principal-Agent Problem:** The conflict of interest when an agent acts on behalf of a principal.
- **Board of Directors:** An elected group representing shareholders to oversee management.
- **Independent Director:** A board member with no employee or family ties to the firm.
- **Shareholder Theory:** The view that firms exist solely to maximize shareholder value.
- **Stakeholder Theory:** The view that firms must balance the interests of all affected parties.
