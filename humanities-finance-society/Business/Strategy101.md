# Strategy 101

> *Business strategy is the long-term plan of action designed to achieve a competitive advantage over rivals.*

---

## The Big Picture

If you open a new pizza restaurant in a town that already has twenty pizza restaurants, why should anyone buy from you? 
- If you sell the exact same pizza at the exact same price as everyone else, your business is a commodity. You will be forced to compete on price, squeezing your profits to near zero.

This is the problem that **strategy** solves. Strategy is not just a list of goals (like "increase sales by 10%"); it is a set of choices that positions a company to be **different** from its competitors. 

Strategy defines where you will compete (which customer segments and products) and how you will win. As Harvard Business School professor Michael Porter notes, the essence of strategy is **choosing what NOT to do**.

This article introduces Porter's Five Forces, the two main paths to competitive advantage, and the SWOT analysis tool.

---

## Porter's Five Forces: Analyzing the Industry

Before designing a strategy, a business must understand its environment. Michael Porter developed a framework of **Five Forces** that shape the profitability of any industry:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │   THREAT OF NEW ENTRY   │
                            │  Ease of starting new   │
                            │        rivals           │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
                                         ▼
┌─────────────────────────┐ ┌─────────────────────────┐ ┌─────────────────────────┐
│   BARGAINING POWER OF   │ │   INDUSTRY RIVALRY      │ │   BARGAINING POWER OF   │
│        SUPPLIERS        │►│ Competition among       │◄│        BUYERS         │
│ Can suppliers force     │ │ existing firms          │ │ Can buyers demand low  │
│ prices up?              │ │                         │ │ prices?                │
└─────────────────────────┘ └─────────────────────────┘ └─────────────────────────┘
                                         ▲
                                         │
                            ┌────────────┴────────────┐
                            │  THREAT OF SUBSTITUTES  │
                            │ Can customers switch to │
                            │ alternative solutions?  │
                            └─────────────────────────┘
```

1. **Threat of New Entrants:** How easy is it for new competitors to start selling? (High barriers like expensive machinery or government licensing protect profits).
2. **Bargaining Power of Suppliers:** If you only have one supplier for a key raw material, they can raise prices easily, hurting your profits.
3. **Bargaining Power of Buyers:** If you only have a few big corporate customers, they can demand discounts and squeeze your margins.
4. **Threat of Substitutes:** Can customers solve their problem in a completely different way? (e.g., travelers taking trains instead of short-haul airline flights).
5. **Intensity of Industry Rivalry:** If there are dozens of identical firms fighting for the same market, price wars will destroy industry margins.

## The Two Paths to Competitive Advantage

To win, a company must achieve a **competitive advantage**—producing value that competitors cannot easily duplicate. Porter argues there are two main generic strategies to achieve this:

### 1. Cost Leadership (Cheaper)
Becoming the lowest-cost producer in your industry. 
- **The Strategy:** Optimize operations, buy raw materials in massive quantities to get discounts, and eliminate all non-essential features. (e.g., Walmart, Southwest Airlines, IKEA).
- **The Goal:** Sell at low prices to capture mass market share while remaining profitable.

### 2. Differentiation (Better/Different)
Creating a product that customers perceive as unique or premium, allowing you to charge a higher price (a price premium).
- **The Strategy:** Invest heavily in design, branding, quality, or customer service. (e.g., Apple, BMW, Starbucks).
- **The Goal:** Build strong brand loyalty so customers refuse to buy cheaper generic alternatives.

## The Tool: SWOT Analysis

To align its strategy with its capabilities, a business uses a **SWOT Analysis**, which maps internal and external factors:

| | Positive | Negative |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal (About the Firm)** | **Strengths (S):** What does the company do exceptionally well? (e.g., proprietary tech, famous brand). | **Weaknesses (W):** Where does the firm lag? (e.g., high employee turnover, outdated software). |
| **External (About the Market)** | **Opportunities (O):** What market trends can the firm exploit? (e.g., new regulations, emerging markets). | **Threats (T):** What market forces could harm the firm? (e.g., trade wars, rising raw material costs). |

---

## Key Terms

- **Strategy:** A company's plan of action to achieve a competitive advantage.
- **Competitive Advantage:** An edge over rivals that allows a company to generate more sales or margins.
- **Cost Leadership:** A strategy to become the lowest-cost operator in an industry.
- **Differentiation:** A strategy to offer unique, premium products that command higher prices.
- **SWOT Analysis:** A framework analyzing Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats.
- **Barriers to Entry:** Factors that make it difficult for new firms to enter a market.
