# Branding 101

> *A brand is a name, term, design, symbol, or any other feature that identifies one seller's good or service as distinct from those of other sellers. It is the emotional shortcut of the market.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why do people pay $4 for a cup of coffee at Starbucks when they could get coffee at a local gas station for $1? Why do consumers line up for hours to buy a new iPhone when competitors offer phones with identical technical specs for half the price?

They do it because of the **brand**. 

A brand is not just a logo, a catchy slogan, or a color palette. A brand is the **sum of all feelings, expectations, and experiences** a customer associates with a company. It is a promise of quality, trust, and identity. 

In a crowded market, a strong brand acts as an emotional shortcut, allowing consumers to make quick buying decisions with confidence, and allowing companies to command premium prices.

This article introduces brand equity, brand positioning, and the strategy of brand extensions.

---

## Brand Equity: The Financial Value of a Name

A successful brand has **brand equity**—the added commercial value that a brand name gives to a product.

If you place two identical white t-shirts on a table:
- Shirt A is unbranded and sells for **$10**.
- Shirt B has a tiny embroidered logo of a luxury brand and sells for **$150**.
- The **$140 difference** is the brand equity.

- **Brand Awareness:** Do consumers recognize and recall your brand name when thinking of a product category?
- **Perceived Quality:** The customer's perception of the overall quality or superiority of a product relative to alternatives.
- **Brand Associations:** The emotional or cognitive connections consumers make when they think of a brand (e.g., Apple associated with "creativity", Nike with "athletic determination").
- **Brand Loyalty:** The tendency of consumers to continue buying the same brand, even when competitors offer cheaper alternatives.

## Brand Positioning: Carving Out Your Niche

To build brand equity, a company must position its brand clearly. Brand positioning answers three questions:
1. **Target:** Who is this product for?
2. **Frame of Reference:** What category does the brand compete in?
3. **Point of Difference:** What makes this brand unique compared to rivals?

- **Example (Targeting Convenience):** FedEx positioned itself around speed: *"When it absolutely, positively has to be there overnight."*
- **Example (Targeting Status):** Rolex positions itself not as a timekeeper (a cheap plastic watch tells time just as well), but as a symbol of personal achievement and luxury.

---

## Brand Extensions: Leveraging the Name

Once a brand has built strong equity, it can leverage its reputation to launch new products in different categories. This is a **brand extension**.

- **Success Example:** Apple extending from computers to MP3 players (iPod), then to phones (iPhone), and then to watches (Apple Watch). The trust built in computers transferred to new devices.
- **Failure Example:** Colgate (the toothpaste brand) launching a line of frozen dinner entrees in the 1980s. The brand was so strongly associated with minty toothpaste that consumers found the idea of Colgate food unappetizing.

---

## Key Terms

- **Brand:** The cognitive and emotional perception of a company or product.
- **Brand Equity:** The value premium that a respected brand name adds to a product.
- **Brand Associations:** Mental links that consumers make between a brand and specific concepts or emotions.
- **Brand Extension:** Using an established brand name on a new product in a different category.
- **Brand Identity:** The visible elements of a brand (logo, design) that identify and distinguish it.
