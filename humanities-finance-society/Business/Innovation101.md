# Innovation 101

> *Innovation is the practical implementation of ideas that result in the introduction of new goods, services, or improvement in offering and operations.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why did Kodak, the world's dominant photography company, go bankrupt in 2012, even though they had actually invented the digital camera back in 1975? 

Why do massive, wealthy corporations with thousands of smart employees often get beaten by tiny startups with a fraction of their budget?

The answer lies in the dynamics of **innovation**. Innovation is not just about having a creative idea; it is about commercializing that idea and adapting to change. 

In business, standing still is the equivalent of moving backward. Companies must constantly innovate to survive. However, as Kodak discovered, managing innovation is incredibly difficult because the systems designed to make a company successful today can prevent it from adopting the technology of tomorrow.

This article introduces the difference between sustaining and disruptive innovation, the concept of the innovator's dilemma, and the curve of innovation adoption.

---

## Sustaining vs. Disruptive Innovation

Harvard Business School professor Clayton Christensen divided innovation into two main types:

```
Performance
  ▲                                / Disruptive Tech (Trajectory)
  │                               /
  │                              /  * High-End Market Match
  │                             /
  │    * Customer Needs        /
  │  ─────────────────────────*─────── (Sustaining Tech Trajectory)
  │                          /
  │                         /
  │   * Cheap/Low-End Match/
  └───────────────────────/───────────► Time
  0
```

### 1. Sustaining Innovation
Improving an existing product to sell it for higher margins to your best customers.
- **How it works:** Incremental progress. Making a smartphone camera slightly clearer, or making a car engine slightly more fuel-efficient.
- **Who wins:** Large, established market leaders. They have the budget and research departments to execute these improvements.

### 2. Disruptive Innovation
Introducing a simpler, cheaper, or more convenient product that appeals to low-end or new customers, which eventually improves and replaces the dominant product.
- **How it works:** A disruptive product starts out with lower performance than the standard (e.g., early digital cameras took terrible photos compared to film). Because it is cheap and convenient, it finds a niche. Over time, the technology improves rapidly until it matches the performance of the old standard, wiping out the old industry.
- **Who wins:** Agile startups.

## The Innovator's Dilemma

Why do established companies fail to adopt disruptive innovations? This is **The Innovator's Dilemma**:

> **The Dilemma:** The exact habits of good management—listening to your best customers, focusing on high-margin products, and investing in proven projects—are the exact reasons why leaders fail to adopt disruptive technologies.

- When digital cameras were invented, Kodak's best customers (professional photographers) wanted high-quality film, not low-quality digital images.
- Kodak's highest-margin business was printing film. Digital photography had no film prints, offering low margins.
- Kodak's management rationally decided to focus on film, leaving digital to others. By the time digital technology improved to match professional standards, Kodak was obsolete.

## The Adoption Curve: How Innovations Spread

Innovations are not adopted by everyone at the same time. According to sociologist Everett Rogers, adoption follows a bell curve split into five groups:

1. **Innovators (2.5%):** Risk-takers who want to try new tech first, even if it is buggy or expensive.
2. **Early Adopters (13.5%):** Opinion leaders who spot the potential of a technology early and use it to gain an advantage.
3. **Early Majority (34%):** Pragmatists who adopt technology once it is proven and stable.
4. **Late Majority (34%):** Skeptics who only adopt technology because of social pressure or because the old option is no longer supported.
5. **Laggards (16%):** Traditionalists who avoid change until they have no choice.

> **Aha! moment:** The gap between the Early Adopters and the Early Majority is often called **the chasm**. Many startups fail because they build a product that appeals to tech-enthusiasts (Early Adopters) but fail to make it simple or reliable enough to cross the chasm to the mass market (Early Majority).

## Key Terms

- **Innovation:** The implementation of new ideas that create commercial value.
- **Sustaining Innovation:** Improvements to existing products targeting existing customers.
- **Disruptive Innovation:** A cheaper, simpler product targeting low-end markets that eventually overtakes the industry.
- **The Innovator's Dilemma:** The phenomenon where successful companies fail by doing exactly what made them successful in the first place.
- **Technology Adoption Life Cycle:** A sociological model describing the adoption of a new product according to demographic profiles.
