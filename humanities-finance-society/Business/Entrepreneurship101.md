# Entrepreneurship 101

> *Entrepreneurship is the process of designing, launching, and running a new business venture, typically starting as a small business, while taking on financial risk in the hope of profit.*

---

## The Big Picture

Where do businesses come from? They don't simply appear. Every company, from your local coffee shop to Apple, began as an idea in the mind of an **entrepreneur**.

Entrepreneurs are the pioneers of the economy. While managers run existing systems, entrepreneurs build new ones. They look at the world and see gaps: *unsolved problems, frustrated customers, or inefficient services*. 

Starting a business is difficult; the majority of new ventures fail within the first few years. To survive, an entrepreneur must combine a unique mindset with practical risk management, bootstrapping skills, and resilience.

This article introduces the entrepreneurial mindset, opportunity recognition, risk management, and the concept of bootstrapping.

---

## The Entrepreneurial Mindset

What makes someone an entrepreneur? It is a combination of specific traits:

- **Opportunity Recognition:** The ability to spot gaps in the market. Where others see a nuisance, entrepreneurs see a potential business (e.g., getting frustrated by trying to hail a cab leads to founding Uber).
- **Calculated Risk-Taking:** Entrepreneurs are not reckless gamblers; they systematically identify risks and find ways to minimize or hedge them before spending money.
- **Resourcefulness:** The ability to do a lot with a little. In the early days of a business, the entrepreneur is the salesperson, the product builder, the accountant, and the janitor.
- **Resilience ("Failing Forward"):** Failure is a common part of the process. Successful entrepreneurs view failures as cheap lessons that help them pivot (change direction) toward a better idea.

## Bootstrapping vs. Fundraising

When starting a business, an entrepreneur needs capital. There are two main paths to funding:

```
                              ┌─────────────────────────┐
                              │     FUNDING PATHS       │
                              └────────────┬────────────┘
                                           │
         ┌─────────────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                                   ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                                ┌──────────────────┐
│   BOOTSTRAPPING  │                                                │   FUNDRAISING    │
│ Self-funded via  │                                                │ Selling equity   │
│ savings & early  │                                                │ to investors for │
│ revenue          │                                                │ rapid capital    │
└────────┬─────────┘                                                └────────┬─────────┘
         │                                                                   │
         ▼                                                                   ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                                ┌──────────────────┐
│ Retain 100% control;                                              │ Fast growth;     │
│ Slower growth    │                                                │ Dilute ownership │
└──────────────────┘                                                └──────────────────┘
```

### 1. Bootstrapping
Funding the company entirely through personal savings and the revenues generated by early sales.
- **Pros:** The founder retains 100% ownership and control. There is no pressure from investors to hit unrealistic growth targets.
- **Cons:** Growth is limited by cash flow. If the business requires expensive equipment, bootstrapping can be very slow or impossible.

### 2. Fundraising
Raising money from external investors (like angel investors, venture capital, or bank loans) in exchange for equity (ownership shares) in the company.
- **Pros:** Provides a large amount of cash immediately, allowing the business to hire a team, build products quickly, and capture the market. (See [Startup101](./Startup101.md)).
- **Cons:** Dilutes the founder's ownership and control. The founder must answer to a board of directors and focus on investor returns.

## Minimizing Risk: The MVP (Minimum Viable Product)

One of the biggest mistakes entrepreneurs make is spending months and thousands of dollars building a perfect product, only to launch it and discover that nobody wants it.

Modern entrepreneurship uses the **MVP (Minimum Viable Product)** method to reduce this risk:

- **The Concept:** Build the simplest, cheapest version of your product that solves the core problem, and get it into the hands of real customers immediately.
- **Feedback Loop:** Watch how customers use the MVP, gather feedback, and iterate (improve it). If customers refuse to use even the basic version, the entrepreneur can pivot to a new idea before wasting their savings.

---

## Key Terms

- **Entrepreneur:** A person who organizes and operates a business, taking on greater than normal financial risks to do so.
- **Opportunity Recognition:** Identifying customer problems that can be solved profitably.
- **Bootstrapping:** Starting a business with little capital, relying on personal savings and early revenues.
- **Fundraising:** Securing capital from external investors in exchange for equity.
- **MVP (Minimum Viable Product):** A version of a new product which allows a team to collect the maximum amount of validated learning about customers with the least effort.
- **Pivot:** A structured course correction designed to test a new basic hypothesis about the product, business model, and engine of growth.
