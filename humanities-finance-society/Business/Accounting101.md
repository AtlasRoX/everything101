# Accounting 101

> *Accounting is the process of recording, summarizing, analyzing, and reporting financial transactions of a business. It is often called the "language of business."*

---

## The Big Picture

If you run a business, how do you know if you are actually making money? 
- You can look at your bank account, but what if you have $50,000 in cash, but owe your suppliers $100,000 next month? Or what if you sold $200,000 worth of goods on credit, but haven't received the cash yet?

To track these complexities, businesses use **accounting**. 

Accounting provides a standardized, objective set of rules to record and report financial activity. This allows business owners to make smart decisions, allows banks to decide if they should lend you money, and allows investors to evaluate your company's performance.

This article introduces the fundamental accounting equation, double-entry bookkeeping, and the three essential financial statements.

---

## The Accounting Equation

All financial accounting is built on a single, unbreakable equation:

$$\text{Assets} = \text{Liabilities} + \text{Owner's Equity}$$

```
                ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────┐
                │             THE BALANCE OF SHEETS             │
                ├───────────────────────┬───────────────────────┤
                │       ASSETS          │      LIABILITIES      │
                │ What the business     │ What the business     │
                │ owns                  │ owes to others        │
                │ (Cash, Inventory,     │ (Bank loans, bills)   │
                │  Equipment)           ├───────────────────────┤
                │                       │    OWNER'S EQUITY     │
                │                       │ The net value belonging│
                │                       │ to the owners         │
                └───────────────────────┴───────────────────────┘
```

- **Assets:** Resources owned by the business that have future economic value. Examples: cash, inventory, delivery trucks, patents.
- **Liabilities:** Debts or obligations the business owes to external parties. Examples: bank loans, unpaid utility bills, mortgages.
- **Owner's Equity (Net Worth):** The residual interest in the assets of the company after deducting all liabilities. It is the value that actually belongs to the owners/shareholders.

## The Three Financial Statements

To summarize financial performance, accounting compiles three reports at the end of each period:

### 1. The Income Statement (Profit & Loss / P&L)
Shows whether the company was profitable over a specific period (e.g., a quarter or a year).
- **Formula:** $\text{Net Income} = \text{Revenue} - \text{Expenses}$
- It starts with total sales (revenue) at the top and subtracts costs (cost of goods sold, wages, interest, taxes) to arrive at the "bottom line" (Net Income).

### 2. The Balance Sheet
Provides a snapshot of the company's financial position at a **specific point in time** (e.g., December 31st).
- It lists all the company's Assets on one side, and its Liabilities and Equity on the other, proving that the accounting equation balances.

### 3. The Cash Flow Statement
Tracks the actual physical cash entering and leaving the business over a period. This is vital because **profit is not cash**. A company can be highly profitable on paper but go bankrupt if all its money is tied up in unpaid invoices (accounts receivable) and it runs out of cash.
It divides cash flows into three categories:
- **Operating Activities:** Cash from day-to-day sales and expenses.
- **Investing Activities:** Cash spent buying or selling assets (like machinery or property).
- **Financing Activities:** Cash from taking out loans, issuing stock, or paying dividends to owners.

## Double-Entry Bookkeeping: The Double Ledger

To prevent errors, accounting uses **double-entry bookkeeping**. Every transaction affects at least two accounts: one is **debited** and the other is **credited**.

- **Example:** You buy a new delivery truck for $20,000 cash.
  - **Account 1 (Truck Asset):** Increases by $20,000 (Debit).
  - **Account 2 (Cash Asset):** Decreases by $20,000 (Credit).
- The total assets remain the same, but the mix changes, and the accounting equation stays balanced.

---

## Key Terms

- **Accounting:** The system of recording and reporting business financial transactions.
- **Asset:** Anything of value owned by a business.
- **Liability:** A financial debt or obligation.
- **Equity:** The owners' stake in the business; Assets minus Liabilities.
- **Income Statement:** A statement showing revenues, expenses, and net income.
- **Balance Sheet:** A statement showing assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific date.
- **Cash Flow Statement:** A statement tracking the physical movement of cash.
