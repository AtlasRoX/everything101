# Startup 101

> *A startup is a temporary organization designed to search for a repeatable and scalable business model under conditions of extreme uncertainty.*

---

## The Big Picture

Is a new local hair salon or restaurant a startup? 

No. While they are new businesses, they are not **startups** in the economic sense. A hair salon has a business model that is well understood, is limited by physical space and local geography, and is designed to grow slowly.

A startup is built for **speed and scale**. A software startup can design a product once and sell it to millions of customers globally with virtually zero additional production cost. 

Because of this massive growth potential, startups behave differently than traditional businesses: they burn through cash quickly to capture market share, rely heavily on venture capital, and chase a milestone known as **product-market fit**.

This article introduces the concept of scalability, the search for product-market fit, and the lifecycle of startup funding.

---

## The Core Metric: Scalability

The defining feature of a startup is **scalability**:

> **Scalability** is the ability of a business to increase its revenue exponentially while its operating costs increase only linearly.

```
Revenue/Costs ($)
  ▲
  │                                    / Revenue (Exponential Growth)
  │                                   /
  │                                  /
  │                                 /
  │                                /
  │    * The "Scalability Gap"    /
  │                             /
  │                            /
  │   ────────────────────────*─────── Operating Costs (Linear Growth)
  └──────────────────────────/────────► Scale (Customers/Sales)
  0
```

- **Low Scalability (Service Businesses):** To serve double the clients, a consulting firm must hire double the consultants. Costs rise at the same rate as revenue.
- **High Scalability (Software/Digital):** To serve double the users, a software company just needs slightly more server space. Revenue shoots up while costs stay flat.

---

## Product-Market Fit: The Startup Holy Grail

Before a startup can scale, it must find **Product-Market Fit (PMF)**. 

> **Product-Market Fit** is the point where a startup has built a product that satisfies a strong market demand, and customers are buying, using, and recommending it fast enough to sustain growth.

- **Before PMF:** The startup is in "search mode." It is talking to users, modifying features, and changing its marketing. (Burning cash).
- **After PMF:** The startup enters "scale mode." The product is flying off the shelves. The challenge shifts from *building* the product to *growing* the company to handle the demand.

## The Startup Funding Lifecycle

Startups rarely fund their rapid expansion through sales revenue alone. They go through structured rounds of investment, selling shares of the company (equity) to raise capital:

```
┌─────────────┐    ┌─────────────┐    ┌─────────────┐    ┌─────────────┐    ┌─────────────┐
│  BOOTSTRAP  │───►│    SEED     │───►│ SERIES A/B  │───►│  LATER STG  │───►│  THE EXIT   │
│ Founder's   │    │ Angels & F&F│    │ Venture Cap │    │ growth equity│    │ IPO or      │
│ cash & ideas│    │ MVP building│    │ Scaling PMF │    │ Pre-IPO prep │    │ Acquisition │
└─────────────┘    └─────────────┘    └─────────────┘    └─────────────┘    └─────────────┘
```

1. **Bootstrap / Friends & Family:** The founder uses their own money or raises small sums from friends to build the first prototype.
2. **Seed Stage:** Raising $500k to $2M from **Angel Investors** (wealthy individuals) to hire a core team, launch the MVP, and prove product-market fit.
3. **Series A:** Raising $2M to $15M from **Venture Capital (VC) firms**. This money is used to optimize the product and build a repeatable sales engine.
4. **Series B & C:** Raising larger sums ($15M+) to scale operations, expand into international markets, and beat competitors.
5. **The Exit:** The final milestone where early investors and founders cash out their shares:
   - **Acquisition:** The startup is bought by a larger company (e.g., Google buying YouTube).
   - **IPO (Initial Public Offering):** The startup lists its shares on a public stock exchange, allowing anyone to buy them.

---

## Key Terms

- **Startup:** A temporary organization searching for a scalable business model under uncertainty.
- **Scalability:** The ability to increase sales without a proportional increase in costs.
- **Product-Market Fit (PMF):** Being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market.
- **Venture Capital (VC):** Private equity financing provided by firms to startups with high growth potential.
- **Angel Investor:** A wealthy individual who provides capital for a business start-up, usually in exchange for convertible debt or ownership equity.
- **IPO:** The first sale of stock by a private company to the public.
