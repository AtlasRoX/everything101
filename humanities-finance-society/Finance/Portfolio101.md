# Portfolio 101

> *An investment portfolio is a grouping of financial assets such as stocks, bonds, commodities, cash equivalents, and mutual funds or ETFs, managed as a single unit to meet specific financial goals.*

---

## The Big Picture

If you decided to build a healthy meal, you wouldn't eat a plate of only broccoli or only steak. You would build a balanced plate containing proteins, carbohydrates, and vegetables.

An investment **portfolio** is no different. 

You should not invest all your money in a single asset class (like only stocks or only cash). Instead, you build a balanced portfolio of different assets. The process of deciding how to split your money among stocks, bonds, and cash is called **asset allocation**. 

By designing your portfolio to match your age and risk tolerance, and by rebalancing it periodically, you can maximize your returns while protecting yourself from market crashes.

This article introduces the principles of asset allocation, the power of diversification, the rule of rebalancing, and the basics of Modern Portfolio Theory.

---

## Asset Allocation: The Stocks vs. Bonds Split

Asset allocation is the most important decision an investor makes, driving over 90% of a portfolio's returns. The split typically comes down to **Stocks (for growth/high risk)** vs. **Bonds (for income/low risk)**:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │    PORTFOLIO STYLES     │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                               ▼                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐            ┌──────────────────┐            ┌──────────────────┐
│ AGGRESSIVE (90/10)│           │ BALANCED (60/40) │            │CONSERVATIVE(30/70)
│ 90% Stocks,      │            │ 60% Stocks,      │            │ 30% Stocks,      │
│ 10% Bonds        │            │ 40% Bonds        │            │ 70% Bonds        │
│ (Younger / 20s)  │            │ (Mid-Career / 40s)│           │ (Near Retirement)│
└──────────────────┘            └──────────────────┘            └──────────────────┘
```

- **The Rule of thumb (110 minus your age):** To find your target stock allocation, subtract your age from 110:
  - At **age 30**: $110 - 30 = 80\%$ stocks, with the remaining 20% in bonds.
  - At **age 70**: $110 - 70 = 40\%$ stocks, with 60% in bonds (protecting capital as you near retirement).

## Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT): The Efficient Frontier

Developed by Nobel laureate Harry Markowitz, **Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)** mathematically proves that you can design a portfolio that maximizes return for a given level of risk by combining assets that are not perfectly correlated (i.e., they do not move in the same direction at the same time).

- **Correlated Assets:** Standard oil stock and gasoline refinery stock. If oil prices fall, both stocks drop together. No diversification benefit.
- **Non-Correlated Assets:** Stocks and bonds. Historically, when stock markets crash, bond prices often rise as investors flee to safety. By owning both, you smooth out the roller coaster ride of the market.

---

## Rebalancing: Buying Low and Selling High

Over time, different assets grow at different speeds. A booming stock market can shift a target **60/40 portfolio** into a **70/30 portfolio**:
- This means your portfolio is now riskier than you intended.
- **Rebalancing** is the act of selling some of your winning assets (stocks) and using the proceeds to buy more of your lagging assets (bonds) to return to your target 60/40 allocation.
- **The Benefit:** Rebalancing forces you, by mathematical rule, to **sell high** (selling the stocks that boomed) and **buy low** (buying the bonds that lagged), boosting your long-term returns.

---

## Key Terms

- **Portfolio:** A collective grouping of financial assets.
- **Asset Allocation:** Splitting portfolio capital among asset classes (stocks, bonds, cash).
- **Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT):** A mathematical framework for assembling a portfolio of assets to maximize expected return for a given level of risk.
- **Correlation:** A measure of how two securities move in relation to each other.
- **Rebalancing:** Realignment of the weightings of assets in a portfolio.
