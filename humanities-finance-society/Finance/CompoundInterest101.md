# Compound Interest 101

> *Compound interest is the addition of interest to the principal sum of a loan or deposit, or in other words, interest on interest. It is the mathematical driver of wealth building.*

---

## The Big Picture

Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest **"the eighth wonder of the world. He who understands it, earns it... he who doesn't... pays it."**

Why did Einstein place such value on a simple interest formula? 

Because the human brain is wired to think linearly, not exponentially. If you add $10 to a pile every day, that is linear growth. But if the pile itself grows by 10% every year, the growth feeds on itself. The pile gets bigger, which makes the 10% growth bigger next year, which makes the pile even bigger. 

Over short periods, compound interest looks small and boring. Over long periods (20 to 40 years), it shoots upward like a rocket, transforming small, regular savings into vast fortunes.

This article introduces the compounding formula, the Rule of 72 shortcut, and the massive cost of delaying your investments.

---

## The Compound Interest Formula

To calculate how much an investment will grow over time under compounding, economists use this formula:

$$A = P \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$$

```
                       ┌─────────────────────────┐
                       │   COMPOUNDING FORMULA   │
                       └────────────┬────────────┘
                                    │
         ┌──────────────┬───────────┴───────────┬──────────────┐
         ▼              ▼                       ▼              ▼
┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐
│  A = FUTURE    ││ P = PRINCIPAL  ││ r = ANNUAL     ││ t = TIME IN    │
│    VALUE       ││ The initial    ││     RATE       ││     YEARS      │
│ The final sum  ││ investment     ││ (as a decimal) ││ How long it    │
│ accumulated    ││                ││                ││ compounds      │
└────────────────┘└────────────────┘└────────────────┘└────────────────┘
```
*(Where $n$ is the number of times interest compounds per year—e.g., $n=1$ for annually, $n=12$ for monthly).*

### The Formula in Action:
Imagine you invest **$10,000** at an **8%** annual interest rate, compounded once a year:
- **Year 1:** $10,000 \times 1.08 = \$10,800$ (Earned $800).
- **Year 10:** $10,000 \times (1.08)^{10} = \$21,589$ (The investment doubled).
- **Year 30:** $10,000 \times (1.08)^{30} = \$100,626$ (The investment grew tenfold).
- **Year 40:** $10,000 \times (1.08)^{40} = \$217,245$ (The investment doubled twice more).

Notice how the growth between Year 30 and Year 40 ($116,619) is greater than the entire growth of the first 30 years combined. That is the power of the exponential curve.

## The Shortcut: The Rule of 72

To estimate how long it takes an investment to double at a given compound interest rate without doing complex math, use the **Rule of 72**:

$$\text{Years to Double} \approx \frac{72}{\text{Interest Rate}}$$

- If you earn a **6%** return, your money doubles in **12 years** ($72 / 6 = 12$).
- If you earn a **9%** return, your money doubles in **8 years** ($72 / 9 = 8$).
- If you earn a **12%** return, your money doubles in **6 years** ($72 / 12 = 6$).

## The Cost of Waiting: Why Time Beats Money

The most important lesson of compound interest is that **time is more valuable than capital**.

Let's compare two investors, Susan and David:
- **Susan** starts investing at **age 25**. She saves **$5,000 a year** for only **10 years** (total $50,000 invested) and then stops. She lets it compound at 8% until retirement at **age 65**.
- **David** waits. He starts investing at **age 35**. He saves **$5,000 a year** for **30 years** (total $150,000 invested) until retirement at **age 65**.

```
                   Retirement Balances at Age 65 (at 8% return)
  ▲
$800k┼───────────────────────────────────* Susan: $787,000
     │                                   (Invested $50k early)
     │
$600k┼
     │
$400k┼──────────────────* David: $611,000
     │                  (Invested $150k late)
  0  └───────────────────────────────────►
```

- **Susan's Balance:** **$787,176**.
- **David's Balance:** **$611,729**.

Even though David invested **three times as much money** as Susan, Susan ended up with **$175,000 more** at retirement simply because she gave her money an extra 10 years to compound.

## Key Terms

- **Compound Interest:** Interest earned on interest.
- **Principal:** The initial sum of money.
- **Rule of 72:** A quick formula to estimate when an investment will double.
- **Exponential Growth:** Growth that becomes rapid in proportion to the total number or size.
