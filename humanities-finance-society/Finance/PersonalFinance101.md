# Personal Finance 101

> *Personal finance is the financial management which an individual or a family unit performs to budget, save, spend, and monetary allocate resources over time, taking into account various financial risks and future life events.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why do some people make high salaries but live paycheck to paycheck, while others make modest incomes but retire wealthy?

The difference lies in **personal finance**. Managing money is not just about how much you make; it is about how much you keep, and how effectively you put that money to work.

Personal finance is built on the same foundations as corporate accounting: you have cash flow (income and expenses) and a balance sheet (assets and liabilities). If you manage these elements systematically, you can build security, reduce stress, and achieve financial independence.

This article introduces the personal balance sheet, the concept of net worth, and how to manage debt.

---

## The Personal Balance Sheet: Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth

To assess your financial health, you must calculate your **Net Worth**. This is done by listing everything you own and subtracting everything you owe:

$$\text{Net Worth} = \text{Total Assets} - \text{Total Liabilities}$$

```
                ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────┐
                │             THE PERSONAL LEDGER               │
                ├───────────────────────┬───────────────────────┤
                │       ASSETS          │      LIABILITIES      │
                │   (What you own)      │    (What you owe)     │
                │ - Cash & Savings      │ - Student Loans       │
                │ - Stock Portfolio     │ - Credit Card Debt    │
                │ - House (Value)       │ - Mortgage (Debt)     │
                ├───────────────────────┴───────────────────────┤
                │                 NET WORTH (Owner's Equity)    │
                │                 The value truly yours         │
                └───────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

- **Assets:** Cash in bank accounts, investments (stocks, bonds), real estate value, or retirement accounts.
- **Liabilities:** Credit card debt, student loans, car loans, or mortgages.
- **Net Worth:** If you sold all your assets today and paid off all your debts, this is the cash you would have left.

---

## Cash Flow: Income vs. Expenses

Net worth grows when you save money from your monthly cash flow.
- **Income (Cash In):** Salary, wages, bonuses, investment dividends, or side-hustle revenue.
- **Expenses (Cash Out):** Rent, groceries, taxes, subscription services, dining out, and travel.
- **The Golden Rule:** Spend less than you earn. The difference between your income and expenses is your **savings rate**. Increasing your savings rate is the only way to build capital for investment.

## Managing Debt: Good Debt vs. Bad Debt

Debt is a powerful tool. Like a chainsaw, it can be incredibly useful if handled carefully, or disastrous if misused. Economists divide debt into two categories:

### 1. Good Debt (Investment Debt)
Borrowing money to buy an asset that increases in value or generates income over time.
- *Examples:* A mortgage to buy a home (housing values tend to rise, and it eliminates rent), or a student loan for a high-paying degree (investing in human capital).
- *Nature:* Typically carries low interest rates.

### 2. Bad Debt (Consumption Debt)
Borrowing money to buy things that depreciate (lose value) or are consumed immediately.
- *Examples:* Buying clothes, vacations, or electronics on credit cards.
- *Nature:* Carries high interest rates (often 15% to 25%). If you only pay the minimum balance, a credit card purchase can cost you double or triple the initial price over time.

---

## Key Terms

- **Personal Finance:** The management of personal money, saving, and investing.
- **Asset:** Anything of financial value owned by an individual.
- **Liability:** A financial debt or obligation owed to others.
- **Net Worth:** The value of assets minus liabilities.
- **Savings Rate:** The percentage of disposable income that is saved rather than spent.
- **Bad Debt:** High-interest debt used to fund consumption.
