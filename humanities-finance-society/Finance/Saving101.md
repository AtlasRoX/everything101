# Saving 101

> *Saving is the act of setting aside a portion of current income for future use, rather than spending it on immediate consumption.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why do we save money? 
- If you save all your money but keep it in a safe under your bed, you are losing purchasing power to inflation every day. If you invest all your money in stocks but have a medical emergency tomorrow, you might be forced to sell your stocks at a loss to pay the bill.

Saving is the bridge between **earning** and **investing**. 

The primary goal of saving is not to grow your wealth (investing does that); the goal is to build **liquidity** and **security**. Saving gives you a financial buffer against the unexpected, allowing you to sleep at night and take calculated career and investment risks.

This article introduces the emergency fund, where to store your savings safely, and the cost of keeping too much cash.

---

## The Foundation: The Emergency Fund

The first financial goal for any individual is to build an **Emergency Fund**.

> An **Emergency Fund** is a reserve of cash set aside to cover unexpected, essential expenses (such as a job loss, car repair, or medical bill).

- **How much?** Economists recommend saving **3 to 6 months of essential living expenses** (needs, not wants).
- **Why?** If you are laid off during a recession, the emergency fund gives you 3 to 6 months to find a job without going into credit card debt or being evicted.

---

## Where to Keep Savings: High-Yield Savings Accounts (HYSAs) & CDs

Because you might need your savings tomorrow, you cannot invest them in volatile assets like stocks or real estate. You need your cash to be **liquid** (easily accessible) and **safe** (guaranteed not to lose principal value).

The two primary vehicles are:

### 1. High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)
A bank account that pays a significantly higher interest rate than a traditional checking or savings account.
- **Pros:** High liquidity (you can withdraw money instantly) and federally insured (guaranteed by the government up to $250,000 via the FDIC in the US).
- **Cons:** Interest rates fluctuate with the central bank's base rate.

### 2. Certificates of Deposit (CDs)
A savings product where you agree to lock your money in the bank for a set period (e.g., 6 months, 1 year, or 5 years) in exchange for a fixed interest rate.
- **Pros:** Guaranteed interest rate. If interest rates fall in the market, your CD rate stays high.
- **Cons:** Low liquidity. If you withdraw your money early, you must pay a penalty fee.

---

## The Costs of Saving: Inflation and Opportunity Cost

While saving is vital for security, you should not save *all* your money. Excess cash carries two significant costs:

1. **Inflation Erosion:** If your savings account pays 2% interest, but inflation is 3%, your cash is losing purchasing power. (See [Inflation101](../Economics/Inflation101.md)).
2. **Opportunity Cost:** Keeping cash in a savings account means you are giving up the higher returns you could have earned by investing that money in stocks, bonds, or real estate over the long run. (See [Investing101](./Investing101.md)).

> **Aha! moment:** Save for your short-term needs (0 to 3 years) and emergencies. Invest for your long-term goals (5+ years).

---

## Key Terms

- **Saving:** Retaining income for future use.
- **Emergency Fund:** A cash buffer of 3-6 months of expenses for emergencies.
- **Liquidity:** The ease with which an asset can be converted into cash without losing value.
- **High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA):** A federally insured bank account with high interest rates.
- **Certificate of Deposit (CD):** A savings account with a fixed interest rate and lock-in period.
- **Opportunity Cost:** The lost potential gain from other alternatives when one alternative is chosen.
