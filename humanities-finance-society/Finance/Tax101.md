# Tax 101

> *A tax is a compulsory financial charge or other levy imposed on a taxpayer by a governmental organization to fund government spending and public expenditures.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why do we pay taxes? 
- If you want a functional legal system, paved highways, public schools, clean parks, and national defense, someone has to pay for them. 

Because these are **public goods**—which are non-excludable and non-rivalrous—private businesses cannot profitably produce them. (See [Microeconomics101](../Economics/Microeconomics101.md)). Therefore, the government must collect revenue from citizens to fund these services.

Taxes are the foundation of **public finance**. However, how taxes are designed has a massive impact on the economy: they can change consumer behavior, influence business investments, and redistribute wealth.

This article introduces the difference between progressive and regressive taxes, how tax brackets work, and the distinction between income and capital gains.

---

## Tax Structures: Who Pays More?

Governments organize taxes in three main ways, depending on how the tax burden falls on different income groups:

| Tax Structure | Definition | Example | Impact on Poor vs. Rich |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Progressive Tax** | The tax rate increases as the taxpayer's income increases. | Personal Income Tax. | The rich pay a larger percentage of their income than the poor. |
| **Regressive Tax** | The tax rate remains flat, meaning it takes a larger percentage of income from low earners than high earners. | Sales Tax (VAT). | The poor are hit harder because they spend a larger share of their income on basic goods. |
| **Proportional Tax (Flat Tax)** | The tax rate is identical for everyone, regardless of income. | Flat 15% income tax. | Simple, but considered unfair by proponents of progressive systems. |

## Understanding Marginal Tax Brackets

A common source of confusion in personal finance is the **marginal tax bracket**. 

If you hear that the tax rate for the highest bracket is **35%**, and your income just crossed into that bracket, does that mean the government takes 35% of all your money?

No. Progressive income taxes use a **ladder system**:

```
      Income ($)
        ▲
        │
$100k+  ┼───────────────── Marginal Bracket C: 35% (Only income above $100k taxed at 35%)
        │
$50k    ┼───────────────── Marginal Bracket B: 20% (Income between $50k and $100k taxed at 20%)
        │
$0      ┼───────────────── Marginal Bracket A: 10% (Income below $50k taxed at 10%)
        └─────────────────► Tax Rate (%)
```

Each rate applies **only** to the income earned within that specific range. If you earn $101,000, only the last $1,000 is taxed at 35%. You do not take home less money overall by moving into a higher tax bracket.

## Income Tax vs. Capital Gains Tax

How you make money determines how you are taxed:

- **Income Tax (Active Income):** Taxes charged on money you earn from labor (wages, salaries, tips). Rates are typically progressive and relatively high.
- **Capital Gains Tax (Passive Income):** Taxes charged on the profit you make when you sell an asset (like stocks or real estate) for more than you bought it for. 
  - **Short-Term Capital Gains:** Assets held for less than a year (often taxed at standard income tax rates).
  - **Long-Term Capital Gains:** Assets held for more than a year (often taxed at a **lower rate** than income tax, to encourage long-term investment).

---

## Key Terms

- **Tax:** A mandatory financial levy collected by the government.
- **Progressive Tax:** A tax system where high earners pay a higher percentage rate.
- **Regressive Tax:** A tax taking a larger percentage of income from low-income earners.
- **Marginal Tax Rate:** The tax rate paid on the next dollar of income earned.
- **Income Tax:** A tax levied on personal wages and salaries.
- **Capital Gains Tax:** A tax levied on the profit from selling investments or property.
- **Tax Deduction:** An expense that can be subtracted from your taxable income, lowering your overall tax bill.
