# Stocks 101

> *A stock (also known as equity) is a security that represents the ownership of a fraction of a corporation, entitling the owner of the stock to a proportion of the corporation's assets and profits.*

---

## The Big Picture

What happens when you buy one share of Apple (AAPL) on your smartphone?
- You didn't just buy a digital numbers ticket. You bought an actual piece of the company. 
- You are now a **shareholder**. You own a tiny fraction of Apple's buildings, patents, inventory, and future profits. If Apple hires smart engineers and sells more phones, you share in their success.

Stocks are the most powerful wealth-building asset class in history. Over the past century, the US stock market has returned an average of **10% per year** (about 7% after adjusting for inflation). 

However, because stock prices fluctuate daily based on market emotions and corporate results, stocks are volatile, requiring a long-term perspective.

This article introduces what stock is, how stock markets work, growth vs. value stocks, and the P/E valuation ratio.

---

## What Is Stock? The Ownership Split

If a corporation is valued at $1 million, and issues **10,000 shares of stock**:
- Each share represents **0.01%** of the company ($100 value).
- If you buy 100 shares, you own **1%** of the company.
- As a shareholder, you receive:
  1. **Voting Rights:** The power to vote for the Board of Directors. (See [CorporateGovernance101](../Business/CorporateGovernance101.md)).
  2. **Dividends:** Your share of the company's profits, paid out in cash periodically (usually quarterly).

---

## How Stock Markets Work: Exchanges & Tickers

Stocks are bought and sold on **Stock Exchanges** (such as the New York Stock Exchange or NASDAQ).
- **Stock Ticker:** A unique abbreviation used to identify a company's shares (e.g., Apple is `AAPL`, Microsoft is `MSFT`, Tesla is `TSLA`).
- **The Market Price:** Determined by supply and demand. (See [Price101](../Economics/Price101.md)). If a company reports high profits, demand for its stock increases, and the price rises. If it reports losses, sellers dump the stock, and the price falls.

---

## Growth vs. Value Stocks

Investors divide stocks into two main categories:

- **Growth Stocks:** Companies expected to grow sales and earnings much faster than the market average. They reinvest all their profits into expanding the business, so they **rarely pay dividends** (e.g., Amazon, Tesla). Investors buy them for capital gains.
- **Value Stocks:** Mature, established companies that are currently trading for less than their intrinsic value, or that pay high, reliable **dividends** (e.g., Coca-Cola, Johnson & Johnson). Investors buy them for safety and income yield.

---

## Stock Valuation: The P/E Ratio

How do you know if a stock's price is cheap or expensive? You cannot look at the share price alone—a $10 share is not necessarily cheaper than a $100 share. 

Instead, you use the **P/E (Price-to-Earnings) Ratio**:

$$\text{P/E Ratio} = \frac{\text{Market Price per Share}}{\text{Earnings per Share (EPS)}}$$

- **What it means:** The P/E ratio tells you how many dollars investors are willing to pay for every $1 of the company's earnings.
- **Interpretation:** A high P/E ratio (e.g., 40) means investors expect rapid future growth and are willing to pay a premium. A low P/E ratio (e.g., 12) means the stock is either in a slow-growth industry or is currently undervalued (a bargain).

---

## Key Terms

- **Stock (Equity):** Share of ownership in a corporation.
- **Shareholder:** An owner of shares in a company.
- **Dividend:** A portion of corporate profits paid to shareholders.
- **Stock Exchange:** A marketplace where stocks are traded.
- **Earnings Per Share (EPS):** A company's net profit divided by the number of common shares outstanding.
- **P/E Ratio:** Price-to-Earnings ratio; a key metric for stock valuation.
