# Investing 101

> *Investing is the act of allocating resources, usually money, with the expectation of generating an income or profit in the future.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why invest? Why not just keep your money in a savings account where it is safe?
- If you save, you protect your cash. But over time, inflation eats away at your money's purchasing power. If inflation is 3%, your money loses half its value in 24 years. (See [Inflation101](../Economics/Inflation101.md)).

**Investing** is the only way to beat inflation and grow your wealth. 

When you invest, you are putting your money to work in the economy: you are lending it to governments, buying pieces of profitable corporations, or buying real estate. In exchange for taking this risk, you receive returns that compound over time, building financial independence.

This article introduces the difference between investing and speculation, the risk-return trade-off, and the four major asset classes.

---

## Investing vs. Speculation: What's the Difference?

Many people lose their savings because they mistake speculation for investing:

- **Investing:** Allocating capital to assets that have intrinsic value and generate cash flow (like stocks paying dividends, or real estate collecting rent) based on thorough analysis. It is long-term and focused on safety of principal and reasonable returns.
- **Speculation (Gambling):** Buying an asset solely because you hope the price will rise tomorrow, even though the asset does not generate any income or value (e.g., meme stocks, buying lottery tickets, or short-term day trading). It is short-term, carries high risk, and is based on emotion and hype.

## The Risk-Return Spectrum

Every investment falls on a spectrum. If you want higher returns, you must accept higher risk:

```
Potential Return (%)
  ▲                                                      * Stocks (Equities)
  │                                                     /
  │                                                    /
  │                                   * Real Estate   /
  │                                  /               /
  │                  * Bonds (Debt) /               /
  │                 /              /               /
  │  * Cash/HYSA   /              /               /
  └─*─────────────*──────────────*───────────────*───► Investment Risk
  0 High Safety                                  Low Safety
```

- **Cash/Cash Equivalents (Savings Accounts, CDs):** Virtually zero risk, but returns are low and often fail to beat inflation. (See [Saving101](./Saving101.md)).
- **Bonds (Debt):** Moderate risk. You lend money to a company or government. They pay you fixed interest, which is safer than stocks but offers lower growth. (See [Bonds101](./Bonds101.md)).
- **Real Estate:** Moderate-to-high risk. Physical property generates rent and appreciates in value, but requires maintenance and is hard to sell quickly (low liquidity).
- **Stocks (Equities):** High risk. You buy a share of a company. If the company succeeds, you make massive profits; if it fails, you can lose your entire investment. (See [Stocks101](./Stocks101.md)).

## Active vs. Passive Investing

How should you manage your investments?
- **Active Investing:** Trying to beat the market average by constantly buying and selling individual stocks. This requires hours of research and high fees, and research shows that **over 90% of active professional investors fail to beat the market average over the long run**.
- **Passive Investing:** Accepting the market average by buying index funds or ETFs that track the entire stock market, holding them for decades. This is low-cost, requires zero effort, and outperforms the vast majority of active traders. (See [ETF101](./ETF101.md)).

---

## Key Terms

- **Investing:** Allocating capital to assets with the expectation of future income or growth.
- **Speculation:** High-risk transactions focusing on short-term price fluctuations.
- **Asset Class:** A grouping of similar financial instruments (e.g., stocks, bonds).
- **Active Investing:** Hands-on buying and selling of individual assets to beat the market.
- **Passive Investing:** Buy-and-hold investing tracking market indices.
