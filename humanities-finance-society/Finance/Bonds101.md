# Bonds 101

> *A bond is a fixed-income instrument that represents a loan made by an investor to a borrower (typically corporate or governmental).*

---

## The Big Picture

When a government wants to build a new highway, or a corporation wants to expand its factories, they need millions of dollars. They can borrow it from a bank, but banks have limits. 

Instead, they turn to the public markets and issue **bonds**.

When you buy a bond, you are playing the role of the bank: you are lending your money directly to the government or company. In exchange, the borrower promises to pay you a fixed interest rate (a coupon) regularly, and return your original money (face value) on a specific date in the future (maturity date).

Bonds are the foundation of **fixed-income investing**. They are safer than stocks and provide predictable income, making them a vital stabilizing force in any investment portfolio.

This article introduces the anatomy of a bond, the difference between government and corporate debt, and how interest rates affect bond prices.

## The Anatomy of a Bond: Key Terms

Every bond has three defined variables:

```
                          ┌─────────────────────────┐
                          │    ANATOMY OF A BOND    │
                          └────────────┬────────────┘
                                       │
         ┌─────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                             ▼                             ▼
┌──────────────────┐          ┌──────────────────┐          ┌──────────────────┐
│   FACE VALUE     │          │   COUPON RATE    │          │  MATURITY DATE   │
│ (Par Value)      │          │ (Interest Rate)  │          │ The date the     │
│ The principal to │          │ The annual percentage│      │ principal is paid│
│ be returned      │          │ paid to investor │          │ back in full     │
└──────────────────┘          └──────────────────┘          └──────────────────┘
```

- **Face Value (Par Value):** The amount of money the borrower is borrowing, and promises to pay back at the end. (e.g., $1,000).
- **Coupon Rate:** The annual interest rate paid by the issuer. If a $1,000 bond has a 5% coupon, the issuer pays you **$50 a year** (usually paid semi-annually).
- **Maturity Date:** The specific date in the future when the loan ends and the issuer returns the Face Value to the investor. (e.g., a 10-year bond).

## Issuer Types: Government vs. Corporate

- **Government Bonds (Treasuries):** Issued by national governments.
  - **Risk:** Extremely low. The US government has never defaulted on its debt, making US Treasury bonds the theoretical "risk-free" baseline of finance.
  - **Return:** Low interest rates.
- **Corporate Bonds:** Issued by private companies.
  - **Risk:** Higher. A company could go bankrupt and default on its debt.
  - **Return:** High interest rates (often called "high-yield" or "junk bonds" for risky companies) to compensate investors for taking the risk.

---

## The Inverse Rule: Bond Prices vs. Interest Rates

Once a bond is issued, it can be bought and sold in the market before its maturity date. The price of a bond in the market is determined by changes in the central bank's interest rates:

> **The Inverse Rule:** When interest rates in the economy **rise**, the price of existing bonds **falls**. When interest rates **fall**, the price of existing bonds **rises**.

### Why?
Imagine you own a bond paying a fixed **5%** interest.
- Next month, the central bank raises interest rates. New bonds are issued paying **6%** interest.
- If you try to sell your 5% bond in the market, nobody will buy it at its original price, because they can get 6% elsewhere. 
- To attract a buyer, you must discount the price of your bond below its face value.

---

## Key Terms

- **Bond:** A debt security where the issuer owes the holder a debt.
- **Face Value (Par):** The principal amount of a bond.
- **Coupon:** The interest payment made to a bondholder.
- **Maturity:** The period of time after which a bond's principal is repaid.
- **Default:** Failure to pay back a loan or meet bond obligations.
- **Yield to Maturity (YTM):** The total return anticipated on a bond if it is held until it matures.
