# Money 101

> *Money is any item or verifiable record that is generally accepted as payment for goods and services and repayment of debts in a particular country or socio-economic context.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why does a piece of green paper with a picture of a historical president have the power to buy a sandwich, a car, or pay for a college education? 
- You can't eat the paper. You can't wear it. 
- It has value only because we all **agree** that it has value.

Money is one of humanity's oldest and most powerful inventions. Before money, societies relied on barter (swapping goods directly), which was highly inefficient because it required a double coincidence of wants. (See [Market101](../Economics/Market101.md)).

By acting as a universal intermediary, money unlocked specialized labor and global trade.

This article introduces the three functions of money, the difference between commodity and fiat money, and the history of currency systems.

## The Three Functions of Money

To be considered "money," an asset must satisfy three functions:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │   FUNCTIONS OF MONEY    │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                               ▼                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐            ┌──────────────────┐            ┌──────────────────┐
│MEDIUM OF EXCHANGE│            │ UNIT OF ACCOUNT  │            │  STORE OF VALUE  │
│ Prevents barter; │            │ The baseline     │            │ Can be saved and │
│ accepted by all  │            │ measure of cost  │            │ spent in the     │
│ for transactions │            │ and value        │            │ future           │
└──────────────────┘            └──────────────────┘            └──────────────────┘
```

1. **Medium of Exchange:** An intermediary used to facilitate trade. It allows you to sell your labor for money and use that money to buy bread, avoiding the barter bottleneck.
2. **Unit of Account:** A standard numerical measure of the market value of goods and services. It provides a common denominator (e.g., dollars, euros) so you can compare the cost of a phone ($800) vs. a coffee ($4) easily.
3. **Store of Value:** An asset that maintains its purchasing power over time. If you earn $100 today, you can save it and buy a $100 jacket next month. *Note: Inflation slowly erodes this function over time.* (See [Inflation101](../Economics/Inflation101.md)).

## Commodity vs. Fiat Money

Throughout history, money has evolved from physical materials to abstract trust:

### 1. Commodity Money
Money that has intrinsic value—it has value outside of being used as currency.
- *Examples:* Gold, silver, salt, tobacco, or cigarettes (in prison camps).
- *Pros:* Intrinsic security; you cannot easily print more gold or salt, preventing hyperinflation.
- *Cons:* Heavy to transport, difficult to divide into small change, and supply is limited by mining capability.

### 2. Fiat Money
Money that has no intrinsic value and is not backed by a physical commodity. Its value is established by government decree (law) and public trust.
- *Examples:* The US Dollar, Euro, British Pound, Japanese Yen.
- *Pros:* Easy to transport, highly divisible, and the government can adjust the money supply to support the economy.
- *Cons:* If the government prints too much, the currency loses its value, leading to inflation or hyperinflation. (See [Inflation101](../Economics/Inflation101.md)).

## The Gold Standard: The Bridge System

Before modern pure fiat currencies, countries used the **Gold Standard** (from the 19th century to 1971):
- Paper money was backed by gold.
- A citizen could walk into a bank and exchange a $20 paper bill for a physical gold coin.
- **The Shift:** In 1971, US President Richard Nixon ended the convertibility of the US dollar into gold (the "Nixon Shock"), converting the global monetary system into a system of pure floating fiat currencies.

---

## Key Terms

- **Money:** A generally accepted medium of exchange.
- **Barter:** Directly swapping goods without money.
- **Commodity Money:** Money with intrinsic physical value (e.g., gold).
- **Fiat Money:** Currency established by government decree with no intrinsic value.
- **Gold Standard:** A monetary system where paper currency is directly exchangeable for a fixed amount of gold.
