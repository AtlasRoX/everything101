# Retirement 101

> *Retirement is the withdrawal from one's active working life, typically requiring a self-sustaining asset portfolio to fund living expenses without active employment.*

---

## The Big Picture

For most of human history, "retirement" did not exist. You worked until you were physically unable to do so, and then you relied on your children or charity to survive.

In the 20th century, governments introduced pensions (like Social Security in the US). But today, demographic shifts mean state pensions are strained. 

The responsibility of retirement has shifted to the individual. 

Fortunately, governments provide powerful tools to encourage saving: **tax-advantaged retirement accounts** like the 401(k) and IRA. By combining these accounts with compound interest, anyone can build a self-sustaining portfolio over their career.

This article introduces the main retirement accounts, the debate between Traditional and Roth tax structures, the 4% withdrawal rule, and the FIRE movement.

---

## Key Retirement Accounts: 401(k) and IRA

In the United States (and similarly under other names in other developed countries), there are two primary retirement savings vehicles:

### 1. 401(k) (Employer-Sponsored)
An account set up by your employer. A portion of your paycheck is automatically redirected into this account before you receive it, and invested in mutual funds or ETFs.
- **The Matching Bonus:** Many employers offer a **401(k) match** (e.g., if you invest 5% of your salary, the employer matches it with another 5% for free). This is literally free money and should be the first goal of any financial plan.

### 2. IRA (Individual Retirement Account)
An account you set up yourself at a brokerage (like Vanguard or Fidelity), giving you complete control over what stocks, bonds, or ETFs you buy.

## The Tax Split: Traditional vs. Roth

Both 401(k)s and IRAs are split into two tax structures, determining *when* the government taxes your money:

```
                              ┌─────────────────────────┐
                              │    TAX STRUCTURES       │
                              └────────────┬────────────┘
                                           │
         ┌─────────────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                                   ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                                ┌──────────────────┐
│   TRADITIONAL    │                                                │       ROTH       │
│ Taxed LATER      │                                                │ Taxed NOW        │
│ (Pre-tax dollars)│                                                │ (Post-tax dollars)
└────────┬─────────┘                                                └────────┬─────────┘
         │                                                                   │
         ▼                                                                   ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                                ┌──────────────────┐
│ Save taxes today;│                                                │ Pay taxes today; │
│ Pay tax on       │                                                │ Withdraw 100%    │
│ withdrawals      │                                                │ tax-free at      │
│ in retirement    │                                                │ retirement       │
└──────────────────┘                                                └──────────────────┘
```

- **Traditional:** You get a tax deduction today. If you earn $50,000 and invest $5,000, you are only taxed on $45,000 this year. However, when you withdraw the money at age 65, you must pay income tax on the withdrawals.
- **Roth:** You get no tax deduction today. You pay full income tax on your salary, and invest post-tax money. However, the money grows tax-free, and when you withdraw it at age 65, **you pay zero tax** on either the principal or the massive capital gains.

## Safe Withdrawal Rates: The 4% Rule

Once you retire, how much of your portfolio can you spend every year without running out of money before you die?

According to the famous Trinity Study, retirees can use **The 4% Rule**:

> **The 4% Rule:** You can safely withdraw 4% of your total retirement portfolio in the first year of retirement, and adjust that dollar amount for inflation every year after, with a 95% probability that your portfolio will last at least 30 years.

- **The Math:** To calculate how much money you need to retire, multiply your desired annual spending by **25**:

$$\text{Retirement Target} = \text{Annual Spending Needs} \times 25$$

- *Example:* If you need $40,000 a year to cover your expenses, your target portfolio size is **$1 million** ($40,000 × 25).

---

## The FIRE Movement

A growing community of young people is using the 4% rule to retire decades early. This is the **FIRE Movement (Financial Independence, Retire Early)**.
- By living minimally and saving 50% to 70% of their income, FIRE adherents build their 25x portfolio in their 30s or 40s, freeing them from active labor for the rest of their lives.

---

## Key Terms

- **Retirement:** Withdrawal from active labor.
- **401(k):** An employer-sponsored, tax-advantaged retirement savings plan.
- **IRA:** An Individual Retirement Account with tax benefits.
- **Traditional Account:** Pre-tax investing; withdrawals are taxed.
- **Roth Account:** Post-tax investing; withdrawals are tax-free.
- **4% Rule:** The safe withdrawal rate for retirement assets.
- **FIRE:** Financial Independence, Retire Early.
