# Interest 101

> *Interest is the cost of borrowing money or the reward for lending it, typically expressed as an annual percentage of the loan amount.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why does a bank pay you 2% to keep your money in a savings account? And why do they charge you 15% when you borrow money to buy a car?

The answer lies in **interest**. Interest is the "price" of money. 

If you lend your money, you are giving up the ability to spend it today, taking a risk that you might not get it back, and losing purchasing power to inflation. Interest is your reward for taking these risks and delaying your gratification. 

Conversely, for the borrower, interest is the cost of getting money *now* instead of waiting to save up.

This article introduces the calculation of simple vs. compound interest, the difference between real and nominal rates, and the concept of APR.

---

## Simple vs. Compound Interest

There are two primary ways interest is calculated:

### 1. Simple Interest
Interest is calculated only on the initial amount of money borrowed or invested (the **principal**):

$$\text{Interest} = \text{Principal} \times \text{Rate} \times \text{Time}$$

- **Example:** You invest $1,000 at a 10% simple interest rate for 3 years.
  - Year 1: You earn 10% of $1,000 = $100.
  - Year 2: You earn 10% of $1,000 = $100.
  - Year 3: You earn 10% of $1,000 = $100.
  - **Total Value:** $1,300.

### 2. Compound Interest
Interest is calculated on the initial principal AND on the accumulated interest from previous periods. This is "interest on interest."

$$\text{Future Value} = \text{Principal} \times (1 + r)^t$$

- **Example:** You invest $1,000 at a 10% compound interest rate for 3 years.
  - Year 1: You earn 10% of $1,000 = $100. (Total = $1,100).
  - Year 2: You earn 10% of $1,100 = $110. (Total = $1,210).
  - Year 3: You earn 10% of $1,210 = $121. (Total = $1,331).
  - **Total Value:** $1,331 ($31 more than simple interest).

Over long periods, compound interest grows exponentially, which is why it is the engine of wealth building. (See [CompoundInterest101](./CompoundInterest101.md)).

## Real vs. Nominal Interest Rates: The Inflation Tax

When you see a bank advertisement offering a 5% interest rate, this is the **nominal interest rate**—the face value rate without adjusting for inflation.

But if you earn 5% interest while the inflation rate is 3%, the goods you want to buy also became 3% more expensive. Your true increase in purchasing power is only 2%. This is the **real interest rate**:

$$\text{Real Interest Rate} \approx \text{Nominal Interest Rate} - \text{Inflation Rate}$$

- If nominal interest is **2%** and inflation is **5%**, your real interest rate is **-3%**. You are actually losing wealth by keeping your cash in that account.

## APR: The True Cost of Borrowing

When taking out a loan (like a mortgage or credit card), you will see two numbers: the interest rate and the **APR (Annual Percentage Rate)**.

- **Interest Rate:** The basic cost of borrowing the principal.
- **APR:** The interest rate PLUS any additional fees, broker costs, or insurance charges required to get the loan, expressed as an annual rate.
- **Rule:** Always look at the APR when comparing loans, as it represents the true, complete cost of borrowing.

---

## Key Terms

- **Interest:** The fee charged for borrowing money or earned for lending it.
- **Principal:** The original sum of money lent or invested.
- **Simple Interest:** Interest paid only on the original principal.
- **Compound Interest:** Interest paid on both principal and accumulated interest.
- **Nominal Interest Rate:** The stated interest rate of a loan or investment.
- **Real Interest Rate:** The interest rate adjusted for inflation.
- **APR:** Annual Percentage Rate; the complete cost of a loan including fees.
