# Mutual Funds 101

> *A mutual fund is a type of financial vehicle that pools money from many investors to purchase a diversified portfolio of securities, managed by professional fund managers.*

---

## The Big Picture

If you have $1,000 to invest:
- If you buy individual stocks, you might only be able to afford a few shares of a couple of companies. If one of those companies fails, you lose a significant portion of your savings (high unsystematic risk).
- How do you get a diversified portfolio of hundreds of stocks with only $1,000?

You buy a **mutual fund**.

A mutual fund acts as a financial co-op. Thousands of investors pool their money together. The fund manager takes this giant pool of cash and buys hundreds of different stocks and bonds. Each investor owns a small slice of the entire pool, providing instant diversification at a low cost.

This article introduces the Net Asset Value (NAV), the cost of expense ratios, and the debate over active management.

---

## Net Asset Value (NAV): The Share Price

Unlike stocks, which trade on an exchange and change price every second, mutual funds are priced only once a day at the close of the market (4:00 PM EST). 

This price is the **Net Asset Value (NAV)**:

$$\text{NAV} = \frac{\text{Total Value of Fund's Assets} - \text{Total Liabilities}}{\text{Number of Shares Outstanding}}$$

When you buy shares of a mutual fund, you pay the NAV calculated at the end of that trading day.

---

## The Cost of Mutual Funds: Expense Ratios & Loads

Mutual funds are not free. Fund managers charge fees to run the fund, which are deducted directly from your investment returns:

- **Expense Ratio:** The annual fee charged as a percentage of your total investment. If an expense ratio is **1%**, and you have $10,000 in the fund, the company deducts **$100 a year** for management fees. (A good fee is under 0.2%; high fees above 1% eat up your compounding returns).
- **Sales Loads:** Commissions charged when you buy or sell shares:
  - **Front-End Load:** A fee charged when you buy shares (e.g., a 5% fee means only $95 of your $100 is actually invested).
  - **Back-End Load:** A fee charged when you sell shares.
  - **No-Load Funds:** Funds that charge no sales commissions. (Always prefer no-load funds).

## The Active vs. Passive Debate

Mutual funds are typically classified by how they are managed:

```
                              ┌─────────────────────────┐
                              │     MUTUAL FUND TYPE    │
                              └────────────┬────────────┘
                                           │
         ┌─────────────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                                   ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                                ┌──────────────────┐
│  ACTIVE FUNDS    │                                                │  PASSIVE FUNDS   │
│ Fund manager     │                                                │ Index funds      │
│ researches and   │                                                │ tracking a market│
│ selects stocks   │                                                │ basket (S&P 500) │
└────────┬─────────┘                                                └────────┬─────────┘
         │                                                                   │
         ▼                                                                   ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                                ┌──────────────────┐
│ High fees (1%+); │                                                │ Low fees (<0.1%)│
│ Underperforms    │                                                │ Matches market   │
│ index long-term  │                                                │ average returns  │
└──────────────────┘                                                └──────────────────┘
```

Historically, active funds were the standard. However, data shows that the salaries of fund managers and trading transaction costs (expense ratios) make active funds drag down performance. **Passive index funds** (introduced by John Bogle, founder of Vanguard) offer a cheaper, more reliable alternative for long-term investors.

---

## Key Terms

- **Mutual Fund:** A pooled investment vehicle managed by professionals.
- **Net Asset Value (NAV):** The market value of a single share of a mutual fund.
- **Expense Ratio:** The annual management fee charged by a fund.
- **Load:** A sales commission fee.
- **Active Management:** A fund strategy aiming to beat the market index through research and trading.
- **Passive Management:** A fund strategy aiming to replicate the performance of a market index.
