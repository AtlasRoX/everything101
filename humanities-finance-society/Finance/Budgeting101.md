# Budgeting 101

> *A budget is a tactical plan that tracks income and expenditures over a specific period, helping individuals allocate resources to reach their financial goals.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why do so many budgets fail? 
- People often think of a budget as a financial prison—a set of rules designed to stop them from buying coffee, dining with friends, or enjoying hobbies. Because it feels like deprivation, they quit after a few weeks.

A real budget is not about restriction; it is about **clarity and control**. A budget gives you permission to spend your money on what you value most, while ensuring you save enough to secure your future. 

Without a budget, money leaks out of your bank account on small, unexamined subscriptions and habits, leaving you with nothing for your major life goals (like buying a home or traveling).

This article introduces fixed vs. variable costs, the famous 50/30/20 rule, and different budgeting methods.

---

## Fixed vs. Variable Expenses

To create a budget, you must audit your monthly expenses and divide them into two categories:

- **Fixed Expenses (Needs):** Costs that remain constant every month and are difficult to change quickly. Examples: rent/mortgage, insurance premiums, utility bills, and minimum loan payments.
- **Variable Expenses (Wants/Adjustables):** Costs that fluctuate depending on your choices. Examples: dining out, groceries, concert tickets, clothing, and travel.

> **Rule of Thumb:** If you face a financial emergency (like a job loss), variable expenses can be cut to zero immediately. Fixed expenses require months of planning or moving to change, making them the primary source of financial risk.

## The 50/30/20 Rule: A Simple Template

Popularized by Senator Elizabeth Warren, the **50/30/20 Rule** is a simple, percentage-based template for allocating net income:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │    THE 50/30/20 RULE    │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                               ▼                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐            ┌──────────────────┐            ┌──────────────────┐
│    NEEDS (50%)   │            │    WANTS (30%)   │            │   SAVINGS (20%)  │
│ Survival essentials│          │ Fun & lifestyle  │            │ Emergency funds, │
│ (Rent, bills,    │            │ (Dining, travel, │            │ investing, & debt│
│  groceries)      │            │  hobbies)        │            │ paydown          │
└──────────────────┘            └──────────────────┘            └──────────────────┘
```

1. **50% for Needs:** Half of your take-home pay goes to essential survival expenses. (If your needs exceed 50%, you are "house poor" or carrying too much fixed debt).
2. **30% for Wants:** Up to 30% goes to non-essential lifestyle spending. This ensures you still enjoy your life today.
3. **20% for Savings:** At least 20% is set aside immediately for your future—emergency funds, retirement accounts, or paying off credit card debt.

## Two Popular Budgeting Methods

If percentage templates don't fit, you can use structured budgeting styles:

### 1. Zero-Based Budgeting
Every single dollar of income is assigned to a specific category at the start of the month, so that **Income - Expenses = $0**.
- If you have $4,000 in income, you assign $1,500 to rent, $500 to groceries, $1,000 to investments, $500 to travel savings, and $500 to entertainment. No dollar is left "floating" in your bank account, which prevents impulsive spending.

### 2. The Envelope System (Cash Budgeting)
You withdraw cash for your variable expense categories (groceries, entertainment, dining out) and place it into labeled paper envelopes.
- You are only allowed to spend what is inside the envelope for that category. Once the "Dining Out" envelope is empty, you cannot eat out for the rest of the month. This uses the physical pain of handing over cash to limit spending.

---

## Key Terms

- **Budget:** A plan comparing estimated income and expenditures.
- **Fixed Expense:** An unchanging cost paid regularly (e.g., rent).
- **Variable Expense:** A cost that changes depending on consumption (e.g., entertainment).
- **50/30/20 Rule:** A percentage-based allocation of income for needs, wants, and savings.
- **Zero-Based Budgeting:** Allocating every dollar of income to an expense category until nothing is left over.
