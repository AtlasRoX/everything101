# Bitcoin 101

> *Bitcoin is a decentralized digital currency, without a central bank or single administrator, that can be sent from user to user on the peer-to-peer bitcoin network.*

---

## The Big Picture

In the midst of the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, as trust in central banks and large financial institutions collapsed, an anonymous programmer (or group of programmers) using the pseudonym **Satoshi Nakamoto** released a whitepaper titled: *Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System*.

In 2009, the Bitcoin network launched. 

Bitcoin was the world's first successful cryptocurrency. It solved a computer science problem that had blocked digital cash for decades: the **double-spending problem** (preventing someone from copying a digital coin and spending it twice). 

By creating the first system of **digital scarcity**, Bitcoin laid the foundation for the entire modern cryptocurrency industry.

This article introduces the Satoshi whitepaper, the concept of digital scarcity, the mining process, and the halving cycle.

---

## Digital Scarcity: The 21 Million Cap

Why does paper fiat currency lose its value over time? Because central banks can print unlimited amounts of it, inflating the supply. (See [Inflation101](../Economics/Inflation101.md)).

Satoshi Nakamoto designed Bitcoin with the opposite logic: **fixed scarcity**.

- **The Limit:** The Bitcoin code dictates that there will only ever be **21 million bitcoins** in existence.
- **The Distribution:** Bitcoins are not created all at once. They are released slowly into the network over time through mining. The last bitcoin is projected to be mined around the year 2140.
- **Divisibility:** You do not need to buy a whole bitcoin. A single bitcoin can be divided down to eight decimal places. The smallest unit ($0.00000001$ BTC) is called a **Satoshi**.

---

## How Bitcoins Are Created: Mining

Bitcoin uses the **Proof of Work (PoW)** consensus mechanism. (See [Cryptocurrency101](./Cryptocurrency101.md)). Special computers on the network, called **miners**, validate transactions and compete to solve a cryptographic puzzle.

- **The Block Reward:** The miner who solves the puzzle first gets to write the next block of transactions onto the blockchain, and is rewarded with newly minted bitcoins. This is the only way new bitcoins enter circulation.
- **Difficulty Adjustment:** The Bitcoin network is designed to release a new block of transactions approximately **every 10 minutes**. If more miners join the network (making the puzzle-solving faster), the code automatically increases the difficulty of the mathematical puzzle to keep the rate at 10 minutes.

---

## The Halving Cycle: Halting Inflation

To ensure the 21 million cap is never exceeded, the Bitcoin code includes a mechanism called **The Halving** (or Halvening):

> **The Halving:** Approximately every four years (or every 210,000 blocks), the reward given to miners for writing a block is cut in half.

- **2009:** The block reward was **50 BTC** every 10 minutes.
- **2012:** The reward halved to **25 BTC**.
- **2016:** The reward halved to **12.5 BTC**.
- **2020:** The reward halved to **6.25 BTC**.
- **2024:** The reward halved to **3.125 BTC**.
- This halving cycle continues until the reward hits zero around 2140, making Bitcoin a highly **deflationary** asset.

---

## The Debate: Store of Value vs. Currency

As Bitcoin grew, a debate emerged over its true purpose:

- **Medium of Exchange (Currency):** Early adopters hoped Bitcoin would be used for everyday purchases (like buying coffee). However, slow transaction speeds (only 7 transactions per second globally) and volatile price swings make it difficult to use as an everyday currency.
- **Store of Value (Digital Gold):** Many investors now view Bitcoin not as a currency, but as a digital alternative to gold. Because it is scarce, durable, divisible, and secure, they buy it to hold long-term to protect their wealth from fiat inflation.

---

## Key Terms

- **Bitcoin (BTC):** The first decentralized peer-to-peer cryptocurrency.
- **Satoshi Nakamoto:** The pseudonymous creator of Bitcoin.
- **Double-Spending:** The risk that a digital transaction can be duplicated.
- **Satoshi (Sat):** The smallest unit of Bitcoin ($10^{-8}$ BTC).
- **Mining:** The process of validating transactions and minting new Bitcoins.
- **Halving:** The cutting in half of the block reward every four years.
