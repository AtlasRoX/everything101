# Politics 101

> *Politics is the process by which groups of people make collective decisions. It is the negotiation of who gets what, when, and how.*

---

## The Big Picture

Many people equate politics with corrupt politicians, electoral campaigns, or arguments on television. But in political science, politics is much broader: it is the fundamental human activity of **resolving conflict and making group decisions**.

Whenever two or more people live together, they have different desires, values, and resource needs. Politics is the mechanism we use to decide how resources are distributed, how rules are set, and who gets to enforce them. It exists in families, classrooms, corporations, and nation-states. 

This article introduces the core concepts of political science: power, authority, legitimacy, and the concept of "the state."

---

## What Is Politics?

At its heart, politics is about managing **scarcity and disagreement**. If resources were infinite and everyone agreed on everything, politics would not exist.

The political scientist Harold Lasswell famously defined politics as **"who gets what, when, [and] how."**
- **Who:** The citizens, interest groups, classes, or leaders.
- **What:** The resources, rights, taxes, public services, or values.
- **How:** The processes (voting, negotiating, lobbying, protesting, or even force).

Without politics, the alternative for settling disputes is either complete isolation or violent conflict (the "might makes right" principle). Politics replaces violence with verbal, institutional, and legal processes.

## The Core Concept: Power

**Power** is the ability to get someone to do something they would not otherwise do. In politics, power is the currency. Political scientists categorize power in several ways:

### 1. Hard Power vs. Soft Power
Coined by political scientist Joseph Nye, this distinction explains how influence is exerted:

| Type of Power | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Hard Power** | The use of coercion, force, or economic incentives to get results. | Military action, economic sanctions, police enforcement, or cash rewards. |
| **Soft Power** | The ability to shape the preferences of others through appeal, attraction, and persuasion. | Cultural influence, democratic values, foreign aid, and reputable education systems. |

### 2. The Three Faces of Power
Steven Lukes proposed that power operates on three distinct levels:
1. **Decision-Making Power:** The visible ability to influence decisions (e.g., winning a vote in parliament).
2. **Agenda-Setting Power:** The ability to prevent decisions from being made by keeping topics off the table (e.g., a political party refusing to allow a bill to be debated).
3. **Ideological/Preference-Shaping Power:** The invisible ability to shape what people actually *want*, making them accept their role in the social order (e.g., using media and education to make people believe a certain inequality is natural).

## Authority and Legitimacy: Power with Consent

Power is not always brute force. In stable societies, power is transformed into **authority**. 
- **Power** is the *ability* to influence.
- **Authority** is the *recognized right* to rule.

If a robber points a gun at you, they have **power**. If a police officer issues a lawful order, they have **authority**.

### Max Weber’s Three Sources of Legitimacy
For authority to work, citizens must believe that the rulers have the right to rule. This belief is called **legitimacy**. The German sociologist Max Weber identified three ways leaders obtain legitimacy:

```
                  ┌─────────────────────────────┐
                  │    SOURCES OF LEGITIMACY    │
                  └──────────────┬──────────────┘
                                 │
         ┌───────────────────────┼───────────────────────┐
         ▼                       ▼                       ▼
┌──────────────────┐    ┌──────────────────┐    ┌──────────────────┐
│   CHARISMATIC    │    │   TRADITIONAL    │    │ LEGAL-RATIONAL   │
│ Based on devotion│    │Based on long-time│    │ Based on rules,  │
│ to a leader's    │    │customs, heredity,│    │ laws, and formal │
│ personal qualities│   │ or historic right│    │ offices          │
│ (e.g., Gandhi,   │    │(e.g., hereditary │    │(e.g., elected    │
│  Napoleon)       │    │ monarchs)        │    │ president)       │
└──────────────────┘    └──────────────────┘    └──────────────────┘
```

1. **Traditional Legitimacy:** Derived from long-established customs and social structures. People obey because "this is how things have always been done" (e.g., hereditary monarchs, tribal chiefs).
2. **Charismatic Legitimacy:** Derived from the extraordinary personal appeal, heroism, or moral authority of an individual leader (e.g., Mahatma Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr., or demagogues). This legitimacy is highly unstable because it dies with the leader.
3. **Legal-Rational Legitimacy:** Derived from a system of institutionalized, written rules and laws. Rulers are obeyed not because of *who* they are, but because of the *office* they hold. This is the foundation of modern democracies.

## The Arena of Politics: The State

Modern politics is organized around **the state**. In political science, a state is not a sub-national unit (like California or Bavaria), but a sovereign country.

Max Weber famously defined the state as an institution that claims a **"monopoly on the legitimate use of physical force within a given territory."**

### Core Components of a State
For an entity to be recognized as a state, it must possess:
1. **Territory:** Clear, recognized geographical borders.
2. **Population:** A permanent community of people.
3. **Government:** An organized system of administration and laws.
4. **Sovereignty:** The supreme, independent authority to govern itself without external interference.

> **Aha! moment:** The state is like a referee. It is the only entity we allow to legally use violence (through the police or military) to enforce rules. We accept this monopoly because it prevents the chaotic violence of individuals fighting each other.

## Key Figures in Political Thought

- **Aristotle (384–322 BCE):** Declared that "man is by nature a political animal." He was the first to systematically categorize different systems of government (democracy, oligarchy, monarchy) based on who rules and in whose interest.
- **Niccolò Machiavelli (1469–1527):** Author of *The Prince*. He separated politics from morality, arguing that politics is about securing and maintaining power through any means necessary (realpolitik).
- **Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679):** Argued that without a strong state (the Leviathan), humans live in a "state of nature" where life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." To escape this, we sign a **social contract** to submit to a ruler in exchange for safety.

---

## Key Terms

- **Politics:** The process of making collective decisions and distributing resources.
- **Power:** The ability to make others do what they would not otherwise do.
- **Soft Power:** Attracting and persuading others rather than forcing them.
- **Authority:** The recognized and accepted right to rule.
- **Legitimacy:** The belief by citizens that their government's rule is right and proper.
- **The State:** An institution with a monopoly on the legitimate use of force within a territory.
- **Social Contract:** An implicit agreement where individuals surrender some freedom in exchange for security provided by the state.
