# Governance 101

> *Government is the institution that rules. Governance is the process of ruling.*

---

## The Big Picture

For a long time, political science focused almost entirely on **governments**—the presidents, parliaments, courts, and police forces of the state. But in the modern world, decisions are not just made by governments. 

Think about how your city is run. Yes, the local council makes rules. But public transit might be run by a private company. Social services might be handled by charities. Business groups lobby for changes, and community associations shape development. 

This network of actors cooperating to solve public problems is called **governance**. Governance refers to the rules, processes, and behavior by which interests are articulated, resources are managed, and power is exercised in a society.

---

## Government vs. Governance: What's the Difference?

To understand the difference, think of a ship:
- **Government** is the physical ship itself—the crew, the engines, and the structure.
- **Governance** is the act of steering—setting the course, coordinating the crew, managing fuel, and responding to storms.

Here is a side-by-side comparison:

| Feature | Government | Governance |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | A formal institution with legal authority. | A process, set of rules, and network of cooperation. |
| **Who is involved** | Only formal state actors (politicians, civil servants, police). | State actors PLUS non-state actors (businesses, NGOs, citizens). |
| **How power is used** | Top-down command and control. | Collaboration, negotiation, and networks. |
| **Scope** | Limited to the physical borders of a country or city. | Can operate at any level, from a single company to the entire globe. |

## The Principles of "Good Governance"

In the 1990s, organizations like the United Nations and the World Bank began promoting **Good Governance** as a critical requirement for economic development and human rights. Good governance is characterized by eight core principles:

1. **Accountability:** Decision-makers (in government, business, or civil society) must be answerable to those who will be affected by their decisions.
2. **Transparency:** Information must be freely available and directly accessible to those who will be affected by decisions and their enforcement.
3. **Rule of Law:** Fair legal frameworks must be enforced impartially, particularly laws on human rights.
4. **Participation:** All citizens must have a voice in decision-making, either directly or through legitimate representatives.
5. **Responsiveness:** Institutions and processes must try to serve all stakeholders within a reasonable timeframe.
6. **Consensus Orientation:** Mediation of different interests in society to reach a broad consensus on what is in the best interest of the whole community.
7. **Equity and Inclusiveness:** All members of society, particularly the most vulnerable, must feel they have a stake in it and are not excluded.
8. **Effectiveness and Efficiency:** Processes and institutions must produce results that meet the needs of society while making the best use of resources.

## Types of Governance

Governance operates in different spheres beyond the state:

### 1. Multi-Level Governance
Decisions are no longer made at a single level of authority. Authority is dispersed vertically (between local, national, and international levels) and horizontally (between governments, markets, and civil society). For example, a European farmer is subject to local zoning laws, national taxes, and European Union environmental regulations.

### 2. Corporate Governance
The system of rules, practices, and processes by which a firm is directed and controlled. It balances the interests of a company's stakeholders—such as shareholders, senior executives, customers, suppliers, financiers, government, and the community.

### 3. Global Governance
The movement towards political cooperation among transnational actors, aimed at negotiating responses to problems that affect more than one state or region (like climate change, pandemics, or global trade). Because there is no "world government," global governance relies on treaties, international organizations (like the UN or WHO), and voluntary standards.

---

## Key Terms

- **Governance:** The process of decision-making and the process by which decisions are implemented (or not implemented).
- **Good Governance:** Governance that is accountable, transparent, responsive, equitable, and follows the rule of law.
- **Transparency:** The free flow of information, enabling citizens to monitor what decision-makers are doing.
- **Accountability:** The requirement that officials answer to the public for their actions and decisions.
- **Global Governance:** Collaborative networks of states, international organizations, and NGOs trying to solve global problems.
- **Civil Society:** Non-governmental organizations, charities, and community groups that represent the interests of citizens.
