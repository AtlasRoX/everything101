# Liberalism 101

> *Liberalism is a political and moral philosophy based on the rights of the individual, liberty, consent of the governed, and equality before the law.*

---

## The Big Picture

We live in a world built on liberal ideas. When you express an opinion online without fear of arrest, vote in an election, buy property, or request a trial by jury, you are participating in a system shaped by **liberalism**.

Historically, liberalism arose in Europe during the 17th and 18th centuries (the Enlightenment) as a challenge to the absolute power of kings and the religious authority of the church. Liberals argued that individuals are born free and equal, and that government exists to serve the individual—not the other way around.

This article introduces the core commitments of liberalism, its historical evolution, and the split between classical and modern social liberalism.

---

## The Core Commitments of Liberalism

While there are many branches of liberalism, they all share four foundational values:

1. **Individualism:** The belief that the individual is of supreme importance, possessing inherent dignity and rights (like life, liberty, and property) that the state cannot violate.
2. **Liberty (Freedom):** The right of individuals to think, speak, act, and live as they choose, provided they do not harm others (the **Harm Principle**).
3. **Consent of the Governed:** The idea that governments only have the right to rule because the citizens have agreed to it (the **Social Contract**). Rulers are delegates, not masters.
4. **Equality and Law:** Every citizen is equal before the law, regardless of birth, wealth, or status (rule of law).

---

## Classical vs. Modern Social Liberalism

Over time, liberals split over how to achieve individual freedom, leading to two major movements:

### 1. Classical Liberalism (Negative Liberty)
Developed in the 18th and 19th centuries during the Industrial Revolution. It defines freedom as the **absence of government interference** (negative liberty).
- **Core Stance:** The state should act only as a "night-watchman"—protecting people from violence and theft, but otherwise leaving them alone.
- **Economy:** Favors laissez-faire (free-market) capitalism, low taxes, and private contract freedom. They argue that free trade and competition lead to prosperity.
- **Key Thinker:** John Locke, Adam Smith.

### 2. Modern Social Liberalism (Positive Liberty)
Developed in the late 19th and 20th centuries as a response to industrial poverty. It defines freedom as the **ability to achieve one's potential** (positive liberty).
- **Core Stance:** If an individual is starving, uneducated, or sick, they are not truly "free" to exercise their rights. Therefore, the government must step in to provide opportunities.
- **Economy:** Favors a regulated economy and a welfare state (public schools, healthcare, social security) funded by progressive taxation to create a level playing field.
- **Key Thinker:** John Stuart Mill (transitional), John Rawls.

## Key Thinkers of Liberalism

- **John Locke (1632–1704):** The "Father of Liberalism." He argued that humans possess natural rights to "life, liberty, and estate." Governments are created solely to protect these rights; if a government fails to do so, citizens have a **right to revolution**.
- **John Stuart Mill (1806–1873):** Wrote *On Liberty*. He formulated the **Harm Principle**: "The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others."
- **John Rawls (1921–2002):** Wrote *A Theory of Justice*. He proposed the **Veil of Ignorance** experiment: if you had to design a fair society without knowing where you would end up in it (rich or poor, healthy or sick), you would naturally design a system that protects the basic rights and economic safety of the most vulnerable.

---

## Key Terms

- **Liberalism:** A belief system prioritizing individual rights, liberty, and equality before the law.
- **Negative Liberty:** Freedom from external interference or constraint by the state.
- **Positive Liberty:** Freedom to act upon one's free will and achieve potential, requiring resources and opportunities.
- **Harm Principle:** The belief that individuals should be free to do as they choose unless their actions cause harm to others.
- **Veil of Ignorance:** A conceptual tool used to design a fair society by ignoring one's own identity and status.
