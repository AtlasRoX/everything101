# Constitution 101

> *A constitution is the set of fundamental rules, principles, and structures that establish how a state is governed and limit the power of the government.*

---

## The Big Picture

Imagine playing a board game without a rulebook. Anyone could change the rules halfway through the game to make themselves win. It would be chaotic and unfair.

A state faces the same problem. A government has immense power—it can collect taxes, arrest people, and go to war. How do we ensure that those in power do not change the rules of society to stay in office forever? How do we protect the basic rights of citizens?

The answer is a **constitution**. A constitution is the "rulebook" for government. It is the supreme law of the land, meaning all other laws must comply with it. If a parliament passes a law that violates the constitution, the courts can strike it down as invalid (unconstitutional).

---

## The Functions of a Constitution

Constitutions serve three main purposes in a society:

1. **Creating the Structure of Government:** Defining the branches of government (Legislature, Executive, Judiciary), how they are chosen, and what their specific jobs are.
2. **Establishing Limits on Power:** Restricting what the government is allowed to do. This is known as **constitutionalism**—the principle that government power is not absolute and must be exercised in accordance with laws and rights.
3. **Protecting Rights:** Guaranteeing the fundamental freedoms of citizens, such as freedom of speech, assembly, and religion, and preventing the government from taking them away (usually listed in a **Bill of Rights**).

---

## Types of Constitutions

Political scientists categorize constitutions in a few ways:

### 1. Codified vs. Uncodified (Written vs. Unwritten)
- **Codified Constitutions:** The rules of government are written down in a single, formal document (e.g., the United States, India, France). 
- **Uncodified Constitutions:** There is no single document. Instead, the constitution is made up of a collection of historical documents, ordinary laws passed by parliament, court precedents, and long-standing customs (e.g., the United Kingdom, New Zealand, Israel).

### 2. Rigid vs. Flexible
This refers to how easy it is to change (amend) the constitution:
- **Rigid Constitutions:** Require a special, difficult process to amend, such as a two-thirds majority in parliament or a national referendum (e.g., the U.S. Constitution has only been amended 27 times in over 230 years).
- **Flexible Constitutions:** Can be amended by a simple majority vote in parliament, just like ordinary laws (e.g., the UK Constitution).

## Core Constitutional Principles

Modern constitutions are built on a few core ideas:

### 1. The Rule of Law
The principle that **no one is above the law**—not even the President, the Prime Minister, or the King. Laws must be clear, public, and applied equally to everyone, regardless of their wealth or power.

### 2. Checks and Balances
While the *separation of powers* divides the government's branches, **checks and balances** gives each branch the power to limit or block the actions of the other branches:

```
                         ┌─────────────────────┐
                         │   SUPREME COURT     │
                         │ (Judicial Branch)   │
                         └──────┬────────▲─────┘
                                │        │
         Declares laws          │        │ Appoints judges
         unconstitutional       │        │ (with Senate approval)
                                ▼        │
    ┌─────────────────────┐            ┌─┴───────────────────┐
    │     CONGRESS        │───────────>│     PRESIDENT       │
    │ (Legislative Branch)│ Overrides  │ (Executive Branch)  │
    └─────────────────────┘ veto       └─────────────────────┘
```

- **The Executive** can veto laws passed by the **Legislature** and appoints judges to the **Judiciary**.
- **The Legislature** can impeach the executive leader and must approve judicial appointments.
- **The Judiciary** can declare laws passed by the legislature or actions taken by the executive to be unconstitutional (this is called **judicial review**).

---

## Key Terms

- **Constitution:** The fundamental law that establishes the framework and limits of government.
- **Constitutionalism:** The belief that government power should be limited by a constitution to prevent tyranny.
- **Rule of Law:** The principle that everyone, including rulers, is subject to the same laws.
- **Judicial Review:** The power of courts to declare laws or executive actions null and void if they violate the constitution.
- **Codified Constitution:** A constitution written down in a single, authoritative document.
- **Amendment:** A formal change or addition to a constitution.
