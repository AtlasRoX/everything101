# Socialism 101

> *Socialism is an economic and political ideology that advocates for collective or public ownership of the means of production, distribution, and exchange, with the goal of creating a more equal society.*

---

## The Big Picture

Under capitalism, individuals own the factories, software companies, and land, and compete to make a profit. While this system drives innovation and wealth creation, it also leads to immense inequality: a small group of owners can accumulate vast wealth while the workers who produce the goods struggle to survive.

**Socialism** arose in the 19th century during the Industrial Revolution to challenge this dynamic. Socialists argue that the resources of the world should be owned and managed by society as a whole for the common good, rather than by private owners for personal profit.

This article introduces the core concepts of socialism, the historical split between different branches, and how it differs from capitalism.

---

## Core Commitments of Socialism

Socialist movements are united by a few central ideas:

1. **Equality of Outcome (Social Justice):** Socialists argue that true freedom cannot exist if people suffer from extreme poverty while others live in luxury. They seek to reduce wealth gaps through taxation, welfare, and public services.
2. **Collective Ownership:** The belief that key resources and industries—such as energy, water, healthcare, and transit (the **means of production**)—should be owned by the public or the workers, rather than private corporations.
3. **Cooperation over Competition:** The belief that human beings are naturally social and cooperative. Capitalism, socialists argue, forces people to compete against one another, fostering greed and division. Socialism aims to structure society around mutual aid and collective goals.
4. **Production for Use, Not Profit:** Economic activity should be planned to meet human needs (e.g., building affordable housing, producing medicine) rather than to generate profits for shareholders.

## Social Democracy vs. Democratic Socialism

Over the last 150 years, socialists split into two major democratic paths:

### 1. Social Democracy (Reform within Capitalism)
Modern social democrats do not want to abolish capitalism. Instead, they want to regulate it and build a strong welfare state.
- **Core Stance:** Private property and markets are allowed, but the government heavily taxes the profits to fund high-quality public services (education, healthcare, pensions).
- **Goal:** A highly regulated capitalist economy with a strong safety net.
- **Examples:** The "Nordic Model" in Sweden, Norway, Denmark, and Finland.

### 2. Democratic Socialism (Transition beyond Capitalism)
Democratic socialists believe that capitalism is fundamentally unjust and cannot be fully repaired. They want to transition to a fully socialist economy.
- **Core Stance:** They want to replace private ownership of major corporations with worker-owned cooperatives, public ownership, or municipal control, but through democratic elections rather than violent revolution.
- **Goal:** An economy where workers democratically manage the workplaces and public goods are completely de-commodified.

## Key Thinkers of Socialism

- **Robert Owen (1771–1858):** A pioneer of "Utopian Socialism." A wealthy factory owner, he built model villages for his workers with free education, shorter workdays, and cooperatively run stores, proving that treating workers well did not destroy business.
- **Karl Marx (1818–1883):** Wrote *The Communist Manifesto* and *Das Kapital*. Although his ideas led to revolutionary communism, his critique of capitalism—how it exploits workers and leads to class conflict—shaped all socialist movements. (See [Communism101](./Communism101.md) for more).
- **Eduard Bernstein (1850–1932):** A key figure in "Evolutionary Socialism." He argued that workers could achieve socialism peacefully through democratic elections and trade unions, rather than through violent revolution. This is the foundation of social democracy.

---

## Key Terms

- **Socialism:** An ideology prioritizing social equality and collective control of resources.
- **Means of Production:** The non-human facilities and resources used for producing goods (factories, land, machinery).
- **Social Democracy:** A system combining a capitalist market economy with a strong welfare state and public services.
- **Democratic Socialism:** A system aiming to replace capitalism with a socialist economy through democratic political processes.
- **Welfare State:** A system where the government protects and promotes the social and economic well-being of its citizens through services, pensions, and healthcare.
