# Conservatism 101

> *Conservatism is a political philosophy that prioritizes social order, traditional institutions, and gradual reform rather than radical societal change.*

---

## The Big Picture

When society changes quickly, is it always progress? Or do we risk breaking the fragile institutions that keep us safe and stable?

While liberalism was born out of a desire to overturn the old order, **conservatism** arose to defend the value of stability and tradition. The conservative view is not a blind opposition to change. Instead, it is a philosophy of caution—a belief that society is a complex, delicate organism, and that we must preserve the wisdom of the past while carefully repairing what is broken.

This article introduces the core principles of conservatism, its origins, and its modern variations.

---

## The Core Principles of Conservatism

Conservatism is held together by several key beliefs:

1. **Skepticism of Radical Change:** The belief that human societies are incredibly complex. Attempts to rebuild society from scratch based on abstract theories (like the French or Russian Revolutions) usually lead to unintended disasters and violence. Change must be gradual.
2. **Traditional Institutions:** A belief that long-standing institutions—such as the family, religious organizations, communities, and local associations—are the glue that holds society together. They teach morals, provide social safety, and maintain order.
3. **Organic Society:** Unlike liberals (who see society as a collection of independent individuals), conservatives see society as an **organic whole**. Different parts rely on one another, and hierarchy is natural. We have obligations to past, present, and future generations.
4. **Human Imperfection:** A belief that humans are flawed, self-interested, and driven by passion as much as reason. Therefore, we need strong laws, traditions, and institutions to guide behavior and keep order.

## The Origin: Edmund Burke and the French Revolution

The founder of modern conservatism is the British-Irish philosopher **Edmund Burke (1729–1797)**. 

During the French Revolution in 1789, many liberals celebrated the overthrow of the King. Burke, however, wrote a famous critique: *Reflections on the Revolution in France*.

> **Aha! moment:** Burke argued that society is a **"partnership not only between those who are living, but between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are to be born."** 

Burke warned that by destroying the monarchy, the church, and all social customs overnight, the French revolutionaries would destroy the social order itself, leading to chaos and, eventually, a military dictatorship (which came true with Napoleon Bonaparte). Burke argued for **reform, not revolution**—pruning the dead branches of a tree rather than tearing it up by the roots.

## Modern Variations of Conservatism

Like liberalism, conservatism has evolved and contains different branches:

### 1. Traditional/One-Nation Conservatism
Focuses heavily on social hierarchy, duty, and community stability. Believes that the wealthy have an obligation to help the poor (**noblesse oblige**) to prevent social unrest and keep the national community united.

### 2. The New Right (Fusion Crossover)
Developed in the 1970s and 1980s under leaders like Ronald Reagan (USA) and Margaret Thatcher (UK). It fused conservative social values with **classical liberal economics**:
- **Socially:** Promotes traditional family structures, patriotism, and law and order.
- **Economically:** Promotes deregulation, privatization of public services, free markets, and a smaller state (often called neoliberalism).

### 3. Neoconservatism
Focuses heavily on foreign policy, promoting democracy and national interests abroad through military and diplomatic strength (hard power).

---

## Key Terms

- **Conservatism:** A political philosophy prioritizing tradition, social order, and gradual change.
- **Organic Society:** The view that society is a living organism where individuals are interconnected and owe duties to one another.
- **Pragmatism:** An approach that evaluates ideas and policies based on practical, real-world results rather than abstract theories.
- **Noblesse Oblige:** The belief that privilege entails responsibility; the rich and powerful should care for the less fortunate.
- **The New Right:** A political movement that combines conservative social policies with free-market economics.
