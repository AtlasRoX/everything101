# Federalism 101

> *Federalism is a system of government in which sovereignty is constitutionally divided between a central national government and regional governments.*

---

## The Big Picture

Who should make the laws? A single central government in the nation's capital, or local governments closer to the communities they serve?

In a small country like Singapore, a single government can easily manage everything. But in a massive, diverse country like the United States, India, or Brazil, a single central government might struggle to make rules that work equally well for everyone. People living in a rural agricultural province have different needs than those in a massive financial metropolis.

The solution is **federalism**. Federalism is a constitutionally protected compromise: it unites different regions under a single national government for shared matters (like defense and foreign policy), while allowing regions to govern their own local affairs (like education and police).

---

## Federal vs. Unitary vs. Confederal States

Political scientists classify how power is distributed geographically into three main systems:

| System | Power Distribution | Sovereignty Holder | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Unitary** | Central government holds all primary authority. Local governments only exist if the center allows them, and their powers can be taken away. | Central Government | United Kingdom, France, Japan. |
| **Federal** | Power is shared. Both central and regional governments have constitutionally protected powers that the other cannot take away. | Both (Divided Sovereignty) | United States, Canada, India, Germany. |
| **Confederal** | A weak central body is created by a group of independent, sovereign states. The center only has powers the states voluntarily give it. | Regional States | Historical US under Articles of Confederation, early Switzerland. |

---

## How Power Is Divided in a Federation

In a federal system, the constitution explicitly outlines who controls what. Powers are usually categorized as:

- **Exclusive/Enumerated Powers (Central):** Powers belonging solely to the national government. These are issues that require a single national voice. Examples: declaring war, printing money, foreign treaties, regulating interstate trade.
- **Reserved Powers (Regional):** Powers left entirely to the states or provinces. Examples: education, local police, road rules, marriage laws.
- **Concurrent Powers (Shared):** Powers that both levels of government can exercise simultaneously. Examples: collecting taxes, building infrastructure, establishing courts.

## Why Choose Federalism? Pros and Cons

Federalism is a balancing act. It has distinct advantages and disadvantages:

### Advantages
- **Local Autonomy:** Regions can tailor laws to their cultural, economic, or geographical needs.
- **Laboratory of Democracy:** States can experiment with new policies (like universal pre-K or carbon taxes). If a policy works in one state, other states or the national government can copy it; if it fails, it only hurts a small area.
- **Closer to the People:** Citizens can interact more easily with local politicians than distant national leaders.
- **Prevents Tyranny:** Dividing power vertically makes it harder for a dictator to seize control of the entire country.

### Disadvantages
- **Inefficiency and Duplication:** Having 50 state governments plus one national government means overlapping bureaucracies and wasted resources.
- **Conflict of Laws:** Leads to confusion when state and federal laws clash. For example, in the United States, marijuana is legal under many state laws but remains illegal under federal law.
- **Inequality Between Regions:** Richer states can fund better schools and healthcare than poorer states, leading to unequal opportunities for citizens within the same country.

---

## Devolution: Unitary States Shifting Power

Unitary states sometimes experience **devolution**—where the central government voluntarily transfers power down to regional assemblies. 

For example, in 1997, the United Kingdom devolved legislative power to a Scottish Parliament, a Welsh Assembly, and a Northern Ireland Assembly. However, because the UK remains a unitary state, the central Parliament in London legally retains ultimate sovereignty and could theoretically revoke these devolved powers. In a true federation, the center cannot unilaterally abolish regional governments.

---

## Key Terms

- **Federalism:** A system where sovereignty is constitutionally shared between central and regional levels.
- **Unitary State:** A state where ultimate political authority is concentrated in a single central government.
- **Devolution:** The transfer of powers from a central government to regional governments in a unitary state.
- **Sovereignty:** The supreme and independent power of a territory to govern itself.
- **Concurrent Powers:** Powers shared by both federal and regional governments under the constitution.
