# Presidency 101

> *The presidency is the executive office held by a head of state or head of government in a republic.*

---

## The Big Picture

The executive branch is responsible for *doing* things—commanding the military, negotiating treaties, police enforcement, and running the public administration. In a presidential republic, the person at the top of this branch is the **President**.

Unlike a prime minister (who is chosen by parliament), a president in a presidential system is elected directly by the people and holds power independently of the legislature. This gives the president a unique mandate, but also creates the risk of **democratic gridlock**—when a president from one political party faces a congress controlled by a rival party, bringing lawmaking to a halt.

This article covers the roles, powers, and limits of the presidential office, and the difference between presidential and semi-presidential systems.

---

## Head of State vs. Head of Government

In political science, the executive role is divided into two distinct functions. A president's power depends on whether they hold one or both of these roles:

| Role | Purpose | Activities |
|---|---|---|
| **Head of State** | The ceremonial representative of the nation's unity, history, and sovereignty. Above daily partisan politics. | Greeting foreign dignitaries, hosting state dinners, awarding medals, and representing the country at international events. |
| **Head of Government** | The active political leader who runs the state's administration, sets policy agendas, and commands the executive departments. | Appointing cabinets, submitting budgets, directing the military, and negotiating laws with the legislature. |

- **In the United States:** The President holds **both** roles. They are both the political leader and the ceremonial figurehead of the country.
- **In Germany or India:** The President is a ceremonial **Head of State** with minimal political power, while the Chancellor or Prime Minister is the actual **Head of Government**.
- **In the United Kingdom:** The King is the ceremonial Head of State, while the Prime Minister is the Head of Government.

## The Powers of a President

Constitutions grant presidents specific powers to run the executive branch:

### 1. Executive Orders / Decrees
The power to issue directives to government agencies that carry the force of law without needing legislative approval. However, these are limited—they can only direct how existing laws are implemented, and the courts can strike them down if they exceed constitutional authority.

### 2. Commander-in-Chief
Ultimate authority over the country's military forces. This ensures civilian control over the military, preventing military generals from seizing power.

### 3. Veto Power
The power to reject bills passed by the legislature. To override a presidential veto, the legislature usually needs a supermajority (e.g., two-thirds in both houses of Congress), giving the president huge leverage in lawmaking.

### 4. Appointment Power
The right to choose cabinet secretaries, ambassadors, federal judges, and military generals (usually subject to legislative approval).

## Presidential vs. Semi-Presidential Systems

Not all presidential offices are structured the same way:

### 1. Pure Presidential System
The President is both Head of State and Head of Government. There is a strict separation of powers. The President is elected independently of the legislature for a fixed term and cannot be removed except by impeachment (e.g., USA, Brazil, Nigeria).

### 2. Semi-Presidential System (Dual Executive)
The executive power is split between an elected **President** (usually focusing on foreign policy and defense) and a **Prime Minister** (usually running domestic policy and chosen by parliament).
- **Used in:** France, Ukraine, Egypt.
- **Cohabitation:** If the President and the parliamentary majority belong to different political parties, they must share power. The President must appoint a Prime Minister from the rival party, leading to a system of "cohabitation" where they govern together.

## Key Terms

- **President:** The executive leader of a republic.
- **Head of State:** The symbolic representative of a country.
- **Head of Government:** The political leader who runs the state administration.
- **Executive Order:** A directive issued by a president to the bureaucracy without legislative vote.
- **Cohabitation:** A situation in semi-presidential systems where the President and Prime Minister are from opposing political parties.
- **Impeachment:** The formal legislative process of accusing and putting a president on trial for high crimes.
