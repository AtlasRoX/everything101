# Parliament 101

> *A parliament is a legislative body of government, typical in representative democracies, where the executive branch is drawn from and held accountable to the legislature.*

---

## The Big Picture

In a presidential system like the United States, the President and Congress are elected separately and often clash. In a **parliamentary system**, the relationship is completely different: the executive and legislative branches are merged.

In a parliamentary democracy, citizens do not vote for the leader of the country (the Prime Minister). Instead, they vote only for their local Member of Parliament (MP). Whichever political party wins a majority of seats in the parliament then forms the government, and its leader becomes the Prime Minister. 

This creates a system of **fusion of powers** rather than separation of powers. It makes passing laws much faster, but it also means that the Prime Minister can be removed from office at any time if their parliament turns against them.

---

## Unicameral vs. Bicameral Legislatures

Parliaments around the world are structured with either one or two chambers:

### 1. Unicameral (One Chamber)
A single assembly of representatives.
- **Used in:** Small or highly unified countries (e.g., New Zealand, Sweden, Singapore, Denmark).
- **Advantage:** Faster lawmaking, clear accountability, and cheaper bureaucracy.

### 2. Bicameral (Two Chambers)
Divided into two separate assemblies:
- **Lower House:** Represents the general population based on local districts (e.g., the House of Commons in the UK, the House of Representatives in the US, the Bundestag in Germany). Holds the primary legislative power and selects the Prime Minister.
- **Upper House:** Often represents regional interests, has historical origins, or acts as a chamber of revision (e.g., the Senate in the US, the House of Lords in the UK, the Bundesrat in Germany). Usually holds less power than the lower house in parliamentary systems.

## How a Parliamentary Government Is Formed

Because parliaments often use *Proportional Representation* (PR) elections, a single party rarely wins an outright majority. To govern, parties must negotiate to form a government:

```
                          │    PARLIAMENT SEATS    │
                          │   (Total: 100 Seats)   │
                          └───────────┬────────────┘
                                      │
         ┌────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                            ▼                            ▼
┌──────────────────┐         ┌──────────────────┐         ┌──────────────────┐
│  RED PARTY (40)  │         │ YELLOW PARTY (15)│         │ BLUE PARTY (45)  │
│ Want to govern;  │         │  The "Kingmaker" │         │ Opposition;      │
│ Needs coalition  │         │  party           │         │ Outnumbered      │
└────────┬─────────┘         └────────┬─────────┘         └──────────────────┘
         │                            │
         └─────────────┬──────────────┘
                       ▼
         ┌──────────────────────────┐
         │     COALITION (55)       │
         │ Forms Government (Red PM)│
         └──────────────────────────┘
```

- **Majority Government:** A single political party wins more than 50% of the seats and can govern alone.
- **Coalition Government:** Two or more parties sign an agreement to govern together, sharing cabinet posts and compromising on policies to exceed the 50% threshold.
- **Minority Government:** A party governs without a majority, relying on support from independent MPs or small parties on a vote-by-vote basis. This is highly unstable.

## Controlling the Executive: Votes of No Confidence

In a parliamentary system, the Prime Minister and their cabinet must maintain the **"confidence of the House."**

If parliament is unhappy with the Prime Minister's performance or if a key government bill (like the budget) fails to pass, the opposition can call for a **vote of no confidence**.
- If a majority of MPs vote against the government, the Prime Minister **must resign** or call for an immediate general election.
- This creates strong discipline: Prime Ministers must keep their party members happy, otherwise they can be ousted in a matter of days. This is why parliamentary executives rarely experience the prolonged legislative gridlock common in presidential systems.

---

## Key Terms

- **Parliament:** The supreme legislative body in a parliamentary system.
- **Prime Minister:** The head of government in a parliamentary democracy, drawn from the legislature.
- **Bicameral:** A legislature with two chambers (Lower and Upper Houses).
- **Unicameral:** A legislature with a single chamber.
- **Vote of No Confidence:** A parliamentary vote showing that the majority no longer supports the Prime Minister and their government.
- **Coalition:** A temporary alliance of political parties to form a majority government.
- **Backbencher:** A Member of Parliament who is not a cabinet minister or opposition spokesperson (they sit on the benches behind their party leaders).
