# Elections 101

> *Elections are the formal mechanism by which democratic societies translate individual citizen votes into seats in legislature and offices in government.*

---

## The Big Picture

In a representative democracy, the citizens do not run the country directly. Instead, they delegate their power to politicians. **Elections** are the hiring process for these representatives.

However, elections are not as simple as "whoever gets the most votes wins." The rules used to count votes and allocate seats—known as the **electoral system**—can completely change who ends up in power. A political party can win the most votes nationally but still lose the election, or win a massive majority in parliament with only 35% of the vote.

This article introduces the two main electoral systems used worldwide, the role of political parties, and what drives voters to choose one candidate over another.

---

## Electoral Systems: How Votes Become Seats

Electoral systems shape the political landscape of a country. The two most common types are:

### 1. First-Past-the-Post (FPTP) / Majoritarian
The country is divided into geographical districts. Each district elects a single representative. The candidate who gets the most votes in that district wins the seat—even if they do not get a majority of the votes (e.g., they win with 35% of the vote because three other candidates split the rest).
- **Used in:** United States, United Kingdom, Canada, India.
- **Result:** Often leads to a **two-party system**. Because voters don't want to waste their votes on small parties that have no chance of winning a district, they coalesce around the two largest parties (Duverger's Law).
- **Pros & Cons:** Easy to understand, creates stable majority governments, but shuts out third parties and can lead to a high number of "wasted" votes.

### 2. Proportional Representation (PR)
Seats in the legislature are allocated to political parties based on the percentage of the vote they win nationally or in large multi-member districts. If a party wins 20% of the vote, they get roughly 20% of the seats in parliament.
- **Used in:** Germany, New Zealand, Brazil, South Africa.
- **Result:** Leads to a **multi-party system**. Small parties can easily win seats because they do not need to win an entire local district; they just need to cross a national threshold (e.g., 5% of the vote).
- **Pros & Cons:** Highly representative of public opinion, encourages compromise, but can lead to unstable **coalition governments** where no single party holds a majority and small fringe parties can hold significant leverage.

## The Role of Political Parties

A **political party** is an organized group of people who share similar political views and goals, who work together to win elections, run the government, and shape public policy.

Parties serve several functions in a democracy:
- **Structuring the Vote:** Simplifying choices for voters. Instead of researching hundreds of individual candidates, voters look at the party label (e.g., Democrat, Republican, Labour, Conservative) to understand a candidate's general stance.
- **Recruiting Candidates:** Finding and vetting people to run for office.
- **Formulating Policy:** Organizing coherent platforms and agendas for governing.
- **Mobilizing Voters:** Registering voters, running campaigns, and encouraging turnout.

## What Drives Voter Behavior?

Political scientists study why voters vote the way they do. Three main factors explain voter choice:

1. **Party Identification:** A long-term psychological attachment to a specific party, often passed down from parents or shaped by social class, religion, or geography. This acts as a filter through which voters view all political news.
2. **Issue Voting:** Voting based on specific issues. A voter compares the parties' platforms on issues they care about (like taxes, healthcare, or climate change) and chooses the one closest to their view.
3. **Retrospective Voting:** Voting based on the government's past performance. If the economy is doing well, voters reward the ruling party. If the economy is in a recession, they punish them by voting for the opposition.

## Key Terms

- **Electoral System:** The rules that determine how votes are cast, counted, and translated into legislative seats or offices.
- **First-Past-the-Post (FPTP):** An electoral system where the candidate with the most votes in a district wins, regardless of whether they have a majority.
- **Proportional Representation (PR):** An electoral system where parties win seats in proportion to their share of the total vote.
- **Coalition Government:** A government formed by two or more political parties cooperating because no single party has a majority.
- **Duverger's Law:** The political science principle that majoritarian (FPTP) electoral systems tend to produce two-party systems.
- **Incumbent:** The current holder of a political office running for re-election.
