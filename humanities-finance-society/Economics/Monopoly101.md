# Monopoly 101

> *A monopoly is a market structure dominated by a single seller of a product or service with no close substitutes.*

---

## The Big Picture

In a competitive market, if a seller raises their prices, you can walk across the street and buy from a rival. But what if there is no rival? What if a single company controls the city's entire water supply, the only bridge out of town, or a life-saving patented drug?

This is a **monopoly**. Without the pressure of competition, a monopolist has immense power: they become a **price maker**. They can restrict the supply of a good to force prices up and maximize their own profits, leaving consumers with high prices, poor quality, and no choices.

This article introduces the barriers that create monopolies, why they are inefficient, and how governments regulate them.

---

## Barriers to Entry: How Monopolies Are Protected

A monopoly cannot survive in the long run unless other businesses are blocked from entering the market to compete. These blockades are called **barriers to entry**:

1. **Control of a Scarce Resource:** If one company owns all the diamond mines in the world, no one else can sell diamonds (e.g., De Beers historically).
2. **Government Regulation (Patents and Copyrights):** To encourage innovation, governments grant inventors a legal monopoly to sell a new drug or technology for a limited time (e.g., a pharmaceutical patent).
3. **Network Externalities:** A product becomes more valuable as more people use it. If all your friends are on one social media app, starting a rival app is very difficult (e.g., Facebook, Microsoft Windows).
4. **High Startup Costs (Natural Monopoly):** In some industries, building the physical infrastructure is incredibly expensive. It makes no sense to have two competing companies lay duplicate water pipes or electricity lines to every house. A single firm can serve the entire market at a lower cost than two competing firms. This is a **natural monopoly**.

---

## The Cost of Monopoly: Deadweight Loss

Why do economists dislike monopolies? Compared to a competitive market, a monopoly:
1. Charges a **higher price**.
2. Produces a **smaller quantity** of goods.
3. Creates a **deadweight loss** (shrinking both consumer and producer benefits, benefiting no one).

```
Price ($)
  ▲                 Supply/Marginal Cost (MC)
  │                /
Pm┼──────────* M  /
  │          │   /
Pc┼──────────┼──* E (Competitive Equilibrium Point)
  │          │ / \
  │          │/   \
  │          │     \ Demand (D)
  └──────────*──────*───► Quantity (Q)
  0         Qm     Qc
```

- **In Competition:** The market operates at Point E, charging price $Pc$ and selling quantity $Qc$.
- **In Monopoly:** The firm restricts production to $Qm$ to charge the higher price $Pm$ (Point M). The shaded area between $Qm$ and $Qc$ represents transactions that would have benefited society but did not happen—this is **deadweight loss**.

## Oligopolies and Cartels

Between perfect competition and monopoly lies the **oligopoly**—a market dominated by a **few large firms** (e.g., mobile networks, aerospace manufacturing).

Oligopolies face a unique temptation: instead of competing, they can secretly cooperate to set prices and act like a monopoly. This is called **collusion**.
- **Cartel:** A formal agreement among oligopolists to fix prices and restrict production (e.g., OPEC regulating global oil supply).
- **The Prisoner's Dilemma:** Cartels are notoriously unstable. Each member has an incentive to secretly cheat by producing slightly more than their quota to make extra profit, causing the cartel to break down over time.

---

## How Governments Fight Monopolies: Antitrust Laws

To protect consumers and maintain market efficiency, governments use two main tools:

- **Antitrust Laws (Competition Policy):** Laws designed to block monopolistic mergers, ban price-fixing cartels, and break up dominant companies that abuse their power (e.g., the U.S. government breaking up Standard Oil or AT&T).
- **Public Regulation:** For natural monopolies (like water or electricity utilities), the government allows the monopoly to exist but sets caps on the prices they are allowed to charge.

---

## Key Terms

- **Monopoly:** A market structure with a single seller and no close substitutes.
- **Natural Monopoly:** An industry where a single firm can supply the market at a lower cost than multiple competing firms.
- **Barriers to Entry:** Obstacles preventing new competitors from entering an industry.
- **Oligopoly:** A market dominated by a small number of large sellers.
- **Collusion:** Secret cooperation among firms to fix prices or restrict supply.
- **Antitrust Laws:** Government regulations designed to prevent monopolies and encourage competition.
