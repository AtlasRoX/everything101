# Labor 101

> *Labor is the human physical and mental effort used to produce goods and services. It is the human engine of the economy.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why does a software engineer make $150,000 a year, while a cashier makes $30,000? Is it because the software engineer works five times harder? 

No. Wages are not decided by moral worth or effort. Wages are **prices**. Just like the price of apples, the price of work (the wage) is determined in a market—the **labor market**.

In the labor market, the roles are reversed from typical shopping:
- **Households** (you and me) are the **sellers** (supplying labor).
- **Firms** (businesses) are the **buyers** (demanding labor).

This article introduces how wages are determined, the concept of human capital, and how unions and laws affect the labor market.

---

## How Wages Are Determined: Derived Demand

Firms do not hire workers because they enjoy paying salaries. They hire workers because those workers help produce goods that the firm can sell for profit. Therefore, the demand for labor is a **derived demand**—derived from the demand for the firm's output.

A firm will hire a worker based on two things:
1. **Marginal Product of Labor (MPL):** The additional output produced by hiring one more worker.
2. **Value of the Marginal Product of Labor (VMPL):** The additional revenue the worker generates (Marginal Product × Price of the product).

To maximize profit, a business will hire workers up to the point where the value of the last worker's output equals the market wage (**VMPL = Wage**).

```
Wage ($)
  ▲                 
  │    \           / Supply of Labor (Workers looking for jobs)
  │     \         / 
$20┼───────* E ──/───── (Equilibrium Wage)
  │      / \     /
  │     /   \   /
  │    /     \ /     Demand for Labor (Firms hiring workers; equals VMPL)
  └───────────*────────► Labor (Number of workers)
  0          100
```

> **Aha! moment:** Why is a software engineer's wage so high? First, their VMPL is extremely high (a single engineer can write code used by millions of customers, generating millions in revenue). Second, the supply of qualified software engineers is relatively scarce. High demand + low supply = high wage.

## Human Capital: Investing in Yourself

Why do people spend thousands of dollars and years of their lives going to university or learning a trade? They are investing in their **human capital**.

> **Human Capital** is the accumulation of education, training, skills, health, and experience that makes a worker productive.

The more human capital you possess:
- The more productive you are (raising your VMPL).
- The scarcer your skills are in the market (reducing the labor supply in your niche).
- The higher the wage you can command.

## Labor Market Interventions

Labor markets are rarely left entirely to free supply and demand. Two major interventions shape outcomes:

### 1. Minimum Wage Laws (Price Floors)
A legally mandated minimum wage a firm must pay its workers. (See [Price101](./Price101.md)).
- **Goal:** Protect workers from poverty and exploitation.
- **The Trade-off:** If the minimum wage is set *above* the market equilibrium wage, it makes hiring low-skilled workers more expensive. While the employed workers benefit from higher pay, some businesses may cut hours or hire fewer workers, potentially leading to **unemployment** among young or low-skilled workers.

### 2. Labor Unions
An organized association of workers formed to protect and further their rights and interests through **collective bargaining**.
- Unions negotiate as a single bloc for higher wages, better benefits, and safer working conditions. By threatening a strike (withholding the labor supply), unions can raise wages above the competitive equilibrium.
- **The Trade-off:** Higher union wages can act like a price floor, causing firms to hire fewer workers overall. It can create an "insider-outsider" dynamic: union "insiders" get high wages, but non-union "outsiders" struggle to find jobs in that sector.

## Key Terms

- **Labor:** The physical and mental effort of human beings in production.
- **Labor Market:** The market where workers supply labor and firms demand it.
- **Derived Demand:** Demand for an input (like labor) that is derived from the demand for the final output.
- **Human Capital:** The skills, education, and health that make workers productive.
- **Minimum Wage:** A legal price floor on wages.
- **Collective Bargaining:** Negotiations between a union representing workers and an employer to establish wages and working conditions.
