# Economics 101

> *Economics is the study of scarcity, choice, and how people, businesses, and governments allocate their limited resources to satisfy unlimited wants.*

---

## The Big Picture

We live in a world where we can't have everything we want. There are only 24 hours in a day. You have a limited amount of money in your bank account. The planet has a finite amount of oil, clean water, and arable land. 

Yet, our desires are virtually infinite. We want better healthcare, faster transit, nicer homes, and more free time. 

This mismatch is the core problem of human existence: **scarcity**. Because resources are scarce, we must make choices. **Economics** is the study of those choices—how they are made, how they interact, and how we can organize society to get the most value out of what we have.

---

## The Core Concept: Opportunity Cost

Every choice has a cost. But in economics, the cost of something is not just the money you pay for it; it is what you had to give up to get it. This is called **opportunity cost**.

> **Opportunity Cost** is the value of the next best alternative that you must give up when you make a choice.

### Examples of Opportunity Cost:
- **Time:** If you spend two hours studying economics, the opportunity cost is the two hours of sleep, work, or leisure you could have had instead.
- **Money:** If you spend $1,000 on a vacation, the opportunity cost is the investment returns or the new computer you could have bought with that same $1,000.
- **Government:** If a government spends $1 billion on a new highway, the opportunity cost is the school building or hospital equipment that money could have funded.

## The Economic Metaphor: Micro vs. Macro

Economics is split into two major branches, looking at the economy from different scales:


### 1. Microeconomics
The study of individual decision-making units (households and firms) and how they interact in specific markets.
- **Example questions:** Why did the price of coffee rise this week? How does a minimum wage affect teenage employment? How does a company decide how many workers to hire?

### 2. Macroeconomics
The study of the economy as a whole, looking at aggregate variables rather than individual choices.
- **Example questions:** What causes inflation? Why do recessions happen, and how do we stop them? How can a country raise its long-term living standards?

## Three Key Assumptions of Economics

To build models of how the world works, economists traditionally rely on three core principles:

1. **Scarcity:** Resources are finite, making choices and trade-offs unavoidable.
2. **Incentives Matter:** People respond to incentives (rewards or punishments). If the price of gasoline rises, people will drive less. If the government taxes carbon emissions, businesses will find cleaner ways to produce goods.
3. **The Rational Actor Model (*Homo Economicus*):** The assumption that individuals make decisions by systematically comparing the marginal costs and benefits of an action, choosing the option that maximizes their own satisfaction (utility). *Note: Modern behavioral economics shows humans are not perfectly rational, but this model remains a highly useful baseline.*

---

## Key Terms

- **Economics:** The study of how society manages its scarce resources.
- **Scarcity:** The condition where unlimited wants exceed limited resources.
- **Opportunity Cost:** The value of the next best alternative foregone when a choice is made.
- **Microeconomics:** The study of individual decision-making in specific markets.
- **Macroeconomics:** The study of economy-wide phenomena (inflation, growth, employment).
- **Incentive:** A reward or punishment that motivates a person to act in a certain way.
