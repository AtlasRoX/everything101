# Demand 101

> *Demand is the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why do stores put items on sale? Why do airlines charge more for tickets during the summer holidays?

The answer lies in **demand**—the buyer side of the market. Demand is not just wanting something. You might *want* a private jet, but if you cannot afford it, you do not have economic demand for it. Demand requires both the **desire** to buy and the **ability to pay**.

Understanding demand helps businesses set prices, helps governments design taxes, and explains how consumer behavior shapes the economy.

---

## The Law of Demand

The most fundamental rule of consumer behavior is the **Law of Demand**:

> **The Law of Demand:** Other things being equal, as the price of a good **falls**, the quantity demanded **rises**. As the price **rises**, the quantity demanded **falls**.

This happens for two reasons:
1. **The Substitution Effect:** If the price of coffee rises, coffee becomes more expensive relative to tea. Some coffee drinkers will substitute coffee with tea.
2. **The Income Effect:** If the price of coffee rises, the money in your wallet has less purchasing power. You feel slightly poorer, so you cut back on your coffee consumption.

## The Demand Curve: Visualizing Consumer Choice

Economists graph demand using a **Demand Curve**, which always slopes **downward** (representing the inverse relationship between price and quantity):

```
Price ($)
  ▲
$5┼───────* A (At $5, consumers buy 10 units)
  │        \
$3┼─────────* B (At $3, price is lower, quantity rises to 20)
  │          \
  │           \  Demand Curve (D)
  └────────────*───► Quantity (Q)
  0        10   20
```

- **Movement Along the Curve:** Occurs **only** when the price of the good itself changes. Moving from Point A to Point B is called a "change in quantity demanded."

---

## Shifting the Demand Curve: Outer Influences

What happens if something other than price changes? For example, what if a new scientific study reveals that coffee prevents aging? 

Consumers will buy more coffee at *every* price. This is a **shift in demand** (the entire curve moves to the right):

```
Price ($)
  ▲         Shift Right (D1 -> D2)
  │          (Increase in Demand)
  │             D1      D2
  │              \       \
  │               \       \
  └────────────────*───────*───► Quantity (Q)
  0
```

The primary factors that shift the demand curve (known as **determinants of demand**) are:

- **Income:** 
  - **Normal Goods:** As income rises, demand increases (e.g., dining out, organic food).
  - **Inferior Goods:** As income rises, demand decreases (e.g., instant noodles, public transit—as people can afford cars).
- **Prices of Related Goods:**
  - **Substitutes:** If the price of Coca-Cola rises, the demand for Pepsi increases (as Pepsi becomes relatively cheaper).
  - **Complements (goods used together):** If the price of gasoline rises, the demand for large SUVs decreases.
- **Tastes and Preferences:** Successful advertising campaigns or health trends increase demand.
- **Expectations:** If you expect the price of housing to rise next month, your demand for housing will increase today.

## Price Elasticity of Demand: How Sensitive Are Buyers?

How much does quantity demanded drop when price rises? The answer depends on **price elasticity of demand**—a measure of consumer price sensitivity.

- **Inelastic Demand (Ineffective response to price):** Consumers are not sensitive to price changes. A big price rise causes only a tiny drop in quantity demanded. This happens for necessities with no close substitutes (e.g., insulin, gasoline, salt).
- **Elastic Demand (Highly sensitive response to price):** Consumers are highly sensitive. A small price rise causes a massive drop in quantity demanded. This happens for luxury goods or items with many substitutes (e.g., a specific brand of cereal, cruises).

```
INELASTIC DEMAND (Steep Curve)           ELASTIC DEMAND (Flat Curve)
Price                                    Price
  ▲                                        ▲
  │   \                                    │
  │    \                                   │   \________
  └─────*────────► Quantity                └─────*───────► Quantity
```

> **Aha! moment:** If you are a business owner selling a product with **inelastic** demand, raising your price will increase your total revenue. If you sell a product with **elastic** demand, raising your price will decrease your total revenue because you will lose too many customers.

---

## Key Terms

- **Demand:** The willingness and ability of buyers to purchase a good at various prices.
- **Law of Demand:** The rule that price and quantity demanded move in opposite directions.
- **Substitute Goods:** Goods that can be used in place of one another.
- **Complement Goods:** Goods that are consumed together.
- **Price Elasticity of Demand:** A measure of how much quantity demanded responds to a change in price.
