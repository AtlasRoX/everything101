# Competition 101

> *Competition is the effort of two or more parties acting independently to secure the business of a third party by offering the most favorable terms.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why does a company spend millions of dollars to design a slightly faster smartphone? Why do restaurants offer coupons, free Wi-Fi, and discounts?

They do it because of **competition**. In a market economy, businesses cannot force you to buy their products. They must *convince* you. 

Competition is the great driver of economic efficiency. It forces businesses to lower their prices, improve their quality, and innovate. Without competition, businesses become lazy and expensive, which is why economists place high value on competitive markets.

This article introduces the different levels of competition in a market and the concepts of consumer and producer surplus.

---

## Four Market Structures of Competition

Not all competitive markets are identical. Economists group markets into four main structures, ranging from high competition to none:

| Market Structure | Number of Sellers | Identical or Unique Product | Influence Over Price | Barriers to Entry (Ease of starting a new firm) | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Perfect Competition** | Thousands | Identical (Commodities) | None (Price Taker) | None (Very Easy) | Wheat farming, foreign exchange markets. |
| **Monopolistic Competition** | Many | Differentiated (Branded) | Some | Low | Restaurants, hair salons, clothing brands. |
| **Oligopoly** | Few (2-5) | Identical or Differentiated | High | High | Airlines, mobile carriers, aircraft manufacturing. |
| **Monopoly** | One | Completely Unique | Absolute (Price Maker) | Very High (Blocked) | Local water utility, patented pharmaceuticals. |

---

## Perfect Competition: The Theoretical Ideal

In a **perfectly competitive** market:
- **Price Takers:** Because all goods are identical (e.g., one farmer's wheat is exactly the same as another's), no single seller can charge a penny more than the market price. If they raise their price, customers will buy from their competitors instead.
- **Normal Profit:** In the long run, firms in perfect competition earn zero economic profit. If a farming sector becomes highly profitable, new farmers will enter the market, increasing supply and pushing prices back down to the cost of production.

---

## Monopolistic Competition: The Real World

Most businesses you interact with daily operate in **monopolistic competition**.
- **Product Differentiation:** While there are many restaurants, they are not selling identical products. A burger from McDonald's is different from a burger at a local gourmet diner.
- **Advertising:** Businesses use branding, packaging, and ads to make you believe their product is unique, allowing them to charge a higher price without losing all their customers.

## Welfare Economics: Consumer and Producer Surplus

Competitive markets are efficient because they maximize **total economic surplus** (the total benefit to society). This surplus is split into two parts:

```
Price ($)
  ▲                 Supply (S)
  │    \           /
  │     \  Consumer/
  │      \ Surplus/
$3┼───────* E ────/──── (Equilibrium Price)
  │      / \     /
  │     /   \   /
  │    /Producer\
  │   /  Surplus \     Demand (D)
  └───────────*────────► Quantity (Q)
  0          100
```

- **Consumer Surplus:** The difference between what a buyer is willing to pay and what they actually pay. If you were willing to pay $10 for a book, but the market price is $3, you receive a consumer surplus of $7.
- **Producer Surplus:** The difference between the price a seller receives and their cost of production. If a publisher can print a book for $1, and sells it for $3, they receive a producer surplus of $2.
- **Deadweight Loss:** If government intervention (like taxes or price ceilings) reduces the quantity bought and sold below the equilibrium point, it shrinks both surpluses. The lost value that benefit no one is called **deadweight loss**.

---

## Key Terms

- **Competition:** The struggle between businesses for customer sales.
- **Price Taker:** A firm that has no power to influence the market price and must accept it as given.
- **Product Differentiation:** Distinguishing a product from competitors through branding, design, or quality.
- **Consumer Surplus:** The benefit consumers receive by buying a good for less than their maximum willingness to pay.
- **Producer Surplus:** The benefit producers receive by selling a good for more than their production cost.
- **Deadweight Loss:** The loss of economic efficiency when the equilibrium outcome is not achieved.
