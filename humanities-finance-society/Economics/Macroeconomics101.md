# Macroeconomics 101

> *Macroeconomics is the study of the behavior, performance, structure, and decision-making of an economy as a whole.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why do some years bring plenty of jobs and rising wages, while other years bring layoffs and bankruptcies? Why is money in some countries worth less every day, while in other countries prices remain flat?

While microeconomics studies individual markets, **macroeconomics** steps back to look at the entire landscape. It studies the aggregate forces that affect everyone: economic growth, inflation, unemployment, and recessions.

To manage these aggregate forces, governments and central banks use two primary sets of levers—**fiscal policy** and **monetary policy**—to steer the economic ship through rough waters.

---

## The Business Cycle: The Economy's Pulse

In the long run, economies tend to grow as technology improves and capital accumulates. But in the short run, this growth is not a smooth, straight line. Instead, it moves in waves known as the **business cycle**:

```
Real GDP ($)
  ▲                     Peak
  │                      *          Peak
  │                     / \          *
  │    Expansion       /   \        / \
  │       ┌───────────┘     \      /   \
  │      /                   \    /     \   Long-Term Trend Line
  │     /                     \  /       \ ───────►
  │    /                       *          \
  │   /                    Recession       \
  └─────────────────────────────────────────*───► Time
  0                                      Trough
```

- **Expansion:** A period of economic growth. GDP rises, firms hire more workers, unemployment falls, and consumer spending increases.
- **Peak:** The highest point of economic activity before a downturn.
- **Recession:** A period of economic decline. GDP falls, sales contract, unemployment rises, and businesses cut investments. (See [Recession101](./Recession101.md)).
- **Trough:** The lowest point of economic decline before the recovery begins.

## How the Economy Is Controlled: The Two Levers

When the business cycle drops into a deep trough, or if the expansion is so fast that it causes high inflation, policymakers intervene using two distinct policy systems:

### 1. Fiscal Policy (Run by the Government)
The use of **government spending** and **taxation** to influence the economy.
- **Expansionary Fiscal Policy (To fight recessions):** The government increases spending (e.g., building roads, cutting taxes) to put more money into citizens' pockets, encouraging them to spend and boost demand.
- **Contractionary Fiscal Policy (To fight inflation):** The government cuts spending or raises taxes to pull money out of the economy, cooling down aggregate demand.

### 2. Monetary Policy (Run by the Central Bank)
Managing the **money supply** and **interest rates** to keep prices stable and encourage employment. This is done by an independent institution called the **Central Bank** (e.g., the Federal Reserve in the US, or the Bank of England in the UK).
- **Expansionary Monetary Policy (Easy Money):** The central bank lowers interest rates. This makes borrowing money cheaper for citizens and businesses, encouraging them to take out loans to buy houses, expand factories, and hire workers.
- **Contractionary Monetary Policy (Tight Money):** The central bank raises interest rates. This makes borrowing expensive and saving more attractive, cooling down spending and bringing inflation down.

---

## Key Terms

- **Macroeconomics:** The study of national economies and aggregate economic forces.
- **Business Cycle:** The natural ups and downs of economic growth over time.
- **Fiscal Policy:** Government decisions on taxes and public spending.
- **Monetary Policy:** Central bank decisions on interest rates and the money supply.
- **Central Bank:** The independent institution that manages a country's currency and monetary system.
- **Aggregate Demand (AD):** The total demand for all finished goods and services in an economy.
- **Aggregate Supply (AS):** The total supply of all goods and services produced in an economy.
