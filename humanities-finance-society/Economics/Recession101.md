# Recession 101

> *A recession is a significant decline in economic activity spread across the economy, lasting more than a few months, normally visible in real GDP, real income, employment, industrial production, and wholesale-retail sales.*

---

## The Big Picture

During a boom, the economy behaves like a well-oiled machine: consumers spend, businesses invest, and workers find jobs easily. But occasionally, this machine grinds to a halt. Sales dry up, warehouses fill with unsold stock, and businesses begin laying off workers. 

This is a **recession**. Recessions are painful periods in the life of a nation. They cause job losses, bankruptcy, and anxiety. 

However, recessions are also a natural part of the business cycle. Understanding what triggers them, how they differ from depressions, and how governments try to pull economies out of them, is one of the most important tasks of modern policymaking.

---

## What Is a Recession? Defining the Downturn

While newspapers often define a recession as **two consecutive quarters of decline in a country's Real GDP**, economists look at a wider set of indicators:

- **GDP:** A general drop in the production of goods.
- **Employment:** A rise in layoffs and cyclical unemployment. (See [Unemployment101](./Unemployment101.md)).
- **Income:** A decline in wages and household purchasing power.
- **Retail Sales:** A drop in consumer spending.

A recession begins at the **peak** of a business cycle (when growth stops) and ends when the economy hits the **trough** (the lowest point of the contraction) and starts growing again.

---

## What Triggers a Recession?

Recessions can be triggered by several economic shocks:

1. **Financial Crises:** Asset bubbles (like housing or stock markets) burst, destroying wealth and causing banks to stop lending (e.g., the 2008 Global Financial Crisis).
2. **Supply Shocks:** A sudden price spike in a vital resource (like oil) raises costs for all businesses, forcing them to cut production and lay off workers. (See [Inflation101](./Inflation101.md)).
3. **Monetary Tightening:** If a central bank raises interest rates too quickly to fight inflation, it can make borrowing so expensive that consumer spending and business investment collapse, accidentally launching a recession.
4. **Sudden Demand Drops:** A shock that causes consumers to panic and freeze spending (e.g., the outbreak of a pandemic).

---

## Recession vs. Depression: What's the Difference?

There is no formal mathematical boundary, but economists use a common rule of thumb:

> **Aha! moment:** *"A recession is when your neighbor loses their job; a depression is when you lose your job."*

More formally, a **depression** is an extreme, prolonged recession characterized by:
- A decline in Real GDP of **10% or more**.
- A contraction that lasts **several years** (rather than months).
- An unemployment rate rising above **20%** (compared to 8% to 10% in typical recessions).
- The collapse of the banking system and severe deflation. (See [Deflation101](./Deflation101.md)).
- *Example:* The Great Depression (1929–1933) in the U.S., where GDP fell by 30% and unemployment hit 25%.

---

## How to Cure a Recession: The Policy Response

To shorten a recession and reduce the pain, policymakers use stabilization policies:

### 1. Fiscal Stimulus (Government Actions)
The government tries to jump-start demand by spending money it doesn't have (deficit spending):
- **Spending Increases:** Funding infrastructure projects (roads, energy) to hire unemployed workers.
- **Tax Cuts:** Sending checks to households or cutting income taxes to encourage immediate spending.

### 2. Monetary Stimulus (Central Bank Actions)
The central bank makes borrowing as cheap as possible:
- **Rate Cuts:** Lowering interest rates close to zero to encourage businesses to borrow for investments and households to buy homes.
- **Quantitative Easing (QE):** If interest rates are already at zero, the central bank buys financial assets to pump cash directly into the banking system, encouraging banks to lend. (See [Deflation101](./Deflation101.md)).

---

## Key Terms

- **Recession:** A significant, widespread contraction in economic activity lasting more than a few months.
- **Depression:** A severe, long-term economic contraction with massive unemployment and GDP decline.
- **Trough:** The lowest point of the business cycle before the recovery begins.
- **Deficit Spending:** Government spending funded by borrowing rather than tax revenue.
- **Quantitative Easing (QE):** An unconventional monetary policy where central banks buy securities to pump cash into the economy.
