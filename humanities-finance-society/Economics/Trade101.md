# Trade 101

> *International trade is the exchange of capital, goods, and services across international borders or territories.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why does the United States buy t-shirts from Bangladesh, coffee from Colombia, and electronics from China, instead of manufacturing everything itself? Is it just because wages are lower in other countries?

While wage differences play a role, the real economic rationale for trade is much deeper. In fact, even if a country were the absolute best in the world at producing *everything*, it would still benefit from trading with other nations.

This is the power of **international trade**. Trade allows countries to specialize in what they do best, increasing total global production and lowering prices for consumers.

This article introduces absolute and comparative advantage, the debate over tariffs and protectionism, and the balance of payments.

---

## Absolute vs. Comparative Advantage

To understand why countries trade, economists look at two types of advantage:

### 1. Absolute Advantage (Who is faster/cheaper?)
The ability of a country to produce a good using fewer resources (or less time) than another country.
- **Example:** Brazil has an absolute advantage in coffee production because of its climate, while Canada has an absolute advantage in timber. It makes natural sense for Brazil to trade coffee for Canadian timber.

### 2. Comparative Advantage (Who has the lowest opportunity cost?)
The ability of a country to produce a good at a **lower opportunity cost** than another country. 
- Coined by David Ricardo in 1817, this is one of the most important concepts in economics.

> **Comparative Advantage** means that even if Country A is better at producing both Bread and Wine than Country B, they should still trade. Country A should specialize in the product where it has the *biggest* advantage, and Country B should produce the product where it has the *smallest* disadvantage.

### The Lawyer and Typist Analogy:
Imagine a wealthy lawyer who is also the fastest typist in town.
- The lawyer has an **absolute advantage** in both practicing law and typing.
- But should the lawyer type their own documents? 
- No. If the lawyer spends an hour typing, the opportunity cost is $300 in lost legal fees. If they hire a typist for $20 an hour, they benefit. The typist has a **comparative advantage** in typing because their opportunity cost is much lower.

## Free Trade vs. Protectionism

Governments often struggle to balance the benefits of trade with the pressure to protect local industries:

- **Free Trade:** Allowing goods and services to flow across borders without tariffs, quotas, or restrictions. This lowers prices for consumers and encourages efficiency.
- **Protectionism:** Using barriers to restrict imports to protect domestic industries from foreign competition:
  - **Tariffs:** Taxes placed on imported goods (making them more expensive, encouraging consumers to buy local).
  - **Quotas:** Limits on the physical quantity of a good that can be imported.
  - **The Trade-off:** While protectionism can protect local jobs in specific sectors (e.g., steelworkers), it raises prices for all consumers and can launch retaliatory **trade wars** that hurt the wider economy.

## The Balance of Payments: Global Accounting

How do we track a country's trade transactions with the rest of the world? We use the **Balance of Payments**, which is divided into:

- **Current Account:** Measures the trade of actual goods and services (the trade balance), plus net income from foreign investments.
- **Financial Account:** Measures the flow of financial assets (e.g., foreigners buying local stocks, property, or companies, and vice versa).
- *The Rule:* In global accounting, the Current Account and Financial Account must balance out. A trade deficit (Current Account) is always offset by a net inflow of foreign capital (Financial Account).

---

## Key Terms

- **Absolute Advantage:** The ability to produce more of a good using the same resources.
- **Comparative Advantage:** The ability to produce a good at a lower opportunity cost.
- **Free Trade:** Policy of unrestricted import and export.
- **Protectionism:** Policy of shielding domestic industries from foreign competition.
- **Tariff:** A tax imposed on imported goods.
- **Balance of Payments:** The record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world.
