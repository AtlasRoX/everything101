# Price 101

> *A price is the quantity of payment or compensation given by one party to another in return for a good or service. It is the coordinator of the market.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why does a cup of coffee cost $3, a movie ticket cost $12, and a house cost $400,000? Who decides these numbers?

In a free-market economy, there is no government department that sets prices. Instead, prices are established by the interaction of millions of buyers and sellers. Prices are determined by the forces of **supply and demand**.

Prices are not just labels; they are **signals**. They tell buyers whether to buy, tell sellers whether to produce, and ensure that resources are directed to where they are valued most.

This article introduces the concept of market equilibrium, what happens when supply and demand clash, and the consequences of government price controls.

---

## Market Equilibrium: Where Supply and Demand Meet

A market is like a tug-of-war. Buyers want the lowest possible price; sellers want the highest. They resolve this tension at a point called **market equilibrium** (or market-clearing price).

On a graph, equilibrium is the point where the demand curve and the supply curve **intersect**:

```
Price ($)
  ▲                 Supply (S)
  │    \           /
$3┼───────* E ────/──── (Equilibrium Price: Quantity Demanded = Quantity Supplied)
  │      / \     /
  │     /   \   /
  │    /     \ /     Demand (D)
  └───────────*────────► Quantity (Q)
  0          100 (Equilibrium Quantity)
```

At the equilibrium price ($3):
- **Quantity Demanded = Quantity Supplied** (100 cups).
- Every buyer who is willing to pay $3 finds a seller willing to sell at $3.
- There are no disappointed buyers and no unsold goods. The market is "cleared."

---

## Shortages and Surpluses: Market Adjustments

What happens if the price is not at equilibrium? The market automatically corrects itself:

### 1. Surplus (Price is Too High)
If the price is set above equilibrium (e.g., $5):
- Sellers want to supply a lot of goods (e.g., 150 cups).
- Buyers only want to buy a few (e.g., 50 cups).
- **Result:** A surplus of 100 cups. Unsold stock piles up on shelves. To clear this stock, sellers will cut their prices, pushing the price back down toward the equilibrium of $3.

### 2. Shortage (Price is Too Low)
If the price is set below equilibrium (e.g., $1):
- Buyers want to buy a lot of goods (e.g., 150 cups).
- Sellers only want to supply a few (e.g., 50 cups).
- **Result:** A shortage of 100 cups. Long lines form. To maximize profit and manage the crowd, sellers will raise their prices, pushing the price back up toward $3.

## Price Controls: Government Intervention

Sometimes governments believe the market equilibrium price is unfair and step in to set limits:

```
                  ┌───────────────────────────────┐
                  │    GOVERNMENT PRICE CONTROLS  │
                  └──────────────┬────────────────┘
                                 │
                 ┌───────────────┴───────────────┐
                 ▼                               ▼
      ┌─────────────────────┐         ┌─────────────────────┐
      │   PRICE CEILING     │         │    PRICE FLOOR      │
      │ Legally mandated    │         │ Legally mandated    │
      │ maximum price       │         │ minimum price       │
      │ (e.g., Rent Control)│         │ (e.g., Minimum Wage)│
      └──────────┬──────────┘         └──────────┬──────────┘
                 │                               │
                 ▼                               ▼
      ┌─────────────────────┐         ┌─────────────────────┐
      │  Creates Shortage   │         │   Creates Surplus   │
      │ Demand exceeds      │         │ Supply exceeds      │
      │ supply              │         │ demand              │
      └─────────────────────┘         └─────────────────────┘
```

### 1. Price Ceiling (Maximum Price Limit)
A legal maximum price a seller can charge. To protect buyers, it is set *below* equilibrium.
- **Example:** Rent control.
- **Consequences:** Because the price is kept artificially low, demand increases while supply decreases. This creates a permanent **shortage**. It also leads to black markets, under-the-table fees, and a decline in product quality (as landlords have no incentive to maintain buildings since they have a waiting list of tenants anyway).

### 2. Price Floor (Minimum Price Limit)
A legal minimum price a buyer must pay. To protect sellers, it is set *above* equilibrium.
- **Example:** Minimum wage laws (for labor), or agricultural price supports.
- **Consequences:** Because the price is kept artificially high, supply increases while demand decreases. This creates a permanent **surplus** (e.g., high unemployment if minimum wage is set too high relative to labor value, or surplus food stockpiles).

---

## Key Terms

- **Equilibrium Price:** The price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.
- **Equilibrium Quantity:** The quantity bought and sold at the equilibrium price.
- **Surplus:** A situation where quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded.
- **Shortage:** A situation where quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied.
- **Price Ceiling:** A legal maximum price.
- **Price Floor:** A legal minimum price.
