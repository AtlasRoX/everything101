# Economic Growth 101

> *Economic growth is the increase in the production of goods and services in an economy over time, typically measured as the percentage rate of increase in real GDP.*

---

## The Big Picture

For most of human history, economic growth was virtually zero. A peasant in medieval Europe lived a life that was materially identical to their ancestors hundreds of years before.

But around 1800, something changed. The Industrial Revolution launched a period of sustained **exponential growth**. Today, the average citizen in a developed country enjoys a standard of living (food, medicine, transit, and information access) that even the kings of the past could not imagine.

Why did this growth happen? What makes some countries grow rapidly while others remain in poverty? 

Understanding economic growth is not just about numbers; it is about finding the keys to lifting millions of people out of poverty and raising human living standards.

---

## The Magic of Compounding Growth: The Rule of 70

Economic growth is exponential, meaning it compounds over time. Because of this, even tiny differences in growth rates can lead to massive differences in wealth over generations.

To calculate how long it takes an economy to double in size, economists use the **Rule of 70**:

$$\text{Years to Double} = \frac{70}{\text{Annual Growth Rate}}$$

### The Rule in Action:
- **Country A** grows at **2%** per year. Its economy will double in size in **35 years** ($70 / 2 = 35$).
- **Country B** grows at **7%** per year (like China during its boom). Its economy will double in size in just **10 years** ($70 / 7 = 10$).

> **Aha! moment:** Over a century, a 5% difference in growth rates is the difference between a country becoming a wealthy superpower and remaining a developing nation. This is why economists are obsessed with marginal improvements in growth policies.

## The Solow Growth Model: Inputs and Technology

The foundational model of economic growth was developed by Nobel laureate **Robert Solow**. The model explains that an economy can grow by increasing three things:

### 1. Capital Accumulation (Investment)
Adding more tools, machines, and buildings. (See [Capital101](./Capital101.md)).
- **The Limit:** Diminishing returns. If you give a worker a second computer, they become slightly more productive. If you give them a tenth computer, they cannot use it. Eventually, adding more capital yields smaller and smaller growth, until you only build enough to replace broken machines. This is called the **steady state**.

### 2. Labor Force Growth
Adding more workers. (See [Labor101](./Labor101.md)).
- **The Limit:** Just having more people increases total GDP, but it does not increase **GDP per capita** (the average living standard), as the wealth is split among more mouths.

### 3. Technological Progress
Finding better ways to combine labor and capital to produce goods. 
- **The Engine:** In the Solow model, **technology is the only force that can sustain long-term economic growth per capita** once capital accumulation hits its diminishing returns limit. Technology shifts the entire production capacity upward.

## The Foundation: Institutions and the Rule of Law

Why do some countries invest in capital and invent technologies, while others do not? 

Economists like Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson (authors of *Why Nations Fail*) argue that the root cause of growth is **institutions**—the legal and social rules of the game:

- **Property Rights:** If you build a factory, you must be confident that the government or a warlord will not steal it from you tomorrow. Without secure property rights, no one will invest or innovate.
- **Rule of Law:** Contracts must be enforceable in fair courts. If businesses can easily cheat one another without penalty, trade collapses.
- **Inclusive vs. Extractive Institutions:** Inclusive institutions allow and encourage participation by the great majority of people in economic activities (markets, education), unlocking human capital. Extractive institutions are designed to extract incomes and wealth from one subset of society to benefit a different subset (the ruling elite), killing the incentive to grow.

---

## Key Terms

- **Economic Growth:** An increase in the capacity of an economy to produce goods and services.
- **Rule of 70:** A simple way to estimate the doubling time of an economy ($70 / \text{growth rate}$).
- **Steady State:** The point in the Solow growth model where capital investment equals capital depreciation, halting growth from capital accumulation alone.
- **Technological Progress:** Improvements in efficiency or methods of production that raise output without increasing input quantities.
- **Property Rights:** The legal authority to determine how a resource is used and to keep the gains from it.
