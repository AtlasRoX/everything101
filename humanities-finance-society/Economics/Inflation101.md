# Inflation 101

> *Inflation is the general increase in prices and fall in the purchasing value of money over time.*

---

## The Big Picture

If you ask your parents or grandparents how much a movie ticket or a cup of coffee cost when they were teenagers, they will name prices that seem incredibly cheap today. 

This is **inflation**. Inflation does not mean a single item (like housing or gas) became expensive. It means the general level of all prices in the economy rose. 

The flip side of inflation is that the value of your currency declines. If prices double, your cash has lost half its **purchasing power**—meaning a $10 bill buys only half as many goods as it did before.

This article introduces how we measure inflation, the two primary causes of price rises, and the danger of extreme inflation.

---

## How Inflation Is Measured: The CPI

To track changes in prices, governments look at what a typical family buys. They construct a theoretical **Consumer Price Index (CPI)**, often called the "market basket" of goods:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │    THE CPI BASKET       │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌──────────────┬────────────────┼────────────────┬──────────────┐
         ▼              ▼                ▼                ▼              ▼
┌────────────────┐┌──────────────┐┌──────────────┐┌──────────────┐┌──────────────┐
│  HOUSING (35%) ││  FOOD (15%)  ││TRANSIT (15%) ││MEDICAL (10%) ││OTHERS (15%)  │
│ Rent, bills    ││ Groceries,   ││ Fuel, cars,  ││ Doctors,     ││ Clothes,     │
│                ││ eating out   ││ train tickets││ medicine     ││ recreation   │
└────────────────┘└──────────────┘└──────────────┘└──────────────┘└──────────────┘
```

- Every month, government researchers check the prices of thousands of items in this basket.
- The **inflation rate** is the percentage change in the CPI from one year to the next. If the CPI basket cost $1,000 last year and costs $1,030 this year, the inflation rate is **3%**.

## What Causes Inflation? Two Main Drivers

Economists divide the causes of inflation into two main categories:

### 1. Demand-Pull Inflation ("Too much money chasing too few goods")
Occurs when consumer demand for goods and services outpaces the economy's ability to produce them.
- **The Mechanism:** When the economy is booming, unemployment is low, and wages are rising, people want to spend more. Because firms cannot build new factories fast enough to meet this demand, they raise their prices to ration the goods. (Aggregate Demand shifts right).

### 2. Cost-Push Inflation ("Supply Shocks")
Occurs when the costs of key production inputs rise, forcing businesses to raise prices to protect their profit margins.
- **The Mechanism:** If a conflict cuts off global oil supplies, oil prices spike. Because oil is used to transport almost all goods and to power factories, the cost of producing and shipping everything rises, causing a general price increase even if consumer demand is weak. (Aggregate Supply shifts left).

---

## The Danger of Extremes: Hyperinflation

While moderate inflation (around 2% per year) is considered normal and even healthy for a growing economy, extreme inflation can destroy a society.

When inflation exceeds **50% per month**, it is called **hyperinflation** (e.g., Weimar Germany in 1923, Zimbabwe in 2008, or Venezuela recently).

> **Aha! moment:** Hyperinflation is almost always caused by governments **printing money** to pay their debts. As the supply of money increases exponentially, the value of each note drops. People lose faith in the currency and try to spend it the moment they receive it (before it loses more value), causing prices to rise even faster. Eventually, the monetary system collapses, and people resort to barter or using foreign currencies.

---

## Key Terms

- **Inflation:** A general and progressive increase in prices.
- **Purchasing Power:** The financial ability to buy goods and services; the value of a currency.
- **Consumer Price Index (CPI):** A measure of the average change over time in the prices paid by consumers for a basket of goods.
- **Demand-Pull Inflation:** Price increases driven by excess consumer demand.
- **Cost-Push Inflation:** Price increases driven by rising production costs.
- **Hyperinflation:** Out-of-control inflation that destroys the value of a currency.
