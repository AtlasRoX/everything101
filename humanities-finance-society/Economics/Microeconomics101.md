# Microeconomics 101

> *Microeconomics studies how individual decision-makers—households and firms—allocate resources and make choices under scarcity.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why do you buy apple juice instead of orange juice? How does a company decide if it should hire one more employee or invest in a new machine?

While macroeconomics takes a step back to look at the entire national economy, **microeconomics** uses a microscope. It looks at the individual building blocks of the economy: you, your family, your local supermarket, and corporations.

At its heart, microeconomics is about **decision-making**. It explains how we measure the value of our choices, how we make trade-offs at the "margin," and what happens when individual choices accidentally harm society (market failures).

---

## Consumer Choice: Utility and the Margin

How do consumers decide what to buy? Economists use the concept of **utility**—a theoretical measure of satisfaction or happiness.

### 1. Marginal Utility
When you make a choice, you rarely make "all-or-nothing" decisions. You don't decide between eating 10 apples or 0 apples; you decide whether to eat *one more* apple. This is called **thinking at the margin**.
- **Marginal Utility (MU):** The additional satisfaction you get from consuming one more unit of a good.

### 2. The Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility
- If you are starving, the **first slice of pizza** gives you immense satisfaction (high marginal utility).
- The **second slice** is good, but slightly less satisfying.
- By the **fifth slice**, you are full; the marginal utility is near zero or even negative (it makes you feel sick).

> **Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility:** As you consume more of a good, the additional satisfaction (marginal utility) derived from each new unit declines.

This explains why demand curves slope downward: because each new unit of a good gives you less satisfaction than the last, you are only willing to buy more if the seller lowers the price.

## Marginal Analysis for Businesses

Just like consumers, businesses make decisions at the margin:
- **Marginal Cost (MC):** The cost of producing one more unit of a good.
- **Marginal Revenue (MR):** The revenue gained from selling one more unit of a good.

To maximize profit, a business should keep producing goods as long as the revenue from the next item exceeds the cost to make it (**MR > MC**). The exact point of maximum profit is where **MR = MC**:

```
           Cost/Revenue ($)
             ▲                  Marginal Cost (MC)
             │                 /
             │  * MR > MC     /
             │ (Keep building)
           $3┼───────────────* Profit Maximizing Point (MR = MC)
             │              / \
             │  * MR < MC  /   \
             │(Stop building)   \ Marginal Revenue (MR)
             └─────────────*─────► Quantity (Q)
             0            100
```

## Market Failures: When the Market Falls Short

Normally, the "invisible hand" of the market coordinates choices efficiently. But sometimes, individual self-interest leads to outcomes that are bad for society. This is called a **market failure**. The two primary types are:

### 1. Externalities
An externality is a cost or benefit of an economic activity that falls on a third party who did not choose to be involved.
- **Negative Externality (Spillover Cost):** Pollution. A factory makes cheap chemicals for profit, but dumps toxic waste in a local river, hurting fishermen and residents. Because the factory doesn't pay for the cleanup, it produces too many chemicals. (Solution: carbon taxes, pollution limits).
- **Positive Externality (Spillover Benefit):** Vaccination. If you get a flu shot, you protect yourself, but you also protect colleagues by not spreading the virus. Because you don't get paid for protecting others, people buy fewer vaccines than is socially optimal. (Solution: government subsidies, free public clinics).

### 2. Public Goods
Goods that private businesses cannot profitably produce because they are:
- **Non-excludable:** You cannot prevent people who didn't pay from using them (the free rider problem).
- **Non-rivalrous:** One person using the good doesn't reduce its availability to others.
- *Examples:* Lighthouses, streetlights, national defense. Because private markets will underproduce these, the government must step in, collect taxes, and provide them.

## Key Terms

- **Microeconomics:** The study of decision-making by households and firms in specific markets.
- **Utility:** The satisfaction or value a consumer receives from a good or service.
- **Marginal Utility:** The change in total utility from consuming one additional unit.
- **Marginal Cost (MC):** The cost of producing one additional unit of output.
- **Marginal Revenue (MR):** The additional revenue gained from selling one additional unit.
- **Externality:** A side effect of an economic activity that affects third parties.
- **Public Good:** A good that is non-excludable and non-rivalrous, leading to market failure if left to private firms.
