# Productivity 101

> *Productivity measures the efficiency of production, calculated as the ratio of aggregate output to aggregate inputs used in the production process.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why do people in wealthy countries enjoy high wages and short work hours, while people in poor countries work long days for low pay?

It is not because workers in wealthy countries work harder or are more moral. It is because they are more **productive**. 

In economics, productivity is the ultimate driver of living standards. As the Nobel laureate Paul Krugman famously wrote: **"Productivity isn't everything, but in the long run it is almost everything. A country's ability to improve its standard of living over time depends almost entirely on its ability to raise its output per worker."**

This article introduces the types of productivity, the factors that drive it, and why efficiency is the secret to wealth.

---

## The Types of Productivity

When economists measure efficiency, they look at different inputs:

### 1. Labor Productivity
The amount of goods and services produced per hour of labor (or per worker).
- **Formula:** $\text{Labor Productivity} = \text{Total Output} / \text{Total Labor Hours}$
- This is the most common measure because it is easy to calculate and directly tracks average wages.

### 2. Total Factor Productivity (TFP)
The efficiency with which *all* inputs (both labor and capital) are combined to produce output.
- If an economy has the same number of workers and the same number of machines, but manages to produce 10% more output because it redesigned the factory floor layout, the increase is due to TFP.
- TFP represents **technological and organizational innovation**.

## The Drivers of Productivity

How can a country raise its productivity? There are three main levers:

```
                      ┌─────────────────────────┐
                      │  DRIVERS OF PRODUCTIVITY│
                      └────────────┬────────────┘
                                   │
         ┌─────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┐
         ▼                         ▼                         ▼
┌──────────────────┐      ┌──────────────────┐      ┌──────────────────┐
│  CAPITAL DEPTH   │      │  HUMAN CAPITAL   │      │   TECHNOLOGY &   │
│ Giving workers   │      │ Education and    │      │   ORGANIZATION   │
│ better machinery │      │ skills to operate│      │ Better methods & │
│ & tools          │      │ complex tools    │      │ inventions       │
└──────────────────┘      └──────────────────┘      └──────────────────┘
```

1. **Capital Depth:** Increasing the amount of physical capital per worker. A farmer with a tractor is far more productive than a farmer with a hoe.
2. **Human Capital:** Improving the skills, education, and health of the workforce. Giving a worker a complex machine (like a computer) is useless unless they have the training to operate it. (See [Labor101](./Labor101.md)).
3. **Technology and Organization:** Inventing new technologies (like the internet or steam engine) or finding more efficient ways to organize production (like assembly lines or supply chains).

## Why Productivity Is the Key to Wealth

Think about a self-contained island economy where a worker can only pick **2 coconuts an hour**.
- The average real wage of the worker cannot exceed 2 coconuts an hour, because you cannot distribute more than is produced.
- If the worker invents a pole that allows them to pick **10 coconuts an hour**, their productivity rises.
- Now, the average real wage can rise to 10 coconuts an hour. The island can consume more coconuts, or the worker can choose to work fewer hours and enjoy leisure, raising their standard of living.

> **Aha! moment:** Wages and living standards are ultimately anchored to productivity. You cannot consume what you do not produce. A country can print money, but that only causes inflation; the only way to make a country wealthier is to make it more productive.

---

## Key Terms

- **Productivity:** The ratio of output produced to inputs used.
- **Labor Productivity:** Output produced per worker or per hour worked.
- **Total Factor Productivity (TFP):** The portion of output growth not explained by increases in labor and capital inputs; measures innovation.
- **Capital Depth:** The ratio of physical capital to labor in an economy.
- **Innovation:** The development of new products, services, or production processes that raise TFP.
