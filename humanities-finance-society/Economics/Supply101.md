# Supply 101

> *Supply is the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to sell at various prices.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why does a farmer grow wheat instead of barley? Why do oil companies drill in deep ocean waters only when oil prices are high?

The answer lies in **supply**—the seller side of the market. While consumers want to buy goods at the lowest possible price to maximize their satisfaction, producers want to sell goods at the highest possible price to maximize their **profit**.

Profit is the driving engine of supply. Understanding supply explains how businesses make production decisions, how technology lowers costs, and how inputs shape prices.

---

## The Law of Supply

The most fundamental rule of producer behavior is the **Law of Supply**:

> **The Law of Supply:** Other things being equal, as the price of a good **rises**, the quantity supplied **rises**. As the price **falls**, the quantity supplied **falls**.

This happens because higher prices make production more profitable, encouraging existing firms to produce more and new firms to enter the market. Conversely, low prices squeeze profit margins, forcing firms to cut production or close down.

---

## The Supply Curve: Visualizing Producer Behavior

Economists graph supply using a **Supply Curve**, which always slopes **upward** (representing the direct relationship between price and quantity):

```
Price ($)
  ▲                  Supply Curve (S)
$5┼───────* B (At $5, price is high, producers supply 20 units)
  │      /
$3┼─────* A (At $3, quantity supplied is 10)
  │    /
  │   /
  └────────────────► Quantity (Q)
  0   10  20
```

- **Movement Along the Curve:** Occurs **only** when the price of the good itself changes. Moving from Point A to Point B is called a "change in quantity supplied."

---

## Shifting the Supply Curve: Input and Technology Shifts

What happens if something other than price changes? For example, what if a new robotic arm is invented that cuts the cost of assembling cars in half?

Producers will supply more cars at *every* price. This is a **shift in supply** (the entire curve moves to the right):

```
Price ($)
  ▲                 Shift Right (S1 -> S2)
  │                  (Increase in Supply)
  │             S1      S2
  │            /       /
  │           /       /
  └───────────────────► Quantity (Q)
  0
```

The primary factors that shift the supply curve (known as **determinants of supply**) are:

- **Input Prices (Costs of Production):** If the price of steel rises, making cars becomes more expensive. Supply decreases (shifts left). If wages fall, making cars becomes cheaper, and supply increases (shifts right).
- **Technology:** Technological improvements lower production costs, increasing supply.
- **Number of Sellers:** More firms entering a market increases market supply.
- **Expectations:** If farmers expect wheat prices to double next month, they will store their wheat in silos and decrease supply today, waiting for the price hike.

---

## The Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns

Why does the supply curve slope upward? Why does it cost more to produce more? The answer lies in the physical limits of production, known as the **Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns**.

Imagine a small pizza kitchen with one pizza oven:
- Adding a **second worker** increases output significantly because they can divide labor (one rolls dough, one adds toppings).
- Adding a **fifth worker** increases output slightly, but they begin getting in each other's way because there is still only one oven.
- Adding a **tenth worker** might actually *decrease* output because the kitchen is overcrowded and workers are idle.

> **Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns:** As you add more of a variable input (like labor) to a fixed input (like capital/ovens), the additional output produced by each new unit of input will eventually decrease.

Because each additional pizza becomes more expensive to produce (requiring more labor time per pizza), a business will only increase production if the market price rises enough to cover these rising marginal costs. This is why the supply curve slopes upward.

---

## Key Terms

- **Supply:** The willingness and ability of sellers to offer a good for sale.
- **Law of Supply:** The rule that price and quantity supplied move in the same direction.
- **Input Prices:** The costs of resources (labor, raw materials) used in production.
- **Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns:** The rule that adding inputs to a fixed asset eventually yields smaller additions to output.
- **Profit:** Total revenue minus total costs.
