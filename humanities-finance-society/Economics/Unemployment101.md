# Unemployment 101

> *Unemployment occurs when people who are active and willing to work are unable to find a job. It represents a waste of a country's labor resources.*

---

## The Big Picture

When you hear that the unemployment rate is **5%**, what does that actually mean? Does it mean that 5% of the country's population does not have a job?

No. If we counted everyone without a job, we would include toddlers, retirees, full-time students, and people who choose to stay home to care for their children. None of these people are considered "unemployed" in economics.

Unemployment is a specific measure of **idle labor resources**. Understanding how it is calculated, the different types of unemployment, and why some unemployment is actually healthy, is a major focus of macroeconomic policy.

---

## How Unemployment Is Calculated: The Labor Force

To calculate unemployment, the government divides the adult population (usually aged 16+) into three groups:

1. **Employed:** Those who did any paid work in the past week (full-time or part-time).
2. **Unemployed:** Those who do not have a job, but **are actively looking for work** in the past four weeks, and are available to start immediately.
3. **Not in the Labor Force:** Those who do not have a job and are **not** looking for one (e.g., retirees, students, homemakers, or discouraged workers who have given up searching).

The **Labor Force** is the sum of the Employed and the Unemployed:

$$\text{Labor Force} = \text{Employed} + \text{Unemployed}$$

The **Unemployment Rate** is the percentage of the labor force that is unemployed:

$$\text{Unemployment Rate} = \frac{\text{Unemployed}}{\text{Labor Force}} \times 100$$

The **Labor Force Participation Rate** measures the percentage of the adult population that is active in the economy:

$$\text{Participation Rate} = \frac{\text{Labor Force}}{\text{Adult Population}} \times 100$$

## The Three Types of Unemployment

Not all unemployment is caused by the same problem. Economists identify three distinct types:

### 1. Frictional Unemployment (Search Time)
Temporary unemployment as workers move between jobs or enter the labor force for the first time.
- **Example:** A university graduate searching for their first job, or a worker who quit their job to move to a new city and is interviewing for a new position.
- **Nature:** Normal, short-term, and healthy. It shows that workers have the freedom to search for jobs that match their skills.

### 2. Structural Unemployment (Mismatch)
Occurs when there is a mismatch between the skills workers have and the skills employers need, or where workers are located vs. where the jobs are.
- **Example:** An assembly line worker laid off due to automation, or a coal miner whose mine was closed, who lacks the skills needed to work in the technology or healthcare sectors.
- **Nature:** Longer-term and difficult to solve. Requires retraining and education rather than just economic growth.

### 3. Cyclical Unemployment (Recessions)
Unemployment caused by a downturn in the business cycle (a recession). (See [Macroeconomics101](./Macroeconomics101.md)).
- **Example:** A car salesman laid off because the economy is in a recession and people are not buying new cars.
- **Nature:** Dangerous. The focus of monetary and fiscal stimulus policies. When the economy recovers, cyclical unemployment disappears.

---

## The Natural Rate of Unemployment

Can we achieve a **0% unemployment rate**?

No. Because frictional and structural unemployment are natural parts of a dynamic, changing economy, a healthy economy will always have some unemployment. 

The sum of frictional and structural unemployment is called the **Natural Rate of Unemployment** (or Full Employment). 
- When an economy is at "full employment," cyclical unemployment is **zero**, but the unemployment rate is still around **4% to 5%** (representing workers transitioning between jobs or retraining).
- If unemployment drops below the natural rate, it means the labor market is unsustainably tight, which can lead to rapid wage increases and inflation.

---

## Key Terms

- **Labor Force:** The total number of workers, including both the employed and the unemployed.
- **Unemployment Rate:** The percentage of the labor force that is unemployed.
- **Labor Force Participation Rate:** The percentage of the adult population that is in the labor force.
- **Frictional Unemployment:** Short-term unemployment arising from normal job search transitions.
- **Structural Unemployment:** Long-term unemployment arising from a mismatch between worker skills and job requirements.
- **Cyclical Unemployment:** Unemployment caused by economic recessions.
- **Natural Rate of Unemployment:** The normal rate of unemployment around which the actual rate fluctuates; the rate when cyclical unemployment is zero.
