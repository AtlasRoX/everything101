# Capital 101

> *In economics, capital refers to manufactured assets used to produce other goods and services, rather than to satisfy human wants directly.*

---

## The Big Picture

When most people hear the word "capital," they think of money—the balance in a business's bank account or the funds used to start a company. 

But in economics, money is not capital. You cannot build a house with dollar bills or manufacture a car with a bank statement. Money is simply a financial tool. 

True economic capital is **physical**: it is the machinery, tools, factories, offices, and computers that workers use to produce value. Capital is what turns human labor into industrial-scale output. A worker with a shovel can dig a trench in a day; that same worker with an excavator (physical capital) can dig a trench in an hour.

This article introduces the different types of capital, the role of investment, and the concept of depreciation.

---

## The Three Faces of Capital

While physical capital is the primary input in economics, the word is used in three related ways:

| Type of Capital | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical Capital** | The man-made physical resources used in production. | Factories, machinery, computers, tools, warehouses. |
| **Human Capital** | The skills, education, and health of the workforce. | A surgeon's medical degree, a programmer's coding skills. (See [Labor101](./Labor101.md)). |
| **Financial Capital** | The money and financial assets businesses use to buy physical capital. | Bank loans, shares of stock, venture capital funds. |

## The Economics of Investment: Building Capital

Capital does not exist naturally in the wild; it must be manufactured. Building capital requires **investment**.

To invest, a society must make a trade-off under scarcity: it must consume less today to produce tools that will increase production tomorrow. (See [Scarcity101](./Scarcity101.md)).

```
                       ┌─────────────────────────┐
                       │     SCARCE RESOURCES    │
                       └────────────┬────────────┘
                                    │
         ┌──────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                     ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                  ┌──────────────────┐
│   CONSUMPTION    │                                  │   INVESTMENT     │
│ Eat resources    │                                  │ Save resources   │
│ today; Feels good│                                  │ today; Build new │
│ now              │                                  │ tools for future │
└──────────────────┘                                  └────────┬─────────┘
                                                               │
                                                               ▼
                                                      ┌──────────────────┐
                                                      │ ECONOMIC GROWTH  │
                                                      │ More production  │
                                                      │ in the future    │
                                                      └──────────────────┘
```

- If a farmer eats all their grain today (**consumption**), they have a good meal.
- If they save some grain to use as seed for next year (**investment**), they temporarily go hungry but will harvest much more grain in the future, increasing their long-term wealth.

This choice is the root of **capital accumulation**—the process of growing a country's stock of tools and machinery to drive long-term economic growth.

---

## Depreciation: The Slow Decay of Capital

Physical capital does not last forever. As machines are used, they wear out, break down, or become outdated. This wear and tear is called **depreciation**.

- **Physical Wear:** A delivery truck's engine breaks down after 200,000 miles.
- **Obsolescence:** A state-of-the-art computer built in 2010 is still physically working today, but it is obsolete because modern software requires faster processors.

For an economy's capital stock to grow, the rate of **gross investment** (new capital built) must exceed the rate of **depreciation** (capital worn out). If a country only builds enough machines to replace the ones that broke down, its capital stock remains flat, and its economic growth will stall.

---

## Key Terms

- **Physical Capital:** Man-made physical tools, buildings, and machinery used to produce goods.
- **Financial Capital:** Money used to acquire physical capital.
- **Investment:** Spending on new capital goods (machinery, buildings, software).
- **Capital Accumulation:** The growth of the total capital stock over time.
- **Depreciation:** The loss of value or physical decay of capital assets over time.
