# Development Economics 101

> *Development economics is a branch of economics that focuses on improving the fiscal, economic, and social conditions in developing countries.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why does a child born in Switzerland expect to live to 83, get a high-quality education, and earn a high wage, while a child born in Burundi faces a life expectancy of 61, limited school access, and extreme poverty?

This is the central question of **development economics**. While traditional economics focuses on how markets allocate resources in wealthy countries, development economics studies how countries can escape poverty and build the systems required to flourish.

Escape from poverty is not just about having more money; it is about building the health, education, and institutional infrastructure that unlocks human potential.

---

## The Poverty Trap

One of the greatest challenges in development is the **poverty trap**—a self-reinforcing mechanism that keeps poor countries poor.

At its simplest:

```
 ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
 │                   Low average income                   │
 └──────────────────────────┬─────────────────────────────┘
                            ▼
 ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
 │        Low savings (all income goes to survival)       │
 └──────────────────────────┬─────────────────────────────┘
                            ▼
 ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
 │   Low investment in capital, health, and education     │
 └──────────────────────────┬─────────────────────────────┘
                            ▼
 ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
 │        Low productivity (lack of tools & health)       │
 └──────────────────────────┬─────────────────────────────┘
                            ▼
 ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
 │    Low economic growth, keeping average income low     │
 └──────────────────────────▲─────────────────────────────┘
                            │
                            └──── [The cycle repeats] ────┘
```

A poor country has low savings because citizens must spend every penny on immediate food and shelter. Without savings, banks have no money to lend to businesses, and the government has no tax revenue to build roads, schools, or hospitals. Without these tools, productivity remains low, and incomes stay depressed.

To develop, an economy must find a way to **break this circle**—either through foreign investment, aid, natural resources, or institutional reform.

## The Core Drivers of Development

Development economists study several levers to break the poverty trap:

### 1. Institutional Quality
As discussed in [EconomicGrowth101](./EconomicGrowth101.md), having secure property rights, a fair legal system, and low corruption is the single most important predictor of long-term development. If citizens fear their wealth will be stolen by corrupt officials, they will not invest.

### 2. Education and Health (Human Capital)
A healthy, educated workforce is vastly more productive.
- **Health:** Combating diseases like malaria or worm infections raises school attendance and adult wages.
- **Education:** Building schools is not enough; the focus must be on learning outcomes (literacy and numeracy) to build useful labor skills.

### 3. Financial Inclusion and Microfinance
In developing nations, poor people often cannot get bank loans to start businesses because they lack collateral (like a house). 
- **Microfinance:** Providing tiny, low-interest loans to poor entrepreneurs (often women) to help them buy inputs (like sewing machines or seeds) to start small businesses, helping them lift themselves out of poverty.

### 4. Foreign Aid: The Debate
Economists argue about the effectiveness of foreign aid:
- **Sachs View (Jeffrey Sachs):** Aid is essential to kick-start development. Poor countries are stuck in a trap and need a "big push" of aid to build roads, vaccinate children, and fertilize soil, which will then launch self-sustaining growth.
- **Easterly View (William Easterly):** Massive top-down aid projects often fail because of corruption, lack of local knowledge, and bureaucratic waste. Development should be driven from the bottom up by local entrepreneurs, with aid focused on small, measurable goals.

---

## Key Terms

- **Poverty Trap:** A self-reinforcing mechanism that causes poverty to persist over generations.
- **Human Capital:** The education, skills, and health of a population.
- **Microfinance:** Extremely small loans given to impoverished entrepreneurs to start businesses.
- **Foreign Aid:** Resources transferred from wealthy countries to developing countries for developmental purposes.
- **Financial Inclusion:** Making financial services (savings, loans, insurance) accessible and affordable to all.
