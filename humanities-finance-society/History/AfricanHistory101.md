# African History 101: Wealth, Diversity, and Connections 🌍

In 1324 CE, a West African emperor named **Mansa Musa** set off on a pilgrimage to Mecca. He traveled with a caravan of 60,000 men and dozens of camels carrying thousands of pounds of pure gold. Along the way, Musa gave away so much gold in Cairo that he accidentally inflated the local economy, crashing the value of gold in Egypt for over a decade.

Mansa Musa remains, by many estimates, the wealthiest individual to have ever lived. 

His story challenges a common misconception: that Africa was historically isolated or poor. In reality, Africa is a vast, diverse continent that was home to advanced empires, bustling university cities, and global trade networks that stretched to Rome, India, and China.

---

## The Trans-Saharan Highway: Gold and Salt 🏜️

For centuries, the Sahara Desert was not a barrier; it was a highway. West African empires like Ghana, Mali, and Songhai grew rich by managing the **Trans-Saharan Trade**:

```
        ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │            NORTH AFRICA (Salt & Goods)                 │
        └───────────────────────┬────────────────────────────────┘
                                │ [Caravans cross Sahara]
                                ▼
        ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │        THE SAHEL EMPIRES (Ghana, Mali, Songhai)        │  ◄─── Middlemen (Taxing trade)
        └───────────────────────▲────────────────────────────────┘
                                │ [Gold brought from South]
                                │
        ┌───────────────────────┴────────────────────────────────┐
        │            WEST AFRICAN FOREST (Gold Mines)            │
        └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

*   **The Exchange:** Merchants from North Africa brought salt (essential for preserving food in hot climates), books, and textiles. West African forest kingdoms brought gold and ivory.
*   **The Middlemen:** The Sahel empires sat in the middle of these regions. By taxing the flow of gold and salt, they built massive wealth, funded professional standing armies, and sponsored scholarship.
*   **Timbuktu:** Under the Mali Empire, the city of Timbuktu became a global center of learning. Its universities and libraries housed thousands of manuscripts covering astronomy, mathematics, law, and medicine.

---

## Other Empires of Africa: East and South 🗺️

African civilization extended far beyond the Sahara:

*   **The Kingdom of Aksum (East Africa):** Located in modern-day Ethiopia and Eritrea, Aksum was a major maritime trading power that connected Rome with India. In the 4th century, it became one of the first empires in the world to adopt Christianity as its official religion.
*   **The Swahili Coast (East Africa):** A string of wealthy city-states (like Kilwa and Zanzibar) that blended Bantu African culture with Arab and Persian influence. They traded gold and ivory across the Indian Ocean in exchange for Chinese silk and Indian textiles.
*   **Great Zimbabwe (Southern Africa):** A powerful kingdom known for its monumental stone architecture built without mortar. It was the hub of a trade network that linked inland gold mines with Swahili coast traders.

## How History is Kept: The Griots 🗣️

In many African societies, history was not written in books. Instead, it was preserved through oral traditions. 

**Griots** were professional historians, musicians, and advisers to kings. They memorized family trees, wars, laws, and treaties, passing them down through generations with absolute precision. Today, historians recognize that oral traditions are valuable primary sources, requiring the same critical analysis as written documents.

---

## External Disruptions: Slave Trade & Partition 💔

Africa's trajectory was violently disrupted by two major events:
1.  **The Transatlantic Slave Trade (16th-19th Century):** Millions of young Africans were captured and shipped to the Americas to work on plantations, draining the continent of its human labor and causing political instability.
2.  **The "Scramble for Africa" (1884):** European powers met at the **Berlin Conference** and drew artificial borders across Africa, dividing ethnic groups and combining rival communities. By 1914, Europeans colonized 90% of the continent.

---

## Why African History Matters Today

*   **Re-thinking Borders:** The artificial borders drawn by European colonizers are the borders of modern African nations, explaining many of the political tensions and conflicts on the continent today.
*   **Economic Potential:** Africa is home to some of the fastest-growing economies and the youngest population in the world, built on resources and trade routes that have connected the continent for thousands of years.
