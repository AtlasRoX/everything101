# Criminal Law 101

> *Criminal law is the body of law that relates to crime, regulating the apprehension, charging, and trial of suspected persons, and fixing the penalties applicable to convicted offenders.*

---

## The Big Picture

If you accidentally hit a neighbor's parked car because you were distracted, you must pay to repair it. This is a civil dispute. (See [CivilLaw101](./CivilLaw101.md)). 

But what if you intentionally steal your neighbor's car to sell it for cash? 

Now you have crossed the line from civil liability to **criminal liability**. 

In criminal law, a crime is viewed not just as a harm against a single victim, but as an **offense against the public order and the state**. Because of this, the state itself prosecutes the offender, and the penalties can include the loss of liberty (prison time) or even life, requiring a much higher standard of proof to protect the innocent.

This article introduces the two components of a crime, the classification of offenses, the high burden of proof, and legal defenses.

---

## The Two Elements of a Crime: Actus Reus and Mens Rea

To convict someone of a crime, the prosecution must prove two physical and mental components beyond a reasonable doubt:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │   ANATOMY OF A CRIME    │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                            ┌──────────────────┐
│   ACTUS REUS     │                                            │     MENS REA     │
│  (Guilty Act)    │                                            │  (Guilty Mind)   │
│ The physical act │                                            │ The mental state │
│ of committing    │                                            │ or intention to  │
│ the crime        │                                            │ cause harm       │
└──────────────────┘                                            └──────────────────┘
```

1. **Actus Reus (The Guilty Act):** The physical action or omission that violates a law. (e.g., pulling a trigger, taking money from a cash register). You cannot be arrested for just thinking about a crime; you must execute a physical act.
2. **Mens Rea (The Guilty Mind):** The mental state of intention, knowledge, or recklessness at the moment the act was committed.
  - **Intentional:** You wanted the result to happen.
  - **Reckless:** You knew there was a high risk of harm but ignored it (e.g., driving 100 mph in a school zone).
  - **Negligent:** You *should* have known of a risk, but failed to notice it.

> **Aha! moment:** If you pick up an umbrella at a restaurant thinking it is yours, there is **no crime** because you lacked the *mens rea* (you had no intent to steal). If you pick it up knowing it belongs to someone else, you have committed theft (both *actus reus* and *mens rea* are present).

## Offense Classifications

Crimes are generally grouped by severity:

- **Infractions (Violations):** Minor offenses that carry only a fine and no prison time (e.g., jaywalking, speeding tickets). Not on your criminal record.
- **Misdemeanors:** Moderate crimes punishable by a fine or up to **one year in local jail** (e.g., petty theft, simple assault, trespass).
- **Felonies:** Severe crimes punishable by **more than one year in state prison** or death (e.g., murder, grand theft, kidnapping).

---

## The Burden of Proof: Beyond a Reasonable Doubt

Because a criminal conviction can take away your freedom, the legal system sets the highest standard of proof:

- **Beyond a Reasonable Doubt (99%+):** The prosecution must prove the defendant's guilt to a point of near certainty. If the jury has any logical, sensible doubt about the defendant's guilt, they must vote "not guilty." (Compare to the lower 51% "preponderance" standard in civil law).

---

## Legal Defenses

Even if the defendant committed the act, they can be acquitted if they prove a legal defense:

- **Self-Defense:** The use of reasonable force to protect yourself or others from imminent physical harm.
- **Insanity:** A mental disease made the defendant unable to understand the nature of their act or know that it was wrong (lack of capacity for *mens rea*).
- **Duress:** The defendant was forced to commit the crime under an immediate threat of death or serious injury.

---

## Key Terms

- **Criminal Law:** Law governing crimes and public order.
- **Actus Reus:** The physical guilty act.
- **Mens Rea:** The mental guilty intent.
- **Felony:** A major crime carrying a prison sentence of over a year.
- **Misdemeanor:** A minor crime carrying a jail sentence of under a year.
- **Burden of Proof:** The obligation to prove assertions in a trial.
