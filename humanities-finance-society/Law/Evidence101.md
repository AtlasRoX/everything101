# Evidence 101

> *Evidence is the law governing the rules and legal principles that determine what proof is admissible in a trial court.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why can't a lawyer walk into a courtroom and present a rumor, a diary, or a lie detector test as proof?

Because without rules governing what counts as legitimate proof, trials would degenerate into chaotic contests of gossip, prejudice, and manufactured facts. Juries — made up of ordinary people without legal or scientific training — are vulnerable to being swayed by unreliable or emotionally inflammatory material.

The **Rules of Evidence** are the gatekeeper of the courtroom. They determine what information — witness testimonies, physical objects, documents, and expert opinions — the judge and jury are allowed to see and hear when deciding a case.

Evidence law is equally critical in criminal and civil proceedings, though the stakes differ: in criminal cases, the stakes include liberty and life; in civil cases, they typically involve money.

This article introduces the concept of admissibility, the types of evidence, the hearsay rule, constitutional exclusions, and privileged communications.

---

## A Brief History of Evidence Law

- **Ancient Trials:** In early legal systems, "proof" was often ordeal or oath. The accused would undergo a physical challenge (carrying red-hot iron, water immersion) — survival being taken as divine proof of innocence.
- **Trial by Jury (12th–13th Century):** As English juries replaced ordeal, courts began developing rules about what neighbors (who served as jurors, initially based on local knowledge) could consider.
- **Common Law Development:** By the 17th and 18th centuries, English courts refined the rules against hearsay, established the oath requirement for witnesses, and developed the concept of the best evidence rule.
- **Federal Rules of Evidence (1975):** The United States codified evidence law into the **Federal Rules of Evidence (FRE)**, which have been adopted (often with modifications) by most US states and have influenced evidence codes internationally.
- **Scientific Evidence:** The 20th century brought new challenges — fingerprints, blood types, lie detectors, DNA. Courts developed standards (like the **Daubert standard**) to determine when scientific evidence is reliable enough to present to a jury.

## Types of Evidence

Evidence falls into several fundamental categories:

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct Evidence** | Evidence that directly proves a fact without inference | An eyewitness who saw the defendant fire the gun |
| **Circumstantial Evidence** | Evidence that requires an inference to connect it to a conclusion | Finding the defendant's fingerprints at the crime scene |
| **Physical Evidence (Real Evidence)** | A tangible object presented in court | A murder weapon, a bloody glove, a forged document |
| **Documentary Evidence** | Written or recorded materials | Contracts, emails, surveillance video, financial records |
| **Testimonial Evidence** | Sworn statements from witnesses | A witness account, an expert's analysis |
| **Demonstrative Evidence** | Visual aids created for the trial | Maps, diagrams, forensic animations |

> **Key insight:** Contrary to popular belief, **circumstantial evidence is not weak** evidence. The vast majority of convictions rest on circumstantial evidence. A fingerprint, a motive, and an inconsistent alibi together can be more compelling than a single eyewitness (who may be mistaken or lying).

## Admissibility: The Two-Step Test

For any piece of evidence to be allowed in court (**admissible**), it must pass a strict two-step test administered by the judge:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │    ADMISSIBILITY TEST   │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
                                         ▼
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │      1. RELEVANCE       │
                            │ Does this proof make a  │
                            │ fact more or less       │
                            │ likely to be true?      │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │ (Yes)
                                         ▼
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │     2. RELIABILITY      │
                            │ Is this proof clean of  │
                            │ hearsay, prejudice, or  │
                            │ illegal gathering?      │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │ (Yes)
                                         ▼
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │    EVIDENCE ADMITTED    │
                            └─────────────────────────┘
```

1. **Relevance:** Does the evidence have any tendency to make a fact of consequence in the case more or less probable? (e.g., a suspect having a gun is relevant to a shooting; their poor grades in high school are not).
2. **Reliability / Exclusionary Rules:** Even if relevant, evidence is excluded if:
   - It is highly **prejudicial** — the emotional impact on the jury far outweighs its factual value.
   - It was **gathered illegally** — violating the suspect's constitutional rights.
   - It violates the **Hearsay Rule**.
   - It is **unreliable scientific evidence** — not generally accepted by the scientific community.

## The Hearsay Rule: No Secondhand Gossip

One of the most famous and complex rules in evidence law is the ban on **hearsay**:

> **Hearsay:** An out-of-court statement, offered in court to prove the truth of the matter it asserts.

The core problem with hearsay is that the person who originally made the statement is **not in court to be cross-examined** — so neither the judge, jury, nor opposing lawyer can assess their credibility, memory, or sincerity.

### Examples:
- Witness Bob says: *"I saw Dave pull the trigger."* → **Direct testimony** (admissible — Dave's lawyer can cross-examine Bob).
- Witness Bob says: *"Sarah told me she saw Dave pull the trigger."* → **Hearsay** (generally inadmissible — Dave's lawyer cannot cross-examine Sarah).

### Major Exceptions to the Hearsay Rule

Because some out-of-court statements carry inherent reliability, courts recognize important exceptions:

| Exception | Rationale | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Dying Declaration** | People are assumed not to lie on their deathbeds | A shooting victim names their attacker before dying |
| **Excited Utterance** | Statements made in the heat of shock are unrehearsed | "He just stabbed me!" shouted immediately after the attack |
| **Present Sense Impression** | A statement describing an event as it happens | "He's reaching into his jacket right now" |
| **Admission by Party-Opponent** | The defendant's own prior statements are their own words | A defendant who bragged about committing the crime |
| **Business Records** | Routine records kept in the ordinary course of business | Hospital intake forms, financial ledgers |

## The Exclusionary Rule: Illegal Evidence Thrown Out

The **Exclusionary Rule** (established in *Mapp v. Ohio*, 1961) prohibits illegally obtained evidence from being used in court — even if it is genuine and relevant.

- **Example:** Police search a house without a warrant and find drugs. Under the Exclusionary Rule, the drugs cannot be introduced as evidence, and the case may be dismissed.
- **"Fruit of the Poisonous Tree" Doctrine:** Evidence derived from an illegal search or confession is also excluded. If police illegally tap a phone, and the phone call leads them to a weapon, the weapon is also excluded.

**The Rationale:** The rule exists not to protect the guilty defendant, but to deter police misconduct. If illegally obtained evidence can never be used, police have no incentive to violate constitutional rights.

## Expert Witnesses and Scientific Evidence

Courts routinely need expert help to understand forensic, medical, financial, or engineering evidence that is beyond the knowledge of ordinary jurors.

An **expert witness** (unlike a fact witness) is allowed to state **opinions** — not just facts — based on their specialized knowledge.

**The Daubert Standard** (US, 1993): Before scientific testimony is admitted, the judge must assess whether the methodology is:
- Testable and subject to peer review.
- Based on a known error rate.
- Generally accepted within the scientific community.

This standard has been crucial in filtering out "junk science" — like some bite mark analysis and hair microscopy — from courtrooms.

## Privileged Communications: Protected Secrets

To protect vital societal relationships, the law establishes **privilege** — forbidding courts from forcing certain people to testify about private conversations:

- **Attorney-Client Privilege:** Conversations between a lawyer and client are protected. Clients must be able to speak honestly to receive effective legal defense.
- **Spousal Privilege:** Spouses generally cannot be forced to testify against each other, protecting family unity (though this varies by jurisdiction).
- **Doctor-Patient Privilege:** Medical discussions are protected to ensure patients seek treatment without fear of disclosure.
- **Clergy-Penitent Privilege:** Confessions to religious leaders are protected in most jurisdictions.
- **Journalist Privilege (Shield Laws):** Some states protect journalists from being forced to reveal their confidential sources.

## Case Study: OJ Simpson Trial (1995) — A Masterclass in Evidence

The criminal trial of former NFL star OJ Simpson for the murders of Nicole Brown Simpson and Ron Goldman is arguably the most famous evidence law demonstration in American history.

**Key Evidence Battles:**

- **The Bloody Glove:** A glove matching one found at the crime scene was discovered at Simpson's estate. Defense attorney Johnnie Cochran famously forced Simpson to try on the glove in front of the jury. It appeared not to fit. His closing line — *"If it doesn't fit, you must acquit"* — became iconic. Prosecutors argued the glove had shrunk from being soaked in blood and then stored in an evidence freezer.

- **DNA Evidence:** The prosecution presented DNA evidence from blood at the crime scene matching Simpson. The defense attacked the **chain of custody** — whether the blood samples had been properly collected, stored, and handled. Detective Mark Fuhrman was exposed as having given false testimony (denying use of a racial slur), severely damaging the prosecution's credibility.

- **The Exclusionary Rule in Action:** Defense attorneys argued that evidence collection procedures were flawed and that Fuhrman's documented racism cast doubt on whether evidence had been planted.

**The Verdict:** Simpson was acquitted in the criminal trial (the burden was *beyond a reasonable doubt*). He was later found liable in a civil suit (the lower *preponderance of evidence* standard).

**The Legacy:** The trial spotlighted racial bias in the criminal justice system, the power of defense attorneys to undermine evidence credibility, and the profound difference between criminal and civil standards of proof.

## Key Terms

- **Admissible:** Permitted to be introduced in court.
- **Relevance:** The quality of making a fact of consequence more or less probable.
- **Direct Evidence:** Proof that directly establishes a fact without inference.
- **Circumstantial Evidence:** Proof that requires inference to connect to a fact.
- **Hearsay:** Out-of-court statement offered to prove the truth of what it asserts.
- **Exclusionary Rule:** Doctrine barring illegally obtained evidence from court.
- **Fruit of the Poisonous Tree:** Evidence derived from an illegal act, also excluded.
- **Expert Witness:** A witness qualified to give opinion testimony in a specialized field.
- **Daubert Standard:** The judicial test for reliability of scientific expert testimony.
- **Privilege:** A legal right protecting private communications from courtroom disclosure.
