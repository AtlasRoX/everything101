# Intellectual Property 101

> *Intellectual property (IP) is a category of property that includes intangible creations of the human intellect, protected by law to encourage innovation and creativity.*

---

## The Big Picture

If you write a song, design a new medicine, or build a famous logo:
- How do you stop someone else from copying your file, manufacturing your pill, or using your logo to sell cheap knock-offs? 
- You cannot place a physical lock on a song or an idea.

To solve this, legal systems created **Intellectual Property (IP) Law**. 

IP law treats intangible creations like physical property. It grants creators a temporary **legal monopoly** over their ideas. This creates a vital economic trade-off: it protects the inventor's profits (encouraging them to invest time and money in R&D) while ensuring that eventually, the ideas enter the public domain for the benefit of all society.

This article introduces the four primary types of intellectual property and the concept of fair use.

---

## The Four Types of Intellectual Property

IP law is divided into four distinct legal protections:

| Type of IP | What It Protects | Duration | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Patent** | Inventions, utility designs, and novel technological processes. | **20 Years** from filing (cannot be renewed). | The chemical formula for a new pharmaceutical drug. |
| **Copyright** | Original creative works of authorship (literature, music, code, art). | **Life of author + 70 Years** (in the US/EU). | The text of a novel, a movie's video file, or code. |
| **Trademark** | Brand identifiers (logos, slogans, names, packaging shapes) used in trade. | **Indefinite** (provided it is actively used and defended). | The Nike Swoosh logo, the slogan *"Just Do It."* |
| **Trade Secret** | Confidential, proprietary business information that gives a competitive edge. | **Indefinite** (until it is leaked or reverse-engineered). | The secret recipe for Coca-Cola, or Google's search algorithm. |

---

## Patents: The Innovation Trade-off

To get a patent, your invention must be **novel** (new), **non-obvious** (not something a standard expert would easily think of), and **useful**.
- **The Trade-off:** The government grants you a 20-year monopoly. In exchange, **you must publish the exact blueprints of your invention** in the public patent registry. Anyone can read your design, but no one can sell it without your license. After 20 years, the patent expires, and the technology becomes free for anyone to use (which is how generic medicines are made).

---

## Copyright & The Fair Use Doctrine

Copyright is granted automatically the moment you write down a song, code, or book. 
- You do not need to register it to own it (though registration helps prove ownership in lawsuits).
- **Fair Use:** A legal exception allowing people to use copyrighted material without permission for specific purposes:
  - Criticism and commentary (e.g., movie reviews).
  - News reporting.
  - Teaching and classroom use.
  - Parody (e.g., comedy sketches).

---

## Key Terms

- **Intellectual Property (IP):** Intangible assets of human intellect.
- **Patent:** Legal monopoly protecting inventions.
- **Copyright:** Legal protection for original creative works.
- **Trademark:** Brand identifier protecting commercial names and logos.
- **Trade Secret:** Confined business information yielding a competitive edge.
- **Fair Use:** Legal exception permitting unauthorized use of copyrighted assets.
- **Infringement:** The unauthorized use or copying of protected IP.
