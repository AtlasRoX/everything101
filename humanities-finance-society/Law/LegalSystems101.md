# Legal Systems 101

> *A legal system is the framework of rules, procedures, and institutions a country uses to interpret and apply laws.*

---

## The Big Picture

If you are accused of a crime in London:
- You will be tried in a court where the judge acts as an objective referee, while two opposing lawyers present evidence before a jury of twelve everyday citizens. 
- The lawyers will argue about what judges decided in similar cases hundreds of years ago.

If you are accused of the same crime in Paris:
- You will be tried in a court where the judge actively questions you and leads the investigation to find the truth. 
- The lawyers will argue by citing specific, written paragraphs of the national Code.

These differences exist because the UK and France use two completely different **legal systems**. 

Most of the world's countries organize their laws around one of two major traditions: **Common Law** or **Civil Law**.

This article introduces these two dominant systems, the concept of precedent, and other minor legal traditions.

---

## Common Law vs. Civil Law

The global legal landscape is dominated by two traditions:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │  GLOBAL LEGAL TRADITIONS│
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                            ┌──────────────────┐
│    COMMON LAW    │                                            │    CIVIL LAW     │
│  (Anglo-American)│                                            │   (Continental)  │
│ - Judge-made law │                                            │ - Statutory codes│
│ - Precedent      │                                            │ - Active judges  │
│ - Adversarial    │                                            │ - Inquisitorial  │
└──────────────────┘                                            └──────────────────┘
```

### 1. Common Law (Anglo-American Tradition)
Originated in medieval England; used today in the UK, USA, Canada, Australia, and India.
- **Source of Law:** A mix of statutes (passed by parliament) and **case law** (judges' rulings on previous cases).
- **Precedent (*Stare Decisis*):** The rule that judges are bound to follow the decisions of previous judges in similar cases. (Ensures consistency and stability).
- **Adversarial System:** The trial is a contest between two opposing parties (prosecutor/plaintiff vs. defense), with the judge acting as a neutral referee.

### 2. Civil Law (Continental/Codified Tradition)
Originated in Roman Law and Napoleon's French Code; used today in Continental Europe, Latin America, and East Asia.
- **Source of Law:** Exclusively written **statutory codes** (comprehensive laws written by the legislature). Judges do not make law; they only apply the written code to the facts.
- **Precedent:** Past rulings are informative, but judges are not legally bound by them.
- **Inquisitorial System:** The judge plays an active role in the investigation, directing the questioning of witnesses and gathering evidence to find the truth.

---

## Other Legal Traditions

- **Religious Law:** Legal systems based on religious texts and scriptures. (e.g., Sharia Law in some Islamic nations, where religious codes govern family, contract, and criminal law).
- **Customary Law:** Systems based on long-established community customs and traditions, often unwritten, used to resolve local disputes. (Common in indigenous communities in Africa and the Pacific).
- **Mixed Systems:** Many countries combine elements of different systems. (e.g., South Africa mixes common law, civil law, and customary law).

---

## Key Terms

- **Legal System:** The institutional structure for creating and enforcing laws.
- **Common Law:** Law based on custom and judicial precedent rather than written statutes.
- **Civil Law:** Law based on written codes.
- **Precedent:** A past court decision used as an authority for deciding subsequent cases.
- **Stare Decisis:** The legal principle of determining points in litigation according to precedent.
- **Adversarial System:** A system where two advocates represent their parties' positions before an impartial referee.
- **Inquisitorial System:** A system where the court is actively involved in investigating the facts of the case.
