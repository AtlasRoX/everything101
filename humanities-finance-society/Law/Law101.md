# Law 101

> *Law is a system of rules created and enforced through social or governmental institutions to regulate behavior, maintain order, and resolve disputes.*

---

## The Big Picture

Why do we need laws? 
- If there were no laws, society would descend into what philosophers call the "state of nature"—a war of all against all, where might makes right and no one's life or property is secure.

Law is the social contract that makes civilization possible. It replaces physical force with peaceful, predictable rules. Instead of fighting a neighbor over a boundary line, we present evidence in a court. Instead of worrying that a merchant will take our money and run, we sign a contract.

At its core, law is about **order, predictability, and justice**.

This article introduces the fundamental schools of legal philosophy, the history of legal codes, the sources of law, and the core concept of the Rule of Law.

---

## Natural Law vs. Legal Positivism

For centuries, legal scholars have argued about where the authority of law comes from. They split into two main schools of thought:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │   LEGAL PHILOSOPHY      │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                            ┌──────────────────┐
│   NATURAL LAW    │                                            │ LEGAL POSITIVISM │
│ Moral Foundation │                                            │ State Authority  │
│ Laws must align  │                                            │ Laws are valid if│
│ with universal,  │                                            │ enacted by the   │
│ moral truths     │                                            │ proper authority │
└──────────────────┘                                            └──────────────────┘
```

### 1. Natural Law
The belief that laws are derived from universal moral principles (nature, reason, or God) that are inherent in human nature.
- **Core Idea:** An unjust law is not a true law (*Lex iniusta non est lex*). If a government passes a law permitting slavery or genocide, that law violates natural moral truths and is invalid, giving citizens a moral right—or even duty—to disobey it.
- **Historically supported by:** Thomas Aquinas, John Locke, and Martin Luther King Jr.

### 2. Legal Positivism
The belief that law is simply a set of rules enacted by a recognized sovereign political authority (the government).
- **Core Idea:** Law and morality are separate. A law is valid if it is passed using correct procedures by the proper authority, regardless of whether it is moral or fair. It must be obeyed until it is formally repealed or amended.
- **Historically supported by:** Thomas Hobbes, Jeremy Bentham, and H.L.A. Hart.

---

### Case Study: The Nuremberg Trials (1945–1946)

The clash between these two philosophies was famously put on trial after World War II:
- Nazi leaders accused of war crimes argued that they were merely following orders and laws that were validly enacted by the German state at the time (**Legal Positivism**). They argued that prosecuting them under new international rules was illegal retroactive punishment.
- The Allied prosecutors argued that some actions violate universal human rights so fundamentally that they are illegal regardless of what a state's written law says (**Natural Law**). The tribunal rejected the Nazi defense, establishing that individuals can be held accountable for "crimes against humanity" even if their actions were legal under domestic laws.

## Historical Legal Codes: The Path to Written Law

Before modern legislatures, laws were dictated by rulers. The transition to written, public codes was a massive victory for human rights:

- **The Code of Hammurabi (c. 1750 BCE):** One of the oldest deciphered writings of significant length in the world, carved onto a massive stone pillar in ancient Babylon. It established the principle of retributive justice (e.g., *"an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth"*) and made laws public so rulers could not change punishments on a whim.
- **Roman Law (Twelve Tables, c. 450 BCE):** The foundation of European legal thought. It established that laws must be written down and applied to all citizens (patrician and plebeian), introducing early concepts of property and civil disputes.
- **The Napoleonic Code (1804):** The French civil code established under Napoleon Bonaparte. It abolished feudal privileges, established the equality of all citizens before the law, and divided civil law into clear, written chapters of property, contract, and family law. It became the blueprint for civil legal systems worldwide.

## The Four Sources of Law

In modern democratic societies, laws are not created by a single king. They flow from **four distinct sources**:

1. **Constitutional Law:** The supreme law of the land. It defines the structure of government and sets the limits of state power. All other laws must align with the constitution. (See [Constitution101](./Constitution101.md)).
2. **Statutory Law:** Written laws passed by a legislative body (such as a parliament or congress). These are codified in statues (e.g., the penal code or tax code).
3. **Administrative (Regulatory) Law:** Detailed rules and regulations created by executive agencies to enforce statutes. (e.g., environmental protection rules written by the EPA).
4. **Common (Case) Law:** Laws established by judges through past court decisions (precedents) rather than written statutes. (See [LegalSystems101](./LegalSystems101.md)).

## The Rule of Law: The Supreme Principle

The most important concept in modern legal systems is the **Rule of Law**:

> **The Rule of Law:** The principle that all people, institutions, and entities—including the government and the rulers themselves—are accountable to laws that are publicly made, equally enforced, and independently adjudicated.

Key pillars of the Rule of Law:
- **No one is above the law:** Kings, presidents, and police officers must obey the same laws as ordinary citizens.
- **Laws must be public and clear:** You cannot be punished for a law that was kept secret or was written in a confusing way.
- **Due Process:** Any accusation must be resolved through a fair trial before an independent judge. (See [Courts101](./Courts101.md)).

## The Four Functions of Law

Law serves four primary functions in any society:

1. **Maintaining Order:** Establishing clear boundaries for acceptable behavior (e.g., traffic rules, criminal codes) to protect life and property.
2. **Dispute Resolution:** Providing peaceful, objective forums (courts) to resolve disagreements between citizens.
3. **Protecting Rights:** Shielding individual liberties from abuse by other citizens or the government. (See [HumanRights101](./HumanRights101.md)).
4. **Promoting the Common Good:** Directing public resources and regulations for health, safety, and infrastructure.

---

## Key Terms

- **Law:** A system of enforceable rules regulating social conduct.
- **Natural Law:** Legal philosophy linking validity to morality.
- **Legal Positivism:** Legal philosophy linking validity to sovereign enactment.
- **Rule of Law:** The principle that the law applies equally to everyone.
- **Due Process:** Fair treatment through the judicial system.
- **Codification:** The action or process of collecting and restating the law of a jurisdiction in a systematic, written code.
