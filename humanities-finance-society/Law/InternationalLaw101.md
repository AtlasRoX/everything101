# International Law 101

> *International law is the set of rules, norms, and standards generally accepted in relations between nations.*

---

## The Big Picture

Inside a country, if you commit a crime, the police will arrest you, a prosecutor will charge you, and a court will sentence you. There is a clear central authority with a monopoly on force.

But what happens in the global arena? 
- There is no world government. There is no global police force. 
- If one country invades another, or violates a trade treaty, who enforces the rules?

This is the challenge of **International Law**. 

International law operates in an environment of **anarchy** (the absence of a supreme global ruler). It relies on the consent of sovereign nations, bilateral treaties, customary practices, and international pressure to coordinate trade, maintain borders, protect human rights, and prevent war.

This article introduces state sovereignty, the sources of international law, the major global courts, and the problem of enforcement.

---

## State Sovereignty: The Starting Point

All international law is built on the concept of **State Sovereignty**:

> **Sovereignty:** The principle that a nation-state has absolute authority over its own territory and internal affairs, free from external interference, and that all states are legally equal on the global stage.

Because states are sovereign, **international law only applies to a state if that state agrees to it**. A nation cannot be forced to obey a treaty it did not sign, making international law fundamentally consensus-based.

## The Sources of International Law

According to the International Court of Justice, there are two primary sources of international rules:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │ SOURCES OF INT'L LAW    │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                            ┌──────────────────┐
│     TREATIES     │                                            │  CUSTOMARY LAW   │
│ (Bilateral/Multi)│                                            │ (State Practice) │
│ Written agreements│                                           │ Unwritten rules  │
│ signed by states │                                            │ nations follow   │
│ (e.g., Geneva)   │                                            │ out of obligation│
└──────────────────┘                                            └──────────────────┘
```

### 1. Treaties (Conventions/Accords)
Written contracts signed by nations that establish legal duties between them.
- **Bilateral:** Between two nations (e.g., a trade agreement).
- **Multilateral:** Between many nations (e.g., the Paris Climate Accord, or the Geneva Conventions governing war).

### 2. Customary International Law
Unwritten rules derived from a consistent practice of states over time, which they follow because they believe they are legally obligated to do so (*opinio juris*). (e.g., the immunity of foreign diplomats, or the prohibition of piracy on the high seas).

---

## International Courts

- **International Court of Justice (ICJ):** The official court of the United Nations. It resolves legal disputes **between countries** (e.g., border disputes). Nations must agree to let the ICJ hear their case.
- **International Criminal Court (ICC):** A permanent tribunal established in 2002 to prosecute **individuals** (not countries) for the worst global crimes: genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity.

---

## The Enforcement Problem

Why is international law often ignored?
- Without a global police force, enforcement relies on **self-help**:
  - **Sanctions:** Cutting off trade with a rogue state.
  - **Diplomatic Pressure:** Expelling ambassadors.
  - **Military Force:** The UN Security Council can authorize military action, but the five permanent members (US, UK, France, Russia, China) hold **veto power**, often paralyzing the system if one of them is involved in the dispute.

---

## Key Terms

- **International Law:** Rules governing interactions between sovereign states.
- **Sovereignty:** Ultimate authority within a territory.
- **Treaty:** A formal written agreement between states.
- **Customary Law:** Rules built on long-standing international practice.
- **ICJ:** International Court of Justice; resolves state-level disputes.
- **ICC:** International Criminal Court; prosecutes individual war criminals.
- **Sanction:** A penalty commercial or diplomatic barrier imposed on a nation.
