# International Law 101

> *International law is the set of rules, norms, and standards generally accepted in relations between nations.*

---

## The Big Picture

Inside a country, if you commit a crime, the police will arrest you, a prosecutor will charge you, and a court will sentence you. There is a clear central authority — a government — with a monopoly on legitimate force.

But what happens in the global arena?

- There is no world government. There is no global police force.
- If one country invades another, or violates a trade treaty, who enforces the rules?
- What happens when a multinational corporation poisons a river that flows through three countries?

This is the challenge of **International Law**.

International law operates in an environment of **anarchy** — not chaos, but the structural absence of a supreme global ruler. It relies on the voluntary consent of sovereign nations, bilateral treaties, customary practices, and international pressure to coordinate trade, maintain borders, protect human rights, regulate the use of force, and manage global commons like the ocean and outer space.

---

## A Brief History of International Law

- **Peace of Westphalia (1648):** Two treaties ending the Thirty Years' War established the modern system of sovereign nation-states — each with exclusive authority over its own territory and population. This is considered the birth of the Westphalian system that international law still rests on.
- **Hugo Grotius (1625):** Dutch lawyer Grotius, called the "Father of International Law," published *The Law of War and Peace*, arguing that natural law governed relations between nations even during armed conflict.
- **Concert of Europe (1815):** After the Napoleonic Wars, European great powers agreed to manage disputes through multilateral diplomacy — an early model of international governance.
- **Hague Conventions (1899, 1907):** The first multilateral treaties codifying the laws of war — prohibiting certain weapons, protecting prisoners, and establishing arbitration procedures.
- **League of Nations (1920):** Created after WWI to resolve disputes peacefully. Failed to prevent WWII due to the absence of the US, lack of enforcement power, and the rise of aggressive nationalism.
- **United Nations (1945):** Founded after WWII with the primary mission of preventing another world war. The UN Charter prohibits the use of force in international relations (with limited exceptions) and creates institutional machinery for peace, development, and human rights.
- **Globalization (1990s–present):** The end of the Cold War and the expansion of global trade created an explosion of international economic law — the WTO, NAFTA, bilateral investment treaties — alongside new challenges like climate change, cyberwarfare, and global pandemics that no single nation can solve alone.

## State Sovereignty: The Cornerstone

All international law is built on the concept of **State Sovereignty**:

> **Sovereignty:** The principle that a nation-state has absolute authority over its own territory and internal affairs, free from external interference, and that all states are legally equal on the global stage.

Because states are sovereign, international law only applies to a state **if that state agrees to it**. A nation cannot be bound by a treaty it has not signed. This makes international law fundamentally **consent-based** rather than imposed by a superior authority.

The tension at the heart of international law is that sovereignty protects both human rights (allowing countries to manage their own affairs) and enables their violation (shielding governments from accountability for how they treat their own people).

## The Sources of International Law

The Statute of the International Court of Justice identifies the primary sources of international legal rules:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │ SOURCES OF INT'L LAW    │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                            ┌──────────────────┐
│     TREATIES     │                                            │  CUSTOMARY LAW   │
│ (Bilateral/Multi)│                                            │ (State Practice) │
│ Written agreements│                                          │ Unwritten rules  │
│ signed by states │                                            │ nations follow   │
│ (e.g., Geneva)   │                                            │ out of obligation│
└──────────────────┘                                            └──────────────────┘
```

### 1. Treaties (Conventions, Accords, Agreements)
Written contracts signed by nations that establish binding legal duties between them.
- **Bilateral Treaties:** Between two nations (e.g., a trade agreement, an extradition treaty).
- **Multilateral Treaties:** Between many nations (e.g., the UN Charter, the Paris Climate Accord, the Geneva Conventions).

### 2. Customary International Law
Unwritten rules derived from a **consistent practice** of states over time, which they follow because they believe they are *legally* obligated to do so (*opinio juris*).
- *Examples:* The diplomatic immunity of foreign ambassadors; the prohibition of piracy on the high seas; the prohibition of torture even without a treaty.
- Even a state that never signed a specific treaty may be bound by customary law if the rule is truly universal.

### 3. General Principles of Law
Legal principles recognized by all major legal systems (e.g., "no one can be a judge in their own case," or the principle of good faith in treaty observance).

### 4. Judicial Decisions and Legal Scholarship
Decisions of international courts, and writings of leading international legal scholars, are persuasive (though not binding) authorities.

## The Major Branches of International Law

| Branch | Scope | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Public International Law** | Relations between states; law of treaties, war, diplomacy | The UN Charter; Geneva Conventions |
| **International Humanitarian Law (IHL)** | Rules governing the conduct of armed conflict | Protecting civilians and POWs in wartime |
| **International Human Rights Law** | Protecting individuals from state abuses | ICCPR, Convention Against Torture |
| **International Economic Law** | Trade, investment, and financial relations | WTO agreements; ICSID arbitration |
| **International Criminal Law** | Prosecuting individuals for the worst global crimes | ICC jurisdiction over genocide, war crimes |
| **Law of the Sea** | Rights over ocean waters and resources | UNCLOS: territorial seas, EEZ, high seas |
| **International Environmental Law** | Managing shared environmental resources | Paris Agreement on climate; Montreal Protocol on ozone |

## International Courts and Tribunals

| Court | Established | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| **ICJ (International Court of Justice)** | 1945, The Hague | Resolves legal disputes **between states** (territorial, treaty disputes). Jurisdiction requires state consent. |
| **ICC (International Criminal Court)** | 2002, The Hague | Prosecutes **individual people** for genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and aggression. |
| **WTO Dispute Settlement** | 1995 | Resolves trade disputes between member states under WTO law. |
| **ITLOS (International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea)** | 1996 | Resolves maritime boundary and ocean rights disputes. |
| **ECHR (European Court of Human Rights)** | 1959, Strasbourg | Hears human rights complaints against European states. Its rulings are binding on Council of Europe members. |

## The Enforcement Problem

Why is international law so often ignored by powerful states?

Without a global police force, enforcement relies on **mechanisms of pressure**:

- **Sanctions:** Cutting off trade, freezing assets, or banning financial transactions with a rogue state (e.g., sanctions on Russia, Iran, North Korea).
- **Diplomatic Pressure:** Expelling ambassadors, issuing condemnations, or mobilizing coalitions.
- **International Courts:** However, states must consent to ICJ jurisdiction, and the ICC cannot arrest anyone — it relies on member states to enforce arrest warrants.
- **UN Security Council:** Can authorize military action to enforce international law, but the **five permanent members (US, UK, France, Russia, China)** each hold **veto power** — making collective action impossible if one is the aggressor or a close ally of one.

**The realist critique:** Some scholars argue international law is simply politics by other means — powerful states obey it when convenient and ignore it when not. The historical record supports this view: powerful states have repeatedly violated international law (invading sovereign territories, ignoring ICJ rulings) without consequence.

**The institutionalist response:** Despite enforcement gaps, international institutions and norms genuinely shape state behavior. Countries comply with thousands of treaty obligations daily — on trade, aviation safety, postal services, and telecommunications — because long-term cooperation benefits outweigh short-term defection. The world is more rule-governed today than at any previous point in history.

## Case Study: Nicaragua v. United States (1986)

**The Facts:** During the 1980s, the United States covertly funded and trained the *Contras* — anti-Sandinista rebels fighting the government of Nicaragua — and mined Nicaraguan harbors. Nicaragua brought a case before the International Court of Justice, arguing these actions violated international law (the prohibition on the use of force and non-interference in other states' affairs).

**The ICJ Decision:** The court ruled **14-1 in favor of Nicaragua**. It found the US had violated customary international law by supporting the Contras and mining harbors. It ordered the US to pay reparations.

**The US Response:** The United States **refused to participate** in the proceedings after the court asserted jurisdiction, and **refused to comply** with the judgment. The US vetoed UN Security Council resolutions calling on states to comply with the ruling.

**The Legacy:** The case is the most famous illustration of the enforcement gap in international law. A powerful state simply walked away from an adverse ruling with no effective consequence. However, the case also established critical precedents about the customary law prohibition on interference in civil wars — precedents that continue to be cited in international legal arguments today.

The tension it exposes — between a rules-based international order and the hard reality of state power — is the central challenge international law continues to grapple with.

## Key Terms

- **International Law:** Rules governing interactions between sovereign states.
- **Sovereignty:** The supreme authority of a state within its own territory.
- **Treaty:** A formal, binding written agreement between states.
- **Customary International Law:** Unwritten rules built from consistent state practice and *opinio juris*.
- **ICJ:** International Court of Justice — settles disputes between states.
- **ICC:** International Criminal Court — prosecutes individuals for the worst global crimes.
- **Opinio Juris:** The belief that a practice is legally required (not just habitual).
- **UNSC Veto:** The power of the five permanent UN Security Council members to block any resolution.
- **Sanctions:** Economic and diplomatic penalties imposed on a state to enforce international norms.
- **Law of the Sea (UNCLOS):** The international treaty governing rights over ocean waters, seabeds, and resources.
