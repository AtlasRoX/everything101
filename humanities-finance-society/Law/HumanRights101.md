# Human Rights 101

> *Human rights are moral principles or norms for certain standards of human behavior and are protected as natural and legal rights in municipal and international law.*

---

## The Big Picture

Does a government have the right to silence its critics? Can a country legally make it a crime for a minority group to practice their religion?

Historically, under pure state sovereignty, a government could treat its citizens however it wanted within its own borders. (See [InternationalLaw101](./InternationalLaw101.md)). 

But after the atrocities of World War II, the international community said: **Never again**.

The concept of **Human Rights** was established to place limits on state power. Human rights are based on a simple, revolutionary premise: **you possess certain fundamental rights simply by virtue of being human**, regardless of your nationality, race, gender, or religion. These rights are inalienable; no government can morally take them away.

This article introduces the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the generations of rights, and the struggle to enforce them.

---

## The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)

Adopted by the United Nations General Assembly in **1948**, the **Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)** is the foundational document of modern rights law:
- It was drafted by representatives from all regions of the world under the leadership of Eleanor Roosevelt.
- It contains **30 Articles** listing the basic rights of all humans, serving as the blueprint for hundreds of national constitutions and international treaties.

## The Three Generations of Human Rights

Rights are generally grouped into three historical categories or "generations":

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │  GENERATIONS OF RIGHTS  │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                               ▼                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐            ┌──────────────────┐            ┌──────────────────┐
│ 1ST GEN: LIBERTY │            │2ND GEN: EQUALITY │            │ 3RD GEN: FRATERNITY│
│ Civil & Political│            │ Economic, Social,│            │ Collective &     │
│ - Free speech    │            │ & Cultural       │            │ solidarity rights│
│ - Fair trial     │            │ - Health, food   │            │ - Clean environ. │
│ - Vote           │            │ - Work, school   │            │ - Peace          │
└──────────────────┘            └──────────────────┘            └──────────────────┘
```

### 1. First-Generation Rights (Civil and Political)
Focused on **liberty** and protecting the individual from government abuse (negative rights).
- *Examples:* Freedom of speech, freedom of religion, the right to a fair trial, and the right to vote.

### 2. Second-Generation Rights (Economic, Social, and Cultural)
Focused on **equality** and ensuring citizens have the basic resources required to live a dignified life (positive rights).
- *Examples:* The right to education, the right to healthcare, the right to work under fair conditions, and the right to food and shelter.

### 3. Third-Generation Rights (Solidarity and Collective)
Focused on **brotherhood** and the rights of global communities (rather than individuals).
- *Examples:* The right to a clean environment, the right to peace, and the right to self-determination.

## Enforcing Human Rights: The Sovereignty Clash

The primary challenge of human rights is **enforcement**. 
- While the UDHR is a powerful moral statement, it is a *declaration*, not a legally binding treaty.
- Nations have signed binding treaties (like the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights), but if a sovereign nation violates the treaty internally, foreign intervention can spark conflicts.
- **The Tension:** The international community must constantly balance the principle of **state sovereignty** (non-interference in local affairs) against the moral duty of **humanitarian intervention** (stopping genocide or mass rights abuses).

---

## Key Terms

- **Human Rights:** Fundamental moral rights belonging to all human beings.
- **UDHR:** Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948).
- **Inalienable:** Unable to be taken away or given up.
- **First-Generation Rights:** Civil and political liberties.
- **Second-Generation Rights:** Economic and social welfare rights.
- **Third-Generation Rights:** Collective community rights (e.g., environment).
