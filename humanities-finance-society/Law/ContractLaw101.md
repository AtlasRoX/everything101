# Contract Law 101

> *A contract is a legally binding agreement between two or more parties that creates mutual obligations enforceable by law.*

---

## The Big Picture

We enter into contracts constantly:
- When you buy a coffee, click "I agree" on software terms, or hire a contractor to paint your house, you are creating a contract.

A contract is the fundamental unit of commerce. Without contract law, trade would collapse. If you paid a builder to build a house, but they walked away with your cash, you would have no recourse. Contract law ensures that promises are kept, or, if broken, that the injured party is made whole.

This article introduces the five essential elements that make a contract legally binding, what constitutes a breach, and the remedies courts use to fix them.

---

## The Five Elements of a Binding Contract

For a contract to be enforceable in court, it must contain **five key elements**:

```
                       ┌─────────────────────────┐
                       │   CONTRACT INGREDIENTS  │
                       └────────────┬────────────┘
                                    │
         ┌──────────────┬───────────┴───────────┬──────────────┐
         ▼              ▼                       ▼              ▼
┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐
│  OFFER (1)     ││ ACCEPTANCE (2) ││ CONSIDERATION  ││ CAPACITY (4)   │
│ Proposal with  ││ Assent to all  ││   (3)          ││ Sound mind,   │
│ clear terms    ││ terms without  ││ Exchange of    ││ legal age, not │
│                ││ changes        ││ value (money)  ││ under influence│
└────────────────┘└────────────────┘└────────────────┘└────────────────┘
```
*(Plus **Legality (5)**: The contract's purpose must be legal. A contract to sell illegal drugs is void).*

1. **Offer:** A clear, specific proposal by one party to enter into an agreement. (e.g., *"I will paint your house for $1,000"*).
2. **Acceptance:** The unconditional agreement of the other party to the exact terms of the offer. (If they say, *"I agree, but only if you paint it blue,"* this is a counteroffer, not an acceptance).
3. **Consideration:** The exchange of value. Each party must give up something of value to get something else. (e.g., you get a painted house, the painter gets $1,000). A one-sided promise (e.g., *"I will give you a car next week for free"*) is a gift, not a contract, and cannot be enforced in court.
4. **Capacity:** The parties must be legally capable of entering an agreement. (Minors, severely intoxicated individuals, or those with cognitive disabilities lack capacity, making the contract voidable).
5. **Legality:** The contract's purpose must be legal. Courts will not enforce a contract to commit a crime (e.g., hiring a hitman).

## The Statute of Frauds: Does it have to be in writing?

A common myth is that verbal contracts are invalid. In reality, **verbal contracts are fully binding in most cases**. 

However, they are difficult to prove in court (a "he said, she said" scenario).

To prevent fraud, the law uses the **Statute of Frauds**, requiring specific contracts to be **written and signed** to be enforceable:
- Contracts for the sale of **Real Estate**.
- Contracts that cannot be completed within **one year**.
- Contracts to pay another person's debt.
- Sales of goods valued over **$500**.

---

## Breach and Remedies

When one party fails to fulfill their contract promise, they commit a **breach of contract**. The court can order several remedies:

- **Expectation Damages (Financial):** The most common remedy. The court orders the breaching party to pay money to put the victim in the position they *would have* been in if the contract had been completed.
- **Specific Performance (Action):** The court orders the breaching party to actually perform their promise. (Rarely used, except for unique assets like buying a specific piece of historic land or artwork, where money cannot buy a substitute).

---

## Key Terms

- **Contract:** A legally binding agreement.
- **Consideration:** The value exchanged in a contract (money, service, goods).
- **Capacity:** Legal competence to enter a contract.
- **Statute of Frauds:** Law requiring specific contracts to be written down.
- **Breach:** Failure to perform a contract obligation.
- **Expectation Damages:** Financial compensation for a breached contract.
- **Specific Performance:** A court order to fulfill a contract's exact action.
