# Contract Law 101

> *A contract is a legally binding agreement between two or more parties that creates mutual obligations enforceable by law.*

---

## The Big Picture

We enter into contracts constantly. When you buy a coffee, click "I agree" on software terms, or hire a contractor to paint your house, you are creating a contract.

A contract is the fundamental unit of commerce. Without contract law, trade would collapse. If you paid a builder to build a house, but they walked away with your cash, you would have no recourse. Contract law ensures that promises are kept, or, if broken, that the injured party is made whole.

This article introduces the five essential elements that make a contract legally binding, the types of contracts, what constitutes a breach, common defenses, and the remedies courts use to fix broken promises.

---

## A Brief History of Contract Law

The modern law of contracts has ancient roots.

- **Ancient Rome:** Roman law recognized *stipulatio* — a formal oral promise that was legally binding — as the basis of commercial exchange. The Roman courts enforced obligations between merchants trading across the Mediterranean.
- **Medieval England:** By the 12th century, English common law courts developed the action of *assumpsit*, allowing parties to sue for broken promises that caused financial harm.
- **17th–18th Century:** The concept of "freedom of contract" emerged during the Enlightenment — the idea that rational individuals should be free to make binding agreements on their own terms, without government interference.
- **Industrial Revolution:** Mass commerce created the need for standardized contract rules. Courts began refining doctrines like consideration, offer, acceptance, and promissory estoppel.
- **20th Century:** Consumer protection laws began limiting absolute freedom of contract. Courts invalidated "unconscionable" contracts that exploited vulnerable parties (e.g., payday loan traps).

Today, contract law is primarily **common law** in English-speaking countries, supplemented by the **Uniform Commercial Code (UCC)** in the United States for transactions involving goods.

## The Five Elements of a Binding Contract

For a contract to be enforceable in court, it must contain **five key elements**:

```
                       ┌─────────────────────────┐
                       │   CONTRACT INGREDIENTS  │
                       └────────────┬────────────┘
                                    │
         ┌──────────────┬───────────┴───────────┬──────────────┐
         ▼              ▼                       ▼              ▼
┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐┌────────────────┐
│  OFFER (1)     ││ ACCEPTANCE (2) ││ CONSIDERATION  ││ CAPACITY (4)   │
│ Proposal with  ││ Assent to all  ││   (3)          ││ Sound mind,    │
│ clear terms    ││ terms without  ││ Exchange of    ││ legal age, not │
│                ││ changes        ││ value (money)  ││ under influence│
└────────────────┘└────────────────┘└────────────────┘└────────────────┘
```
*(Plus **Legality (5)**: The contract's purpose must be legal. A contract to sell illegal drugs is void).*

1. **Offer:** A clear, specific proposal by one party to enter into an agreement. (e.g., *"I will paint your house for $1,000"*).
2. **Acceptance:** The unconditional agreement of the other party to the exact terms of the offer. If they say, *"I agree, but only if you paint it blue,"* this is a **counteroffer**, not an acceptance — it extinguishes the original offer.
3. **Consideration:** The exchange of value. Each party must give up something of value to get something else. (e.g., you get a painted house, the painter gets $1,000). A one-sided promise (*"I will give you a car next week for free"*) is a **gift**, not a contract.
4. **Capacity:** The parties must be legally capable of entering an agreement. Minors, severely intoxicated individuals, or those with serious cognitive disabilities lack capacity, making the contract **voidable** at their option.
5. **Legality:** The contract's purpose must be legal. Courts will not enforce a contract to commit a crime.

## Types of Contracts

Not all contracts look the same. They vary in form, formality, and context:

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Express** | Terms are stated explicitly (oral or written) | A written lease agreement |
| **Implied** | Terms are inferred from conduct or circumstances | Sitting in a barber's chair implies you will pay for the haircut |
| **Bilateral** | Both parties make promises to each other | "I'll pay you $500 if you fix my roof" |
| **Unilateral** | One party promises in exchange for a specific act | A lost pet reward posted on a flyer |
| **Adhesion** | A "take it or leave it" contract drafted by the stronger party | Software End User License Agreements (EULAs) |
| **Void** | A contract with no legal force from the start | A contract for illegal services |
| **Voidable** | A contract that one party can cancel (e.g., due to incapacity or fraud) | A minor's contract |

## The Statute of Frauds: Does it Have to be in Writing?

A common myth is that verbal contracts are invalid. In reality, **verbal contracts are fully binding in most cases**. However, they are difficult to prove in court ("he said, she said").

To prevent fraud, the law uses the **Statute of Frauds**, which requires specific contracts to be **written and signed** to be enforceable:

- Contracts for the sale of **Real Estate**.
- Contracts that cannot be completed within **one year**.
- Contracts to pay another person's debt (guarantees).
- Sales of goods valued over **$500** (under the UCC).
- Contracts related to marriage (prenuptial agreements).

> **Why "Statute of Frauds"?** The name comes from the English *Statute of Frauds (1677)*, originally passed to prevent witnesses and parties from fabricating contract terms in court.

## Defenses Against Contract Enforcement

Even if a contract has all five elements, certain circumstances allow a party to escape enforcement:

- **Duress:** One party was forced to sign under threat of harm.
- **Undue Influence:** An authority figure (e.g., a caregiver over an elderly person) exploited their position to pressure signing.
- **Misrepresentation / Fraud:** One party lied about a material fact. If you sold a car knowing the engine was broken but said it was "perfect," the buyer can void the contract.
- **Mistake:** Both parties operated on a shared false assumption. If you both believed the painting was an original Picasso but it was a copy, the contract may be voidable.
- **Unconscionability:** A contract so one-sided and unfair that no reasonable person would have agreed to it. Courts can refuse to enforce predatory contracts.
- **Impossibility / Frustration of Purpose:** A contract to perform at a concert becomes impossible if the venue burns down.

## Breach and Remedies

When one party fails to fulfill their contract promise, they commit a **breach of contract**. Breaches range in severity:

- **Minor Breach (Partial):** One party mostly performs but falls slightly short. (e.g., a contractor finishes a home renovation 2 days late). The other party can sue for the resulting damages but cannot walk away from the contract.
- **Material Breach:** A failure so serious it defeats the entire purpose of the contract. The innocent party can terminate the contract and sue for full damages.
- **Anticipatory Breach:** Before the performance date, one party declares they will not perform. The innocent party does not need to wait — they can sue immediately.

### Remedies Available to the Injured Party

| Remedy | Description |
|---|---|
| **Expectation Damages** | Money to put the victim in the position they *would have been* in if the contract had been performed |
| **Consequential Damages** | Compensation for foreseeable indirect losses caused by the breach |
| **Nominal Damages** | A small symbolic award when a breach occurred but caused no real financial harm |
| **Restitution** | The breaching party must return any benefit they received (to prevent unjust enrichment) |
| **Specific Performance** | A court order to actually perform the promise (used only for unique items — a specific piece of land or artwork) |
| **Liquidated Damages** | A pre-agreed amount written into the contract for the event of a breach |

## Case Study: Hadley v. Baxendale (1854)

This landmark English case established **the rule of foreseeability** in contract damages — one of the most important principles in contract law worldwide.

**The Facts:** The Hadleys owned a mill. A crankshaft broke, halting their entire operation. They hired Baxendale's delivery company to transport the broken shaft to a manufacturer so a new one could be made. Baxendale promised delivery in one day, but was negligent and took several days. During that time, the mill stood idle, losing significant profit.

**The Dispute:** Hadley sued for the full profits lost during the shutdown — a large sum.

**The Decision:** The court ruled against Hadley on the full amount. The reasoning was that Baxendale had no reason to know the mill would be completely shut down just because the shaft was late. Consequential damages are only recoverable if they were **reasonably foreseeable** at the time the contract was made. Since Hadley did not tell Baxendale how urgent the shipment was, those special damages could not be claimed.

**The Legacy:** This single ruling, from 1854, still governs how courts in the US, UK, Canada, and Australia calculate contract damages today. When negotiating important contracts, parties are now advised to explicitly notify the other side of the potential consequences of breach — establishing foreseeability on the record.

## Key Terms

- **Contract:** A legally binding agreement.
- **Offer:** A specific proposal to enter into an agreement.
- **Acceptance:** Unconditional agreement to all terms.
- **Consideration:** The value exchanged in a contract.
- **Capacity:** Legal competence to enter a contract.
- **Statute of Frauds:** Law requiring specific contracts to be written.
- **Breach:** Failure to perform a contract obligation.
- **Expectation Damages:** Financial compensation for a breached contract.
- **Specific Performance:** A court order to fulfill a contract's exact action.
- **Anticipatory Breach:** A declaration before due date that a party will not perform.
- **Unconscionability:** A contract so unfair that courts refuse to enforce it.
