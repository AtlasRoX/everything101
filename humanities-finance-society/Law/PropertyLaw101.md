# Property Law 101

> *Property law is the area of law governing the various forms of ownership and tenancy in real property and in personal property.*

---

## The Big Picture

What does it mean to "own" something? 
- If you own a laptop, you have the right to use it, let a friend borrow it, sell it for profit, or smash it with a hammer. 
- But if you own a house, you do not have unlimited rights. You cannot build a twenty-story tower that blocks your neighbors' sunlight, and you cannot run a noisy factory in a residential zone.

Property law is not just about physical things; it is about **rights**. It defines a "bundle of rights" between people regarding things. It establishes who has the right to use a resource, who can exclude others from using it, and how those rights can be sold or transferred.

This article introduces real vs. personal property, the difference between ownership and possession, and limitations on property rights like easements and zoning.

---

## Real Property vs. Personal Property

Property is divided into two primary categories:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │    TYPES OF PROPERTY    │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                            ┌──────────────────┐
│  REAL PROPERTY   │                                            │PERSONAL PROPERTY │
│ (Immovable)      │                                            │ (Movable/Chattel)│
│ - Land           │                                            │ - Tangible       │
│ - Buildings      │                                            │   (Cars, books)  │
│ - Fixtures (pipes)│                                           │ - Intangible     │
│                  │                                            │   (Stocks, IP)   │
└──────────────────┘                                            └──────────────────┘
```

### 1. Real Property (Real Estate)
Land and anything permanently attached to it (buildings, trees, underground mineral rights, and built fixtures like plumbing).
- **Nature:** Immovable. Ownership is recorded in public land registries.

### 2. Personal Property (Chattel)
All other property that is movable.
- **Tangible Personal Property:** Physical items like your car, clothes, furniture, or smartphones.
- **Intangible Personal Property:** Assets that have no physical shape but hold value, such as shares of stock, bank balances, patents, and copyrights. (See [IntellectualProperty101](./IntellectualProperty101.md)).

## Ownership vs. Possession: The Split

You can possess something without owning it, and you can own something without possessing it:

- **Ownership (Title):** The legal right to possess, use, enjoy, and transfer an asset.
- **Possession:** The physical control of an asset.
- *Example:* If you lease an apartment, the landlord retains **ownership** (they hold the title), but you have the legal right of **possession** (you occupy the space, and the landlord cannot enter without notice).

---

## Limits on Property: Easements & Zoning

Property rights are not absolute. Governments and neighbors can limit your rights through:

- **Easements:** A legal right to use another person's land for a specific, limited purpose. (e.g., a utility company holding an easement to run power lines across your backyard, or a neighbor holding a right-of-way easement to drive across your driveway to reach their house).
- **Zoning Laws:** Government regulations designating how land can be used. (e.g., dividing a city into residential, commercial, and industrial zones to keep noisy factories away from homes).
- **Eminent Domain (Condemnation):** The power of the government to take private property for public use (like building a highway), provided they pay the owner "just compensation."

---

## Key Terms

- **Property Law:** The rules governing asset ownership rights.
- **Real Property:** Land and permanent structures.
- **Personal Property:** Movable assets (chattel).
- **Easement:** The right to use another's land for a specific purpose.
- **Zoning:** Municipal rules dividing land by permitted use.
- **Eminent Domain:** The government's right to acquire private property for public projects in exchange for payment.
