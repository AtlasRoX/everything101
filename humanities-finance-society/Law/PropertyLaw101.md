# Property Law 101

> *Property law is the area of law governing the various forms of ownership and tenancy in real property and in personal property.*

---

## The Big Picture

What does it mean to "own" something?

- If you own a laptop, you have the right to use it, let a friend borrow it, sell it for profit, or smash it with a hammer.
- But if you own a house, you do not have unlimited rights. You cannot build a twenty-story tower that blocks your neighbors' sunlight, and you cannot run a noisy factory in a residential zone.

Property law is not just about physical things — it is about **rights**. Philosophers describe ownership as a "bundle of rights" between people with respect to things. It establishes who has the right to **use** a resource, who can **exclude** others, and how those rights can be **transferred**.

Property law sits at the foundation of every economy. Without clear, enforceable ownership rights, markets for land, goods, and intellectual creations cannot function. Investors cannot build factories on uncertain land, farmers cannot secure loans against their fields, and creators cannot profit from their inventions.

This article introduces the key categories of property, the different forms of ownership, landlord-tenant relationships, and the government's power to limit private property rights.

## A Brief History of Property Rights

The concept of private property is ancient but contested.

- **Ancient Societies:** Land was often communally owned by tribes or clans. Individual private property as a dominant concept emerged gradually with settled agriculture.
- **Roman Law:** The Romans developed sophisticated concepts of *dominium* (absolute ownership) and *possessio* (physical possession) — distinctions that persist in modern law.
- **Feudal England:** Land was held by lords and granted to vassals in exchange for military service. True private ownership of land by ordinary people was severely limited.
- **Magna Carta (1215):** Established that even the king could not seize property from freemen without due process — a foundational protection for property rights.
- **John Locke (1689):** In his *Two Treatises of Government*, Locke argued that property rights arise from **labor** — a person who mixes their labor with unowned land acquires a property right in it. This "labor theory" profoundly influenced the American founding.
- **Industrial Revolution:** Mass urbanization created urgent needs for landlord-tenant law, zoning regulations, and property registration systems.
- **20th Century:** Environmental laws and social welfare considerations have progressively limited absolute property rights, requiring landowners to account for impacts on neighbors and the public.

## Real Property vs. Personal Property

Property is divided into two primary categories:

```
                            ┌─────────────────────────┐
                            │    TYPES OF PROPERTY    │
                            └────────────┬────────────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                                               ▼
┌──────────────────┐                                            ┌──────────────────┐
│  REAL PROPERTY   │                                            │PERSONAL PROPERTY │
│ (Immovable)      │                                            │ (Movable/Chattel)│
│ - Land           │                                            │ - Tangible       │
│ - Buildings      │                                            │   (Cars, books)  │
│ - Fixtures (pipes)│                                          │ - Intangible     │
│                  │                                            │   (Stocks, IP)   │
└──────────────────┘                                            └──────────────────┘
```

### 1. Real Property (Real Estate)
Land and anything permanently attached to it: buildings, trees, underground mineral rights, and built fixtures like plumbing and installed cabinetry.
- Ownership is recorded in public **land registries** or title systems.
- Transfer requires a formal deed, signed and often notarized.

### 2. Personal Property (Chattel)
All other property that is movable.
- **Tangible Personal Property:** Physical items — cars, clothes, furniture, electronics.
- **Intangible Personal Property:** Assets with no physical form but real value, such as shares of stock, bank account balances, patents, and copyrights. (See [IntellectualProperty101](./IntellectualProperty101.md)).

## Forms of Ownership

When multiple people share ownership of real property, the law recognizes several distinct arrangements:

| Form | Description | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Sole Ownership** | One person holds the entire title | Complete control; passes to estate on death |
| **Joint Tenancy** | Two or more owners hold equal shares with the **right of survivorship** | If one owner dies, their share automatically passes to the other owners — not through a will |
| **Tenancy in Common** | Two or more owners hold shares (can be unequal) with **no** right of survivorship | Each share is inheritable and can be sold independently |
| **Community Property** | Spouses own all marital assets equally (used in ~9 US states) | On divorce or death, each spouse is entitled to 50% |

## Ownership vs. Possession: The Split

You can possess something without owning it, and you can own something without possessing it:

- **Ownership (Title):** The legal right to possess, use, enjoy, and transfer an asset.
- **Possession:** The physical control of an asset.

*Example:* If you lease an apartment, the landlord retains **ownership** (holding the title), but you have the legal right of **possession** — the landlord cannot enter without giving proper notice.

### Adverse Possession ("Squatter's Rights")
In most jurisdictions, a trespasser who occupies someone else's land openly, continuously, and without the owner's permission for a statutory period (often 10–20 years) can actually acquire **legal title** to the land.

This doctrine encourages owners to actively monitor and use their property. Courts reason that a sleeping owner who ignores encroachment for decades has, in effect, abandoned their claim.

## Landlord-Tenant Law

When an owner of real property (the **landlord**) rents it to another person (the **tenant**), a specialized body of law governs the relationship:

- **Lease Agreement:** A contract setting out the rental period, price, and rules. Most residential leases run month-to-month or for a fixed term (e.g., one year).
- **Security Deposit:** Money held by the landlord to cover damage beyond normal wear and tear. Most states cap the amount and require return within a set number of days after the tenant vacates.
- **Implied Warranty of Habitability:** A legal guarantee that the landlord maintains the property in a livable condition — with working heat, water, and structural safety. A tenant may legally withhold rent if the landlord fails to meet this standard.
- **Constructive Eviction:** If a landlord's negligence makes a property uninhabitable, the tenant may legally break the lease and move out without penalty.
- **Eviction (Unlawful Detainer):** A formal court process the landlord must follow to remove a tenant who fails to pay rent or violates the lease. A landlord cannot simply change the locks or remove belongings — this is illegal "self-help eviction."

## Limits on Property Rights

Property rights are **not absolute**. Governments and neighbors can impose significant limitations:

### Easements
A legal right to use another person's land for a specific, limited purpose.
- *Example 1:* A utility company holds an easement to run power lines across your backyard.
- *Example 2:* A neighbor holds a **right-of-way easement** to drive across your driveway to reach their landlocked property.

Easements typically "run with the land" — they remain attached to the property even if it is sold to a new owner.

### Zoning Laws
Government regulations designating how land can be used. Municipal governments divide land into zones:
- **Residential (R):** Single-family homes, apartments.
- **Commercial (C):** Shops, offices, restaurants.
- **Industrial (I):** Factories, warehouses.
- **Mixed-Use:** A combination of residential and commercial.

### Eminent Domain (Condemnation)
The power of government to **take private property for public use**, provided the owner receives "just compensation" (fair market value).
- Public uses include highways, schools, parks, and utility lines.
- The controversial 2005 US Supreme Court decision *Kelo v. City of New London* extended this to allow government takings for private economic development projects, sparking significant public outcry and legislative reform in many states.

---

## Case Study: Kelo v. City of New London (2005)

**The Facts:** New London, Connecticut, approved a development plan to allow private companies — including Pfizer — to build a research campus on land adjacent to its waterfront. To do so, the city used its eminent domain power to forcibly acquire 115 privately-owned homes, including the small home of Susette Kelo, who had lived there for years.

**The Legal Question:** Can the government use eminent domain to transfer land from private homeowners to private developers for the purpose of economic development (i.e., to create jobs and increase tax revenue)? Does this count as a "public use"?

**The Decision (5-4):** The Supreme Court held that economic development that would benefit the public — by increasing tax revenue and creating jobs — was a legitimate "public use" under the Fifth Amendment. The city's taking was constitutional.

**The Reaction:** The decision was one of the most controversial Supreme Court rulings in recent history. Critics across the political spectrum argued it allowed wealthy corporations and city planners to bulldoze poor and middle-class homeowners for private profit.

**The Legacy:** Within years, **over 40 US states** passed laws restricting the use of eminent domain for private economic development. Ironically, the specific New London development never succeeded — Pfizer ultimately closed its facility, and the land sat largely empty for years.

The case is a vivid illustration of the tension between individual property rights and the government's power to plan for the public good.

## Key Terms

- **Property Law:** The rules governing asset ownership rights.
- **Real Property:** Land and permanent structures attached to it.
- **Personal Property:** Movable assets (chattel).
- **Easement:** The right to use another's land for a specific purpose.
- **Zoning:** Municipal rules dividing land by permitted use category.
- **Eminent Domain:** Government's power to acquire private property for public use with fair compensation.
- **Adverse Possession:** Acquiring title to land by long-term open occupancy.
- **Joint Tenancy:** Co-ownership with a right of survivorship.
- **Tenancy in Common:** Co-ownership without a right of survivorship.
- **Implied Warranty of Habitability:** A landlord's legal duty to maintain livable rental conditions.
