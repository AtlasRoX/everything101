# Cyber Law 101

> *Cyber law is the branch of law that deals with legal issues related to the internet, computer networks, software, and digital information systems.*

---

## The Big Picture

The internet was designed to be a borderless, decentralized network. 
- But what happens when a hacker in Russia steals data from a company in California and sells it to a buyer in Germany? Which country's laws apply? 
- What happens when a user posts defamatory lies about someone on a social media site? Can you sue the platform, or only the user?
- How do you sign a contract online if there is no physical paper or pen?

These are the challenges of **Cyber Law**. 

Because traditional legal systems are based on physical boundaries (nations, states, counties), the internet has forced a major rewrite of legal principles, creating new rules for digital contracts, data privacy, cybercrime, and platform liability.

This article introduces digital contracts, data privacy laws, Section 230 platform liability, and cybercrime.

---

## Digital Contracts: Clickwrap and Shrinkwrap

In the physical world, you sign a contract with a pen. In the digital world, contracts are created through user clicks:

- **Clickwrap Agreements:** The standard "I Agree" button. You are shown the terms and conditions, and you must click a box to proceed. Courts consistently enforce clickwrap agreements, assuming you had the opportunity to read the terms, even if you clicked past them in seconds.
- **Browsewrap Agreements:** The website states that by simply browsing or using the site, you automatically agree to their terms of service (often linked at the bottom of the page). Courts rarely enforce browsewrap agreements unless the site can prove the user had actual notice of the terms.

## Data Privacy Laws: GDPR and Beyond

Who owns your digital data? Your search history, location logs, and shopping habits are valuable assets. Modern cyber law protects this through strict data privacy regulations:

- **GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation):** The European Union's landmark privacy law. It applies to *any* company in the world that targets or collects data from EU citizens. It mandates:
  - **The Right to be Forgotten:** You can demand a company delete all your personal data.
  - **Data Portability:** You can download your data and transfer it to a competitor.
  - **Consent:** Clear, active consent is required to track cookies or share data.

---

## Section 230: The Law That Built the Modern Internet

In the United States, a 26-word sentence in the Communications Decency Act of 1996 established the foundation of the modern web:

> **Section 230:** *"No provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider."*

- **What it means:** Social media platforms (like Facebook, X, or YouTube) are not legally liable for the posts, videos, or comments uploaded by their users. If a user posts a defamatory lie on Facebook, you can sue the user, but you cannot sue Facebook.
- **The Contrast:** If a newspaper publishes a defamatory letter to the editor, the newspaper *is* liable because they act as a "publisher" who edits and approves the content.

## Cybercrime and Jurisdiction

Cybercrime includes hacking, identity theft, malware distribution, and DDoS attacks. 
- **The Jurisdictional Challenge:** The primary problem is enforcement. If a cybercriminal is located in a country without an extradition treaty (like Russia or North China), local police cannot arrest them, making international cooperation and cybersecurity defense vital.

---

## Key Terms

- **Cyber Law:** The legal framework governing internet and digital technologies.
- **Clickwrap:** A digital contract accepted by clicking an "I Agree" button.
- **GDPR:** The EU data privacy framework protecting user data rights.
- **Section 230:** U.S. law shielding platforms from liability for user-generated content.
- **Jurisdiction:** The geographical area over which a court has legal authority.
- **Extradition:** The formal process of one country surrendering an accused criminal to another.
