# Brain 101

> *A 1.4-kg lump of fat and protein that somehow produces every thought, feeling, memory, dream, and decision you've ever had.*

---

## The Big Picture

Your brain contains roughly **86 billion neurons** (nerve cells). Each neuron connects to thousands of others through synapses. The total number of connections is estimated at **100 trillion** — more than all the stars in the Milky Way.

This three-pound organ:
- Processes 11 million bits of sensory information per second (while making you conscious of only about 50)
- Runs continuously for your entire life with no scheduled maintenance
- Consumes 20% of your body's energy while making up only 2% of your body weight
- Generates your entire subjective experience of reality

Understanding the brain is arguably the most important scientific frontier humanity has ever faced — because the brain is both the tool and the object of study.

## Brain Architecture: The Big Regions

The brain isn't one uniform mass — it's a collection of specialized regions that evolved at different times and do very different things.

### The Three-Layer Model (Triune Brain)

Paul MacLean's classic model (though simplified) is a useful starting framework:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│     NEOCORTEX (Thinking Brain)      │  ← Planning, language, reason
│  ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─   │
│    LIMBIC SYSTEM (Emotional Brain)  │  ← Emotions, memory, survival
│  ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─   │
│    BRAINSTEM (Reptilian Brain)      │  ← Breathing, heart rate, reflexes
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Note:** Neuroscientists know this model is oversimplified — the brain doesn't divide neatly into three separate "minds." But it's a useful map for beginners.

## The Major Brain Regions

### The Brainstem
**What it does:** Controls the body's automatic functions — breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, sleep cycles.

**Think of it as:** The autopilot. You don't consciously breathe. The brainstem handles it.

Damage to the brainstem is almost immediately fatal. It's evolutionarily ancient — fish have brainstems.

### The Cerebellum ("Little Brain")
**Location:** The golf-ball-sized structure at the back/bottom of the brain.

**What it does:** Coordinates movement and balance. Fine motor control — playing piano, catching a ball, walking without falling.

**The surprise finding:** The cerebellum also seems to play a role in cognition and emotion — it's more than just a motor controller.

### The Limbic System (The Emotional Core)

The limbic system is a set of interconnected structures critical for emotion, memory, and motivation:

**The Amygdala**
- **Function:** Fear, threat detection, emotional memory
- **Size:** About the size of an almond (Greek: *amygdalē*)
- **The fear response:** When you see something threatening, the amygdala fires before your conscious mind registers the threat — producing a fear reaction you only understand afterward
- **In depression and anxiety:** The amygdala is overactive, making ordinary situations feel threatening

**The Hippocampus**
- **Function:** Formation and retrieval of memories; spatial navigation
- **Critical insight:** The hippocampus doesn't *store* long-term memories permanently — it consolidates them and distributes them across the cortex. Sleep is when this consolidation happens most intensely.
- **In depression:** The hippocampus actually *shrinks* under chronic stress (measurable on MRI) — this is why severe depression impairs memory

**The Hypothalamus**
- **Function:** Regulates hunger, thirst, temperature, sleep, and the hormonal system
- **Key output:** Controls the **pituitary gland** — the "master gland" that regulates all other hormone-producing glands

**The Cingulate Cortex**
- **Function:** Error detection, emotional regulation, decision-making under uncertainty
- **The anterior cingulate** is particularly active when you experience social pain (rejection, exclusion) — the same region that activates for physical pain

### The Cerebral Cortex (The Thinking Brain)

The cortex is the wrinkled outer layer of the brain — if you unfolded it, it would cover a desktop. The wrinkling (called **gyri** and **sulci**) is how evolution fit more surface area into a skull.

The cortex is divided into **four lobes**:

| Lobe | Location | Main Functions |
|---|---|---|
| **Frontal** | Behind the forehead | Planning, decision-making, personality, language production, impulse control |
| **Parietal** | Top/back | Spatial awareness, touch, body position |
| **Temporal** | Sides | Hearing, language comprehension, face recognition, long-term memory |
| **Occipital** | Back | Visual processing |

**The Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)** — the very front of the frontal lobe — is the most "human" part of the brain. It handles:
- Long-term planning
- Impulse control
- Social reasoning
- Weighing consequences

> **Aha! moment:** The prefrontal cortex doesn't fully mature until around **age 25**. This is why teenagers genuinely have less capacity for impulse control — it's not just attitude, it's incomplete neurodevelopment.

## How Neurons Work

A neuron is a specialized cell that transmits information via electrical and chemical signals.

### The Basic Signal
1. A neuron receives signals from thousands of other neurons via **dendrites** (branch-like receivers)
2. If the combined signal is strong enough, it fires an **action potential** — an electrical pulse down its **axon** (the long output cable)
3. The pulse reaches the **synapse** — the gap between neurons
4. The neuron releases **neurotransmitters** — chemical messengers — into the synapse
5. The receiving neuron picks up these chemicals and either fires or is inhibited

### Key Neurotransmitters

| Neurotransmitter | Role | What happens when low? |
|---|---|---|
| **Dopamine** | Reward, motivation, movement | Depression, Parkinson's disease |
| **Serotonin** | Mood, sleep, appetite | Depression, anxiety |
| **Norepinephrine** | Alertness, attention, stress response | Depression, ADHD |
| **GABA** | Inhibition — calms the brain | Anxiety, seizures |
| **Glutamate** | Excitation — primary "on" signal | Involved in learning; excess causes seizure |
| **Acetylcholine** | Attention, memory, muscle activation | Alzheimer's disease |

> **The serotonin myth:** You've probably heard that depression is caused by "low serotonin." This is a massive oversimplification — see [Depression101](./Depression101.md) for the full picture.

## Neuroplasticity: The Brain Changes Itself

For most of the 20th century, scientists believed that the adult brain was fixed — neurons you had at 25 were the ones you'd have forever, and the connections between them couldn't meaningfully change.

This was wrong.

**Neuroplasticity** is the brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections throughout life. Key findings:

- **Learning creates new synapses.** Every time you practice a skill, the relevant neural pathways are strengthened ("neurons that fire together, wire together" — Hebb's Rule)
- **New neurons can grow.** The hippocampus generates new neurons throughout adult life (**neurogenesis**) — a process promoted by exercise and suppressed by chronic stress
- **The brain can rewire after injury.** Stroke patients can recover function as other brain regions compensate
- **Meditation changes brain structure.** Long-term meditators show measurable thickening of the prefrontal cortex and reduced amygdala reactivity

> **Practical implication:** The brain you have isn't fixed. Habits, practice, environment, and deliberate effort literally change the physical structure of your brain.

## The Default Mode Network (DMN)

When you're not focused on anything in particular — daydreaming, mind-wandering, thinking about yourself — a specific network of brain regions becomes active: the **Default Mode Network**.

The DMN includes the medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, and hippocampus.

**What it does:**
- Self-referential thinking ("What do people think of me?")
- Mental time travel — reliving the past, imagining the future
- Theory of mind — modeling other people's mental states
- Narrative identity — constructing the story of "who you are"

**Why it matters:** The DMN is overactive in depression (too much negative self-referential rumination). It's quieted by meditation. And it may be where creativity happens — see [Creativity101](./Creativity101.md).

## The Two Brain Hemispheres

The brain is divided into two **hemispheres** connected by the **corpus callosum** (a thick cable of 200–250 million nerve fibers).

**Pop-psych myth:** "Left-brained = logical, right-brained = creative." This is neuroscience mythology. Both hemispheres are involved in virtually every complex cognitive task.

**What is true:**
- **Language production** is primarily in the left hemisphere (Broca's area)
- **Language comprehension** is primarily left (Wernicke's area)
- **Spatial processing** leans right
- **Emotional expression** involves both, with some right-hemisphere specialization for negative emotions

In **split-brain patients** (whose corpus callosa were severed to treat epilepsy), the two hemispheres can behave almost independently — producing some of the most bizarre and illuminating experiments in neuroscience history.

## Sleep and the Brain

Sleep is not downtime. It's when the brain does critical maintenance:

- **Memory consolidation:** The hippocampus replays daily experiences, transferring important memories to long-term cortical storage
- **Glymphatic clearance:** During deep sleep, the brain's glymphatic system flushes out metabolic waste products — including amyloid-beta proteins associated with Alzheimer's disease
- **Emotional processing:** REM sleep processes emotional memories, reducing their emotional charge (why "sleeping on it" actually works)
- **Synaptic homeostasis:** Weak synaptic connections are pruned during sleep, making the brain more efficient

Chronic sleep deprivation is measurably neurotoxic — it accelerates cognitive decline, emotional dysregulation, and physical illness.

## Key Terms

- **Neuron:** Basic cell of the nervous system; transmits signals via electrical and chemical means
- **Synapse:** The gap between neurons where neurotransmitters are exchanged
- **Neuroplasticity:** The brain's ability to change its structure and connections in response to experience
- **Prefrontal cortex (PFC):** The executive control center — planning, impulse control, social reasoning
- **Amygdala:** The brain's alarm system — processes fear and emotional significance
- **Hippocampus:** Critical for forming new memories; shrinks under chronic stress
- **Default Mode Network:** Active during self-reflection, daydreaming, and mind-wandering
- **Neurotransmitter:** Chemical messenger released at synapses
