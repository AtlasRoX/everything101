# Personality 101

> *Personality is the consistent pattern of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that makes you recognizably you — across situations, across decades, and across the eyes of people who know you.*

---

## The Big Picture

Are you the same person you are at work as you are with close friends? Probably not in every way — but your core tendencies persist. The curious person stays curious. The conscientious person stays organized. The introvert needs alone time whether they're at a party or a business meeting.

**Personality** is what persists. It's the stable individual differences that make prediction of a person's behavior possible across contexts.

The study of personality asks: What are the fundamental dimensions of human personality? How stable are they? What causes them? And can they change?

---

## Why Early Theories Failed

Personality psychology has a troubled history. Early approaches were either:
- **Too vague** (Freud's personality types: oral, anal, phallic — not scientifically testable)
- **Too arbitrary** (Myers-Briggs: no predictive validity for job performance; people's types change significantly between tests)
- **Too culture-bound** (early American personality scales normed on narrow populations)

The field was transformed by a statistical approach: instead of theorizing what personality should be, ask what it actually is by analyzing how traits naturally cluster together in large populations.

## The Big Five: The Current Scientific Consensus

Since the 1990s, the **Five-Factor Model (FFM)** — often called the **Big Five** — has become the dominant framework in personality research. It was discovered independently by multiple research teams analyzing language and questionnaire data across cultures.

The Big Five factors are:

### O — Openness to Experience
- **High:** Curious, imaginative, aesthetic appreciation, receptive to new ideas, unconventional
- **Low:** Practical, conventional, prefers routine, concrete over abstract
- **Predicts:** Creative achievement, political liberalism, artistic interests
- **Stability:** Most associated with intellectual and creative tendencies

### C — Conscientiousness
- **High:** Organized, dependable, disciplined, goal-directed, thorough
- **Low:** Spontaneous, flexible, disorganized, procrastinating
- **Predicts:** Academic performance, job performance (most powerful predictor across all occupations), longevity, relationship stability
- **The big deal:** Conscientiousness is the single most practically important Big Five trait — it predicts outcomes across more domains than any other

### E — Extraversion
- **High:** Sociable, assertive, energetic, talkative, thrill-seeking
- **Low (Introversion):** Reserved, prefers solitude, reflective, quieter environments
- **Predicts:** Leadership emergence, social relationship quantity (not quality), positive mood
- **Important nuance:** Introversion ≠ shyness. Introversion is about energy (social situations drain introverts); shyness is about fear of social evaluation.

### A — Agreeableness
- **High:** Cooperative, trusting, empathetic, kind, conflict-avoidant
- **Low:** Competitive, skeptical, critical, direct, willing to confront
- **Predicts:** Relationship harmony, prosocial behavior, avoiding conflict
- **Trade-offs:** High agreeableness correlates with being liked but also with being exploited; low agreeableness correlates with negotiating higher salaries

### N — Neuroticism (Emotional Stability at the low end)
- **High Neuroticism:** Emotionally reactive, prone to anxiety, depression, anger, mood swings
- **Low Neuroticism (Emotionally Stable):** Calm, even-keeled, resilient under stress
- **Predicts:** Mental health outcomes, relationship conflict, job dissatisfaction
- **The most clinically significant:** High neuroticism is the strongest personality predictor of anxiety and depression

> **Aha! moment:** Introversion is not a personality disorder. High neuroticism is not the same as introversion. These are frequently confused — introverts are often calm and stable (low neuroticism); the neurotic extravert exists too.

## How Stable Is Personality?

**Rank-order stability** (your position relative to others) is approximately **0.65** over a 40-year period in adults. This is high by social science standards — personality is one of the most stable individual difference variables.

**What changes with age:**
- Conscientiousness increases through adulthood (people become more organized and reliable)
- Agreeableness increases with age (people become kinder and more tolerant)
- Neuroticism decreases somewhat with age
- Openness peaks in young adulthood and declines somewhat
- Extraversion is relatively stable but decreases slightly

**The "set-point" model:** Like body weight, personality may have a biological set-point that major life events can temporarily shift — but you tend to drift back.

**Can therapy change personality?** Research suggests psychotherapy produces modest but real changes in neuroticism — reducing emotional reactivity. Conscientiousness can be improved through behavioral interventions. The Big Five are not entirely fixed.

## Genetics and Personality

Twin studies consistently show **40–60% heritability** for all Big Five traits. This means roughly half of the variation in personality across people is explained by genetic differences.

**The "shared environment paradox":** Surprisingly, growing up in the same household accounts for very little of personality similarity between siblings. Non-shared environmental influences (different peer groups, different life experiences, random developmental variation) matter more than shared family environment.

This initially seems impossible — but it suggests that *how* parents treat specific children matters more than the general family environment.

## Dark Triad

Beyond the Big Five, personality researchers have identified a cluster of socially aversive traits sometimes called the **Dark Triad**:

| Trait | Core Feature | Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Narcissism** | Grandiosity, entitlement, need for admiration | Fragile self-esteem underneath |
| **Machiavellianism** | Manipulativeness, strategic exploitation, cynicism | Deliberate, calculating, strategic |
| **Psychopathy** | Callousness, impulsivity, lack of empathy | Shallow affect; fearlessness |

These three overlap but are distinct. They share a core of **callousness** and **manipulativeness**.

People high in Dark Triad traits are overrepresented in leadership positions and tend to be charming in short interactions. They're harmful over longer exposure.

## Beyond Big Five: Other Important Traits

**Grit** (Angela Duckworth): Passion and perseverance for long-term goals. Predicts achievement beyond IQ and conscientiousness, particularly in challenging domains.

**Locus of Control:** The degree to which you believe your outcomes are controlled by yourself (internal) vs. external forces (luck, others). Internal locus of control correlates with better health, academic achievement, and well-being.

**Attachment Style:** Secure, anxious, avoidant, or disorganized — patterns of relating in close relationships, formed in early childhood but modifiable across life.

## Popular Personality Systems: Caveat Emptor

**Myers-Briggs (MBTI):** Widely used in corporate settings. Problems: Based on Jungian theory rather than empirical research; poor test-retest reliability (people's "type" changes on retest ~50% of the time); poor predictive validity for job performance. Not recommended by personality psychologists.

**Enneagram:** Ancient typological system. Psychometrically weak; not peer-reviewed science. Can be a useful framework for self-reflection but should not be treated as empirically validated.

**HEXACO model:** An extension of the Big Five that adds a sixth factor: **Honesty-Humility** (sincerity, fairness, lack of greed and pretension). Increasingly supported by cross-cultural research as capturing something the Big Five misses.

## Personality in Practice

**Hiring:** Conscientiousness is the best Big Five predictor of job performance. Emotional stability (low neuroticism) predicts job satisfaction and retention. Extraversion predicts performance in sales and leadership roles.

**Relationships:** Partner personality matters — high neuroticism in either partner predicts relationship conflict; similar extraversion levels correlates with relationship satisfaction.

**Mental health:** Neuroticism is the trait most strongly associated with depression, anxiety, and other mental disorders. Understanding one's neuroticism level helps contextualize mental health challenges without pathologizing normal variation.

---

## Key Terms
- **Big Five (OCEAN):** The five-factor model of personality: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism
- **Conscientiousness:** The most practically predictive Big Five trait — organization, discipline, goal-directedness
- **Neuroticism:** Emotional reactivity and instability; strongest personality predictor of mental health difficulties
- **Extraversion:** Preference for social stimulation and external engagement (vs. introversion — not shyness)
- **Dark Triad:** Narcissism, Machiavellianism, and psychopathy — socially aversive personality traits
- **Heritability:** ~40–60% of personality variation is genetic in origin
- **Grit:** Passion and perseverance for long-term goals — predicts achievement beyond intelligence
