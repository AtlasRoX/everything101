# Psychology 101

> *The science of the mind — why you think what you think, feel what you feel, and do what you do.*

---

## What Is Psychology?

**Psychology** is the scientific study of behavior and mental processes. It sits at the intersection of biology, philosophy, medicine, and social science — trying to answer the oldest questions humans have asked about themselves:

- Why do we feel emotions?
- How do we form memories?
- What makes someone fall into depression?
- Why do smart people make dumb decisions?
- What is personality, and where does it come from?

Psychology isn't just therapy. It's the science behind how your brain processes light into images, how social pressure makes you conform, why habits are hard to break, and what "being smart" actually means.

---

## The Big Branches

Psychology isn't one field — it's a family of related disciplines:

| Branch | Studies | Example Question |
|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive Psychology** | Thinking, memory, attention | How do we remember faces? |
| **Neuroscience/Biopsychology** | The brain and nervous system | Which brain regions control fear? |
| **Clinical Psychology** | Mental disorders and treatment | How do we treat depression? |
| **Social Psychology** | How others influence us | Why do people obey authority? |
| **Developmental Psychology** | How we change from birth to death | How do children learn language? |
| **Personality Psychology** | Individual differences in traits | Why are some people more conscientious? |
| **Evolutionary Psychology** | How psychology evolved | Why do we fear snakes more than cars? |

## A Brief History

### Before Psychology Was a Science

For most of human history, the mind was philosophy's territory. Plato thought the soul was immortal. Aristotle thought thoughts were stored in the heart. René Descartes proposed mind-body dualism — the mind and body are separate substances.

### The Birth of Experimental Psychology (1879)

**Wilhelm Wundt** opened the first psychology laboratory in Leipzig, Germany in 1879. He wanted to study mental processes scientifically — with experiments, measurement, and replication. This is the founding moment of psychology as a discipline.

**William James** wrote *The Principles of Psychology* (1890) — still one of the most readable introductions to the field. He introduced **functionalism**: instead of just cataloging mental elements, we should ask what they *do*.

### Freud and the Unconscious

**Sigmund Freud** (1856–1939) proposed that most of our mental life is unconscious — driven by hidden desires, repressed memories, and internal conflicts we can't directly access. While many of Freud's specific claims have been revised or rejected, his central insight — that we are not fully aware of why we do what we do — is now scientifically mainstream.

### Behaviorism: Only What We Can Observe

**John Watson** and **B.F. Skinner** argued that psychology should study only observable behavior, not internal mental states (which can't be measured directly). Their work on conditioning, reinforcement, and reward is the foundation of behavioral economics, education theory, and animal training.

### The Cognitive Revolution (1950s–1970s)

**Noam Chomsky, George Miller**, and others pushed back against behaviorism — you can't explain language acquisition or problem-solving without talking about internal mental representations. The cognitive revolution brought the mind back in, now studied through information processing models.

### Modern Psychology: Neuroscience + Computation

Today, psychology merges with neuroscience (brain imaging), computer science (AI models of cognition), and genetics (twin studies, genome-wide association studies) to understand the mind with precision that Freud or Watson couldn't imagine.

## Core Concepts

### The Nature-Nurture Question

One of psychology's oldest debates: How much of who you are is genetic vs. shaped by environment?

Modern answer: **Both, always, inseparably.** Genes set ranges of possibility; environments determine where within that range you land. A person with genetic risk for depression may never develop it without certain life experiences. This is called **gene-environment interaction**.

### The Conscious and Unconscious Mind

You are aware of only a fraction of your mental activity. Research shows that:
- Most emotional reactions are formed before consciousness catches up
- Decisions are often made unconsciously before we "consciously decide"
- Biases operate below the level of awareness
- Habits run without conscious attention

> **Aha! moment:** Your conscious mind is less like a CEO making decisions and more like a spokesperson narrating decisions that have already been made.

### The Brain as Hardware, Mind as Software

The brain is a physical organ. The mind is the experience that arises from it. The relationship between them is one of psychology's deepest puzzles — the **mind-body problem** (also addressed in [PhilosophyOfMind101](../Philosophy/PhilosophyOfMind101.md)).

## Why Study Psychology?

- **Self-understanding:** Why do you procrastinate? What triggers your anxiety? What makes you happy?
- **Relationships:** How do attachment styles shape romantic relationships? Why do people gaslight?
- **Decisions:** What cognitive biases distort your thinking? How do you make better choices?
- **Society:** Why do people follow crowds? How do cults form? What creates prejudice?
- **Health:** What is depression, really? How does stress damage the body? What actually works in therapy?
