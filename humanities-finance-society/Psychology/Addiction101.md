# Addiction 101

> *Addiction is not a moral failing. It's a brain disorder — one in which the learning and motivation systems that make humans extraordinary get captured by a substance or behavior.*

---

## The Big Picture

**~280 million people** worldwide have drug use disorders. When you add behavioral addictions (gambling, gaming, pornography, food), the number is far larger.

Addiction has been explained as weakness, sin, disease, choice, and genetic destiny. Each framing captures something while missing something.

The current scientific understanding: **addiction is a chronic brain disorder** — one in which the circuits responsible for reward, motivation, memory, and self-control are profoundly dysregulated. Understanding it as a brain condition is not about excusing behavior — it's about understanding why willpower alone rarely works, and what does.

---

## What Is Addiction? Defining the Disorder

**Addiction** (Substance Use Disorder in DSM-5 terminology) is characterized by:

1. **Compulsive use** despite significant negative consequences
2. **Loss of control** — using more or longer than intended
3. **Preoccupation** — significant time spent obtaining, using, recovering
4. **Craving** — intense urges that dominate attention
5. **Continued use despite awareness of harm** — this is the hallmark that separates addiction from heavy use

**Tolerance:** Needing more of the substance to achieve the same effect.

**Withdrawal:** Uncomfortable (sometimes dangerous) physiological and psychological symptoms when use stops.

## The Neuroscience: The Hijacked Reward Circuit

The brain's **mesolimbic dopamine system** — the reward circuit — is the core target of all addictive substances.

### How the Reward Circuit Works Normally

The **nucleus accumbens** (the "pleasure center") receives dopamine from the **ventral tegmental area** (VTA). Dopamine release signals reward and drives learning: *this was good, remember it, do it again.*

Natural rewards (food, sex, social connection, achievement) produce moderate dopamine releases that drive adaptive behavior.

### How Drugs Hijack It

Addictive drugs produce dopamine releases **2–10 times larger** than natural rewards — through mechanisms that bypass normal regulatory controls:

| Drug | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Cocaine** | Blocks dopamine reuptake — dopamine accumulates in synapse |
| **Amphetamine** | Forces dopamine release AND blocks reuptake |
| **Opioids** | Activate opioid receptors that inhibit GABA, disinhibiting dopamine neurons |
| **Nicotine** | Directly activates acetylcholine receptors on dopamine neurons |
| **Alcohol** | Multiple mechanisms — GABA enhancement, glutamate inhibition, dopamine release |
| **Cannabis** | Endocannabinoid system activation, indirectly increases dopamine |

### What Repeated Drug Use Does to the Brain

**Dopamine dysregulation:** The brain's response to dopamine is not linear — it **downregulates**. After repeated massive dopamine spikes:
- Fewer dopamine receptors are expressed
- The remaining receptors become less sensitive
- Natural rewards produce even smaller dopamine responses (anhedonia — nothing feels good anymore)
- Only the drug produces the now-expected dopamine surge

This is **tolerance** at the cellular level. And it creates a terrible trap: the person needs more of the drug to feel anything good, and normal life becomes affectively gray.

### The Shift from Liking to Wanting

**Kent Berridge** made a crucial distinction:

- **Liking:** The subjective pleasure of a reward — associated with opioid systems
- **Wanting:** The motivational pull toward a reward — associated with dopamine

In addiction, these decouple. An addicted person *wants* the drug intensely — craving dominates their mental life — but often *likes* it less and less. The dopamine system drives wanting independently of pleasure.

This explains the addicted person's own confusion: "I don't even enjoy it anymore, but I can't stop." The wanting system has been captured, even as the liking system has burned out.

## The Prefrontal Cortex: The Regulator Falls

The prefrontal cortex — the seat of impulse control, long-term planning, and self-regulation — is directly damaged by chronic drug use AND by the pattern of addictive behavior.

In addicted brains:
- **PFC activity** during decision-making is reduced
- **Gray matter volume** in PFC decreases
- **Connectivity** between PFC and reward circuits is disrupted

The result: the system that would inhibit drug-seeking behavior is weakened precisely because of drug use. This is why "just use willpower" fails — the circuitry required for willpower has been impaired.

This is not permanent. With extended abstinence, PFC function gradually recovers — and this is why sustained recovery becomes easier over time, even though it's hardest at the start.

---

## Stress and the HPA Axis in Addiction

**Corticotropin-releasing factor (CRF)** is released in withdrawal and stress, producing dysphoria and craving. This is why:

- Stress is the **#1 trigger for relapse**, even after extended abstinence
- Childhood trauma (ACEs) dramatically increases addiction risk — the HPA axis is already dysregulated
- Negative affect (anxiety, depression) and addiction co-occur at very high rates
- Treating co-occurring disorders is essential for addiction recovery

---

## Memory: Why Cues Are Dangerous

Drug use is powerfully conditioned. Environmental cues (places, people, paraphernalia, even music) associated with drug use become **conditioned stimuli** that trigger:
- Intense craving
- Dopamine release in anticipation
- Physiological responses that prepare the body for drug administration

This is **conditioned cue reactivity** — and it can persist for years or decades after the last drug use. A recovered heroin user walking past their former dealer's street can experience intense craving from contextual cues alone.

This is why **environmental change** is critical in recovery — and why "the geographic cure" (moving away from the environment of use) actually has some evidence behind it.

---

## Behavioral Addictions: The Same Circuit

Brain imaging shows that **behavioral addictions** (gambling disorder, internet gaming disorder, compulsive sexual behavior) activate the same reward circuits as substance addictions.

- Gamblers show the same dopamine release patterns to gambling-related cues as cocaine users show to drug cues
- The same variable-ratio reinforcement schedule drives gambling as drives slot machine and social media use
- Internet gaming disorder produces measurable PFC volume reduction similar to substance use disorders

The mechanism is identical: the reward circuit is captured, regulation fails, compulsive behavior continues despite consequences.

## Why People Use: The Social and Environmental Story

Biology explains why addiction is *maintained*. It doesn't fully explain why it *starts*.

**Rat Park experiment (Alexander, 1978):** Rats in isolated cages with only morphine water drank themselves to death. Rats in enriched environments with social contact, activities, and space mostly avoided the morphine water even when available.

**Human analogue:** Addiction rates are dramatically higher in:
- Poverty and economic marginalization
- Social isolation and loneliness
- Trauma history (especially childhood)
- Mental health conditions (addiction as self-medication)
- Hopelessness (no competing future orientation)

**Vietnam veterans:** ~20% of US soldiers in Vietnam used heroin. Of those who returned home, ~95% ceased use without treatment. The change in environment, social context, and purpose was transformative.

> **Aha! moment:** Addiction is partly about the drug — but it's also about the life the drug is embedded in. Treatment that addresses only the substance while ignoring poverty, isolation, and trauma fails because it ignores what drove use in the first place.

## What Actually Works: Evidence-Based Treatment

### Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT)

The most evidence-based treatments for opioid use disorder combine medication with behavioral support:

- **Methadone:** Full opioid agonist — reduces craving and withdrawal, allows functioning without getting high
- **Buprenorphine (Suboxone):** Partial agonist — similar benefits, lower overdose risk, can be prescribed by doctors
- **Naltrexone:** Opioid antagonist — blocks opioid effects; eliminates reward from use

For alcohol use disorder:
- **Naltrexone:** Reduces craving
- **Acamprosate:** Reduces withdrawal discomfort
- **Disulfiram:** Makes alcohol consumption acutely unpleasant (aversive conditioning)

For nicotine: **Varenicline** (Chantix) is the most effective pharmacotherapy.

### Behavioral Treatments

**Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy:** Identifies triggers, builds coping skills, restructures addictive thinking patterns.

**Contingency Management:** Positive reinforcement (vouchers, prizes) for clean drug tests. One of the most effective behavioral approaches — operant conditioning applied directly.

**Motivational Interviewing:** A non-confrontational approach that explores ambivalence about change and builds intrinsic motivation for recovery.

**12-Step Programs (AA, NA):** Peer support, narrative identity change ("I am an addict in recovery"), community accountability. Meta-analyses show 12-step programs are approximately as effective as CBT for alcohol — partly because they address the social isolation component.

## Recovery: What the Science Says

**Recovery is common.** The public perception is that addiction is permanent and recovery is rare — this is wrong. Most people with substance use disorders eventually achieve lasting remission.

- Approximately **75% of people** who develop a substance use disorder eventually achieve stable recovery
- Most do so **without formal treatment** (natural recovery)
- **Extended abstinence rebuilds the brain:** PFC function recovers, dopamine receptor density increases, emotional regulation improves

**The chronic disease model:** Addiction is better understood as a chronic, relapsing condition like hypertension or diabetes — requiring ongoing management, not expecting a single cure. Relapse is part of the disease course, not evidence of failure.

---

## Key Terms
- **Nucleus accumbens:** The "pleasure center" — primary site of dopamine reward signaling
- **Tolerance:** Diminished response to a drug after repeated exposure — requiring more to achieve the same effect
- **Withdrawal:** Physiological and psychological symptoms when a substance is discontinued
- **Wanting vs. Liking (Berridge):** Dopamine drives wanting (craving) independently of pleasure (liking) — explains compulsive use without enjoyment
- **Conditioned cue reactivity:** Environmental cues triggering craving through classical conditioning
- **Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT):** Combining pharmacotherapy with behavioral support for substance use disorders
- **Natural recovery:** Remission from addiction without formal treatment — the most common recovery pathway
