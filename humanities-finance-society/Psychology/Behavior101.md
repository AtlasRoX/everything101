# Behavior 101

> *To change what you do, you must first understand what makes you do it — and the answer is more environmental than internal.*

---

## The Big Picture

**Behaviorism** — the study of observable behavior and its environmental determinants — was the dominant school of psychology from roughly 1913 to the 1960s. It was radical, productive, and in some ways deeply wrong.

Its key insight: **behavior is shaped by its consequences.** This principle is simple, powerful, and still the foundation of behavior change science, education theory, animal training, and organizational design.

Its key error: claiming internal mental states don't exist or don't matter. That view was overturned by the cognitive revolution — but behavioral principles remain essential.

---

## Classical Conditioning: Learning by Association

**Ivan Pavlov** (1849–1936) was studying dog digestion when he noticed something interesting: dogs began salivating before food arrived — at the sound of the assistant's footsteps, or the sight of the bowl.

He had accidentally discovered **classical conditioning** — learning through the pairing of stimuli.

### The Basic Pavlovian Setup

```
Before conditioning:
Unconditioned Stimulus (US) → Unconditioned Response (UR)
[Food]                         [Salivation]

During conditioning:
Conditioned Stimulus (CS) + US → UR
[Bell]              [Food]    [Salivation]

After conditioning:
CS → Conditioned Response (CR)
[Bell] → [Salivation]
```

The neutral stimulus (bell) acquires the power to trigger the response by being repeatedly paired with the unconditioned stimulus (food).

### Key Principles of Classical Conditioning

**Acquisition:** The process of forming the association — requires CS to reliably *predict* US.

**Extinction:** The CR weakens when CS is repeatedly presented *without* US. But extinction doesn't erase the original learning — it suppresses it. The original response can **spontaneously recover** after a rest period.

**Generalization:** Stimuli similar to the CS also elicit CR. A dog conditioned to a 1000Hz tone salivates to a 900Hz tone too.

**Discrimination:** Through differential conditioning, organisms learn to respond to CS but not to similar stimuli.

### Classical Conditioning in Human Life

- **Phobias:** Often acquired through one-trial conditioning with a traumatic US (a dog bite → fear of all dogs)
- **Advertising:** Attractive people + brand logo → positive brand response
- **Cravings:** The smell of popcorn (CS) triggers hunger in a cinema (because cinema = CS for popcorn eating)
- **Taste aversion:** One pairing of a food with nausea produces lasting aversion — evolution built in one-trial learning for food poisoning

**Watson's "Little Albert":** John Watson conditioned an infant to fear a white rat by pairing its presentation with a loud noise. This foundational (and deeply unethical) experiment demonstrated that fear responses can be learned through classical conditioning.

## Operant Conditioning: Learning by Consequences

**B.F. Skinner** (1904–1990) extended Pavlov's work in a crucial direction: behavior is shaped not just by what precedes it but by what **follows** it.

**Operant conditioning:** Behavior is strengthened or weakened by its consequences.

### The Four Contingencies

|  | **Adds** something | **Removes** something |
|---|---|---|
| **Strengthens behavior** | **Positive Reinforcement** | **Negative Reinforcement** |
| **Weakens behavior** | **Positive Punishment** | **Negative Punishment** |

**Positive Reinforcement:** Adding something pleasant to increase behavior.
- Press lever → food pellet → press lever more often
- Complete project → receive praise → complete projects more reliably

**Negative Reinforcement** *(not punishment — this is commonly confused)*: Removing something unpleasant to increase behavior.
- Fasten seatbelt → annoying beeping stops → fasten seatbelt reliably
- Take painkiller → headache goes away → take painkiller for headaches

**Positive Punishment:** Adding something unpleasant to decrease behavior.
- Child misbehaves → receives scolding → child misbehaves less
- Speed → speeding fine → speed less

**Negative Punishment:** Removing something pleasant to decrease behavior.
- Child misbehaves → loses TV privileges → child misbehaves less

### Schedules of Reinforcement

*When* reinforcement occurs matters enormously for how behavior is maintained:

| Schedule | Description | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Continuous** | Reward every time | Fast learning; rapid extinction when stopped |
| **Fixed Ratio** | Reward after N responses | High response rate; pause after each reward |
| **Variable Ratio** | Reward after unpredictable N responses | Highest response rate; most resistant to extinction |
| **Fixed Interval** | Reward after fixed time | Scallop pattern — low then surge near reward time |
| **Variable Interval** | Reward after unpredictable time | Steady moderate response rate |

**Variable ratio schedules are the most addictive.** This is the schedule used by slot machines, social media notifications, and video game loot boxes — unpredictable rewards drive compulsive, extinction-resistant behavior. See [Habit101](./Habit101.md) and [Addiction101](./Addiction101.md).

### Shaping: Building Complex Behaviors

You can't wait for a complex behavior to occur spontaneously and then reinforce it. **Shaping** builds complex behaviors by reinforcing successive approximations:

- To teach a rat to press a lever: reinforce moving toward the lever → touching it → pressing it
- To teach public speaking: reinforce eye contact → clear articulation → confident posture → structured argument

This is how all skill training works — feedback on approximations, not just on perfect performance.

## From Behaviorism to Behavior Analysis

Skinner's methods were extended into **Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA)** — systematic application to human behavior in clinical and educational settings.

**ABA in practice:**
- Autism spectrum interventions: teaching language and social skills through systematic reinforcement
- Organizational Behavior Management: applying behavioral principles to workplace productivity
- Behavioral medicine: changing health behaviors through environmental redesign

## The Cognitive Revolution's Critique

Pure behaviorism claimed you could explain all behavior without reference to internal mental states. Noam Chomsky's 1959 review of Skinner's "Verbal Behavior" was the decisive refutation — children acquire grammatical structures they've never heard, following rules no one explicitly taught them. Learning theory couldn't explain this without invoking internal representations.

**What survived the critique:**
- Reinforcement and punishment principles are real and powerful
- Environmental design shapes behavior reliably
- Observable behavior is an important unit of analysis

**What didn't survive:**
- The claim that internal states are irrelevant
- Simple stimulus-response models of complex human behavior
- The dismissal of cognition and meaning

Modern behavior science is **cognitive-behavioral** — integrating behavioral principles with cognitive processes.

## Social Learning Theory: Bandura's Bridge

**Albert Bandura** showed that learning doesn't require direct experience of consequences. People learn by **observing others** — modeling.

**Bobo Doll experiments (1961):** Children who watched adults aggressively beat an inflatable doll were significantly more aggressive toward the doll themselves — without any reinforcement for doing so.

**Bandura's key additions:**
- **Vicarious reinforcement:** You learn from others' consequences, not just your own
- **Self-efficacy:** Belief in your ability to perform a behavior is itself a major determinant of whether you try
- **Reciprocal determinism:** Behavior, personal factors, and environment all influence each other bidirectionally

Self-efficacy is one of the most powerful predictors of behavior change — more powerful than knowledge or intention. "I know I should exercise and I want to — but I can't do it" is a self-efficacy failure.

## Key Terms
- **Classical conditioning:** Learning through pairing of stimuli; CS comes to elicit CR
- **Operant conditioning:** Behavior shaped by its consequences (reinforcement and punishment)
- **Positive/Negative reinforcement:** Adding/removing something to *increase* behavior
- **Positive/Negative punishment:** Adding/removing something to *decrease* behavior
- **Variable ratio schedule:** Unpredictable rewards — most addictive reinforcement schedule
- **Shaping:** Reinforcing successive approximations to build complex behaviors
- **Social learning theory:** Learning through observation; Bandura's extension of behaviorism
- **Self-efficacy:** Belief in your ability to perform a specific behavior — a critical determinant of action
