# Cognitive Psychology 101

> *The mind is an information processing system. Understanding how it processes, stores, transforms, and outputs information explains almost everything about how you think.*

---

## The Big Picture

**Cognitive psychology** is the scientific study of mental processes: how we perceive the world, pay attention, remember, think, use language, and solve problems.

It arose from the **cognitive revolution** of the 1950s–70s as a reaction against behaviorism's exclusive focus on observable behavior. The key insight that launched it: behavior cannot be explained without reference to internal mental representations.

Cognitive psychology treats the mind as an **information processing system** — analogous (but not identical) to a computer. Input → process → output. This metaphor is imperfect but enormously productive.

### Working Memory: The Mental Workspace
**George Miller's "Magical Number Seven" (1956):** Working memory holds approximately 7 ± 2 items simultaneously. Modern research revises this to about **4 chunks** — but "chunk" is key. An expert chess player can hold board positions (chunks) that a novice cannot, even though both have the same working memory capacity.

**Working memory is not the same as short-term memory.** It's active — it manipulates information, not just stores it. It's the scratch paper on which thinking happens.

**Three components (Baddeley's model):**
- **Phonological loop:** Verbal/auditory information (the "inner voice")
- **Visuospatial sketchpad:** Visual and spatial information (the "inner eye")
- **Central executive:** Directs attention and coordinates the other components

Working memory capacity is one of the strongest predictors of fluid intelligence and academic performance.

## Thinking: Concepts and Categories

### How We Organize Knowledge

The mind doesn't store information as isolated facts — it organizes it into **concepts** and **categories**.

**Classical view (Aristotle):** Categories have defining features — necessary and sufficient conditions. A triangle must have 3 sides, 3 angles, and sum to 180°.

**Prototype theory (Rosch):** For most natural categories, membership is graded, not all-or-nothing. A robin is a more typical "bird" than a penguin. Categories have fuzzy edges and graded membership around a prototype (most typical exemplar).

**Exemplar theory:** We categorize by comparing to stored examples (exemplars) from memory, not to an abstract prototype.

**In practice:** Both prototypes and exemplars are used — prototypes for quick initial categorization, exemplars when more precision is needed.

### Schemas: Organized Knowledge Structures
**Schemas** are organized frameworks of related knowledge — cognitive structures that represent how the world typically works (restaurants have menus, you order, pay, tip; etc.).

Schemas enable:
- Rapid situation interpretation
- Inference of unstated information
- Direction of attention to relevant features

Schemas also cause systematic errors — we remember schema-consistent information better and distort memory toward schema expectations.

## Problem Solving

### The Structure of Problems

Problems have:
- **Initial state** (where you are)
- **Goal state** (where you want to be)
- **Operators** (actions that change states)
- **Constraints** (rules about what's allowed)

### Problem-Solving Strategies

**Algorithms:** Step-by-step procedures that guarantee finding the correct answer if followed exactly. Reliable but computationally expensive.

**Heuristics:** "Rules of thumb" — quick strategies that usually work but occasionally fail. Faster but fallible.

**Means-ends analysis:** Identify the difference between current and goal state; find an operator that reduces the difference. Repeat recursively.

**Analogical reasoning:** Map the structure of a solved problem onto a new problem. Some of history's greatest insights came from this — Bohr's nuclear model used the analogy of the solar system; Kekulé discovered benzene's ring structure from a dream of a snake biting its tail.

### Problem-Solving Obstacles

**Functional fixedness:** Inability to see an object used in a non-typical way. The **two-string problem** (Maier, 1931): two strings hang from the ceiling, too far apart to grab both at once. Solution: tie a heavy object to one string and swing it as a pendulum, grab the other, catch the swinging one. Most people don't see the pliers in the room as a pendulum weight.

**Mental set / Einstellung effect:** A previous solution strategy interferes with finding a better one. Once you've found a method that works, you persist with it even when a simpler solution is available.

**Insight:** Sudden restructuring of a problem representation — the "aha!" moment. Correlated with gamma-wave burst in right temporal lobe, preceded by alpha-wave suppression of visual cortex (closing your eyes to the world to see internally).

## Language: The Architecture of Thought

Language is arguably the most complex cognitive achievement of any species.

### Key Properties

**Generativity/Productivity:** Finite means (vocabulary + grammar) produce infinite novel sentences. You can understand "The purple elephant unicycled through downtown Ljubljana" despite never having heard it.

**Displacement:** Language can refer to things not present in time or space (past, future, hypothetical).

**Arbitrariness:** The word "dog" has no inherent connection to the animal — it's a convention. (Exceptions: onomatopoeia.)

### Language and Thought: The Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis

**Linguistic relativity** (Sapir-Whorf): The language you speak influences the way you think.

**Strong version:** Language determines thought — you cannot think what your language can't express. This version is largely rejected.

**Weak version:** Language influences cognitive patterns and ease of thought. Supported by evidence:
- Languages with more color terms produce better color discrimination in perception tasks
- Languages with absolute spatial terms (north/south/east/west instead of left/right) produce better spatial navigation
- Russian speakers are faster to distinguish shades of blue (Russian has separate words for light/dark blue) in the cross-categorical pairs

### Chomsky and Universal Grammar

**Noam Chomsky** argued that humans are born with an innate **Universal Grammar** — a set of abstract grammatical principles common to all human languages. Evidence: children acquire grammatical structures far beyond what they're explicitly taught (the "poverty of the stimulus" argument).

**Current status:** The specific UG hypothesis is debated, but the evidence for **language acquisition device** — some innately constrained, rapid, universal capacity for language learning in children — is strong.

## Dual Coding Theory

**Allan Paivio:** Information is stored in two separate but interconnected systems — a **verbal system** and a **nonverbal (imagery) system**. Information encoded in both systems is more retrievable than information in either alone.

**Why this matters for learning:**
- Text + relevant images is better than text alone
- Visualizing what you're reading improves comprehension and retention
- Concept maps combine verbal labels with spatial relationships — dual coding in practice

---

## Cognitive Load Theory

**John Sweller:** Learning is limited by working memory capacity. When instructional design imposes too much **cognitive load** — information that exceeds WM capacity — learning fails.

**Three types of cognitive load:**
- **Intrinsic:** Inherent complexity of the material (can't be reduced)
- **Extraneous:** Load from poor instructional design (can and should be reduced)
- **Germane:** Load from schema construction — the productive effort of learning

**Implications for education:**
- Worked examples reduce extraneous load (showing the solution before asking students to solve)
- Reduce split-attention effect (integrate text and diagrams rather than separating them)
- Novices need more support (guidance, examples); experts are slowed down by the same guidance (the "expertise reversal effect")

## The Unconscious Mind in Cognitive Psychology

Cognitive psychology revealed the scale of unconscious processing — not in Freud's sense (repressed desires) but in the sense of pre-attentive, automatic computation:

- Visual scene recognition is largely complete before you're aware of it
- Word recognition and semantic priming happen below conscious threshold
- Social categorization (age, gender, race, status) occurs automatically in ~100ms
- Emotional evaluations (like/dislike) precede conscious awareness

The "unconscious" in cognitive psychology is not a hidden wish — it's the vast automatic processing machinery that runs your perception, language comprehension, and motor control without requiring attention.

## Key Terms
- **Working memory:** The active mental workspace for holding and manipulating information (~4 chunks)
- **Chunking:** Grouping information into meaningful units that expand effective working memory
- **Schema:** An organized knowledge structure that enables rapid interpretation and inference
- **Functional fixedness:** Inability to see objects used in non-typical ways
- **Insight:** Sudden restructuring of a problem representation — the aha moment
- **Linguistic relativity:** The weak hypothesis that language influences (not determines) thought
- **Cognitive load:** The demand on working memory during learning — excessive load prevents schema formation
- **Dual coding:** Encoding information in both verbal and visual systems for better retention
