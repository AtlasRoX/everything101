# Bias 101

> *Your brain is not a logic machine with occasional errors. It's a fast-pattern-matching system with occasional flashes of deliberate reasoning — and it's biased by design.*

---

## The Big Picture

A **cognitive bias** is a systematic, predictable error in thinking — a consistent deviation from rationality that occurs in particular circumstances. Biases are not random mistakes; they're **systematic patterns** that affect people in the same ways.

The field was largely founded by **Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky** through decades of experiments showing that human judgment departs from rational models in predictable, consistent ways. Kahneman won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2002 for this work.

The existence of biases doesn't mean humans are stupid. Most biases are shortcuts that work well most of the time in the environments humans evolved in. They become problems in modern contexts — financial markets, medical diagnosis, legal systems — where different kinds of accuracy matter.

---

## System 1 and System 2: The Dual-Process Framework

**Kahneman's** framework from *Thinking, Fast and Slow* organizes cognition into two systems:

| Feature | System 1 | System 2 |
|---|---|---|
| **Speed** | Fast, automatic | Slow, effortful |
| **Consciousness** | Unconscious | Conscious |
| **Capacity** | Unlimited parallel processing | Limited serial processing |
| **Accuracy** | High for familiar patterns; biased | High for novel problems; lazy |
| **Effort** | None | Significant |

Most cognition is System 1. System 2 is what you use when you do long division or carefully read a contract. System 2 is accurate but expensive — it tires, and it relies on System 1 for most inputs.

**Biases arise when System 1 answers are wrong and System 2 fails to correct them** — either because System 2 is engaged elsewhere, lazy, or doesn't realize the error.

## Category 1: Heuristics That Mislead

### Availability Heuristic
**Definition:** Judging the probability of something by how easily examples come to mind.

**Example:** Plane crashes receive massive media coverage. Car accidents don't. People judge flying as more dangerous than driving — it's significantly less dangerous per mile. Your brain uses *availability of examples* as a proxy for frequency.

**Where it matters:** Risk assessment, medical diagnosis, policy decisions. After a newsworthy event (shark attack, school shooting), people massively overestimate its probability.

### Representativeness Heuristic
**Definition:** Judging probability by how closely something resembles a prototype or stereotype.

**The Linda Problem (Tversky & Kahneman):**
> Linda is 31, outspoken, single, very bright. As a student, she was deeply concerned with discrimination and social justice and participated in anti-nuclear demonstrations.
> Which is more probable? A) Linda is a bank teller. B) Linda is a bank teller and a feminist.

85% of people choose B. But B must be *less* probable than A — it's a subset. This is the **conjunction fallacy**: adding detail makes things *feel* more probable while making them mathematically less so. Linda *seems* more like a feminist bank teller than just a bank teller.

### Anchoring Effect
**Definition:** Heavy reliance on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making estimates.

**Example:** Asked "Is the population of Turkey greater or less than 35 million?" then "What is the population of Turkey?" — people estimate lower than if asked "Is it greater or less than 100 million?" The initial number anchors subsequent estimates, even when it's arbitrary.

**Real-world:** Salary negotiations (first number sets the range); court sentencing (higher charges → longer sentences, even when charges are reduced); price tags that start high.

## Category 2: Self-Serving Biases

### Confirmation Bias
**Definition:** Seeking, interpreting, and remembering information that confirms existing beliefs; dismissing disconfirming evidence.

This is arguably the most dangerous cognitive bias because it compounds over time — the longer you hold a belief, the more confirmed it becomes regardless of new evidence.

**Mechanisms:**
- **Selective exposure:** Consuming news that agrees with you
- **Biased interpretation:** Interpreting ambiguous evidence as supportive
- **Myside testing:** Generating arguments for your position, not against it
- **Memory bias:** Better remembering confirming instances

**In practice:** This is why political polarization intensifies, why conspiracy theories self-reinforce, and why experts can be overconfident.

### Dunning-Kruger Effect
**David Dunning and Justin Kruger** (1999) found that incompetent people tend to overestimate their ability, while highly competent people tend to underestimate theirs.

**Why:** Competence in a domain is required to accurately assess your own competence in that domain. Novices don't know what they don't know.

**Caveat:** The Dunning-Kruger effect is often misrepresented. It's not that *dumb people think they're geniuses*. It's that *across all skill levels*, people have poor metacognitive accuracy — and the less you know, the less aware you are of how much you don't know.

### Overconfidence Bias
People are systematically overconfident in their own judgments, predictions, and knowledge.

**The calibration test:** Ask people to give a range they're 90% confident contains the correct answer (e.g., "How many airports are in the US?"). Most people are only right ~50% of the time — their "90% confident" intervals don't capture the truth 50% of the time.

Overconfidence is highest for:
- Tasks that feel familiar (but are subtly different)
- Rare, difficult-to-test predictions (stock market, elections)
- Expert judgments in complex fields

### Self-Serving Bias
Attributing successes to personal ability and failures to external circumstances. "I got the promotion because I'm talented; I lost the client because the market was bad."

Combined with the Fundamental Attribution Error (attributing others' failures to character), this creates a consistently self-flattering world-view.

## Category 3: Memory and Hindsight Biases

### Hindsight Bias ("I Knew It All Along")
After an event, we believe we predicted it — and that it was obvious. This makes it hard to learn from unexpected outcomes and inflates our sense of predictive accuracy.

**Medical and legal consequences:** After a bad outcome, retrospective evaluation makes prior decisions look obviously wrong. Doctors, pilots, and generals are judged on outcomes in ways that ignore the genuine uncertainty at decision time.

### Rosy Retrospection
We remember the past more positively than it was and more negatively than the present. "The good old days" — which weren't that good.

This distorts nostalgia, creates false baseline comparisons, and can make the present feel inadequate in comparison to a past that didn't exist.

## Category 4: Framing Effects

### The Framing Effect
The same information, framed differently, produces different decisions.

**Kahneman & Tversky:** "A new treatment saves 200 of 600 people" vs. "400 of 600 people die." Same outcome; doctors made different recommendations based on the frame.

We are loss averse — losses loom larger than equivalent gains. This means negative frames ("200 die") produce more risk-averse decisions than positive frames ("200 saved").

**In practice:** How options are framed (default settings, price presentation, health communications) dramatically affects choices — which is why "nudge" policy works.

### Status Quo Bias
We prefer the current state of affairs to alternatives. Change is experienced as a loss (loss aversion); not-changing is experienced as maintenance of the baseline.

**Organ donation:** Countries with opt-in systems have ~10-15% donation rates. Countries with opt-out (same choice, different default) have ~90%+ rates. The option is identical; the default is the whole difference.

### Sunk Cost Fallacy
Continuing an investment because of resources already spent, rather than based on future expected value.

"I've already paid for the concert ticket — I have to go, even though I'm sick."
"We've already invested $10 million in this project — we can't abandon it now."

The money is gone either way. Future decisions should be based on future costs and benefits, not past expenditures. But emotionally, abandoning feels like waste — so we keep going.

## Category 5: Social Biases

### In-Group Bias
Favoring members of our own group — our nationality, ethnicity, political party, sports team, university — over out-group members, often without realizing it.

This bias is **remarkably easily triggered** — even arbitrary group assignment produces in-group favoritism (Tajfel's Minimal Group experiments).

### Halo Effect
A positive trait in one area causes assumptions of positive traits in other areas. Attractive people are judged as more intelligent, kind, and honest. Eloquent speakers are judged as more knowledgeable. A good first impression colors all subsequent judgments.

The reverse is the **horn effect** — one negative trait produces a halo of negative assumptions.

## Debiasing: Can You Think Better?

Awareness of biases helps but is not sufficient — knowing about confirmation bias doesn't prevent it.

**Strategies that actually help:**
- **Consider the opposite:** Deliberately generate arguments against your current position
- **Pre-mortem:** Before committing to a decision, imagine it failed — what went wrong?
- **Use base rates:** Ground estimates in statistical frequencies rather than gut feelings
- **Slow down:** Biases are more prevalent under time pressure and cognitive load
- **Seek disconfirming evidence:** Actively look for information that challenges your view
- **Disaggregate judgments:** Separate information gathering from evaluation from decision

**The sobering limit:** Even trained statisticians show biases in everyday reasoning. Debiasing is a practice, not a destination.

---

## Key Terms
- **Cognitive bias:** A systematic, predictable deviation from rationality in judgment
- **System 1 / System 2:** Fast automatic cognition vs. slow deliberate reasoning
- **Availability heuristic:** Judging probability by ease of recall
- **Anchoring:** Disproportionate influence of the first piece of information
- **Confirmation bias:** Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs
- **Dunning-Kruger effect:** Incompetent people overestimating their ability due to metacognitive deficits
- **Framing effect:** Same information presented differently produces different decisions
- **Sunk cost fallacy:** Continuing investment because of past expenditure, ignoring future value
- **Loss aversion:** Losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good
