# Perception 101

> *You don't see the world as it is. You see a model of the world that your brain constructs — and it's constantly guessing.*

---

## The Big Picture

Right now, your eyes are sending your brain about 10 million bits of information per second. Your brain is processing only a fraction. It's filling in the rest — using expectations, prior knowledge, and statistical inference to construct the scene you experience as "reality."

Perception is not passive reception. It's active **construction**. This has profound implications for how we understand experience, disagreement, witness testimony, and the reliability of our own senses.

---

## From Sensation to Perception: The Two Stages

**Sensation:** The raw detection of physical energy by sensory receptors.
- Photoreceptors in the retina detect light
- Hair cells in the cochlea detect vibration
- Mechanoreceptors in skin detect pressure and temperature

**Perception:** The interpretation and organization of that raw sensory data into meaningful experience.

The same raw stimulus produces different perceptions in different people, different contexts, and different mental states. Sensation is relatively fixed; perception is highly variable.

---

## Bottom-Up vs. Top-Down Processing

**Bottom-up processing:** Building perception from sensory data — driven by the stimulus itself. No prior knowledge needed. If something is bright red and moving fast toward you, bottom-up processing detects it.

**Top-down processing:** Using prior knowledge, expectations, and context to interpret sensory data. Your brain matches incoming data against stored schemas.

**Both always operate simultaneously.** What you experience is a synthesis:

**Example — Reading:** You read degraded text (letters with parts missing) more easily in familiar words than unfamiliar ones. Top-down knowledge of likely words fills in missing letter features.

**Example — The Cocktail Party Effect:** You detect your own name in background noise you weren't consciously monitoring. Top-down schemas for self-relevant information boost bottom-up detection.

## Gestalt Principles: How the Brain Organizes Scenes

The **Gestalt psychologists** (Germany, early 20th century) identified principles by which the brain organizes visual elements into coherent wholes:

**Figure-Ground:** We automatically separate scenes into a figure (object of attention) and ground (background). Ambiguous figures (like the Rubin Vase — two faces or a vase?) oscillate because either interpretation is equally valid.

**Proximity:** Elements close together are perceived as groups.

**Similarity:** Elements that look alike are perceived as belonging together.

**Continuity:** The brain prefers smooth, continuous contours to abrupt changes.

**Closure:** The brain fills in gaps to complete familiar shapes. ◯ with a gap is still perceived as a circle.

**Common Fate:** Elements moving in the same direction are perceived as a group.

These are not arbitrary conventions — they reflect statistical regularities in the natural world. Objects that share color or proximity usually belong together. The brain has learned these patterns through evolution.

## Depth Perception

We live in a 3D world but have 2D retinas. How do we perceive depth?

**Binocular cues (require two eyes):**
- **Binocular disparity:** Each eye sees a slightly different image. The brain combines them; the degree of difference = distance information. This is how stereoscopes and 3D movies work.
- **Convergence:** The degree to which eyes turn inward when focusing close objects.

**Monocular cues (work with one eye):**
- **Linear perspective:** Parallel lines converge in the distance
- **Relative size:** Smaller = farther
- **Interposition:** Objects that overlap are in front
- **Texture gradient:** Fine texture = farther
- **Atmospheric perspective:** Distant objects appear hazier
- **Motion parallax:** As you move, close objects move faster relative to far ones

Artists use monocular cues to create the illusion of depth on a flat canvas.

## Perceptual Constancy

Despite constant changes in the retinal image, we perceive objects as stable:

**Size constancy:** A person 10 meters away produces a much smaller retinal image than at 2 meters — but we perceive them as the same size. The brain adjusts based on perceived distance.

**Color constancy:** A white shirt in yellow light and in blue light produces very different wavelength patterns at the retina — but you perceive it as white in both cases. The brain corrects for illuminant.

**Shape constancy:** A door from the side looks like a narrow rectangle, from the front like a rectangle, when opening like a trapezoid — you perceive it as a door throughout.

**Why it matters:** Perceptual constancy is why the world feels stable even though your sensory input is constantly changing. Without it, every movement of your head would make the world look like a different place.

## Optical Illusions: The Brain's Vulnerabilities

Optical illusions reveal the brain's perceptual shortcuts — because they work in natural scenes, they fail in artificial ones designed to exploit them.

**The Müller-Lyer illusion:** Two lines of equal length appear different when one has inward-pointing arrowheads and one has outward-pointing ones. The arrows trigger size constancy corrections based on depth cues — and the correction misfires.

**The Checker Shadow illusion:** Two squares on a checkerboard appear to be different shades but are actually the same shade of gray. Color constancy processing (correcting for shadow) makes the brain assign different lightness values to identically colored areas.

**The McGurk Effect:** Hearing the sound "ba" while watching lips say "ga" produces the perception of "da." Neither audio nor visual alone — the brain combines them. Multimodal perception can override individual senses.

> **Aha! moment:** Optical illusions don't go away when you know they're illusions. Knowing the two Müller-Lyer lines are equal doesn't make them look equal. Perception is pre-rational — it operates below the level of deliberate thought.

## Perceptual Learning

Perception improves with experience in a domain. Radiologists perceive patterns in X-rays that novices can't see. Sommeliers detect flavors invisible to untrained palates. Chess masters see board positions that novices see as random.

This is **perceptual learning** — not just knowing more, but literally perceiving differently. The brain's perceptual systems are tuned by experience to extract domain-relevant features.

**Implication:** Expert performance involves not just knowledge and reasoning, but genuinely different sensory processing.

---

## Multisensory Perception

Perception is not one sense at a time — it's a synthesis of all senses simultaneously.

**The rubber hand illusion:** You see a rubber hand being stroked while your hidden real hand is stroked simultaneously. Within minutes, most people feel touch in the rubber hand and proprioceptively "locate" their hand there. Vision captures touch.

**Sound affects taste:** Wine tastes better with music in a minor key. Chips crunch "louder" when headphones play amplified crunch sounds. Food in heavy plates tastes better than food in light plates.

**Vision dominates:** When visual and auditory or tactile signals conflict, vision usually wins — this is called **visual capture**. The ventriloquist effect: we "hear" the dummy's voice because we see the dummy's mouth moving.

---

## Pain Perception: Not Just a Signal

Pain is often described as a pure signal from damaged tissue. It's actually a **construction** by the brain — an interpretation of nociceptive (pain receptor) signals in the context of expectations, attention, and emotion.

**Phantom limb pain:** Amputees feel intense pain in limbs they no longer have. The pain is real — but it originates in the brain's body map (the somatosensory cortex), not the missing limb.

**Nocebo effect:** Expecting a treatment to hurt makes it hurt more. Expecting a placebo to help actually provides measurable pain relief — the brain modulates pain transmission based on expectations.

**Gate control theory (Melzack & Wall):** Pain signals are modulated in the spinal cord by other signals (touch, pressure, emotional state). Rubbing a hurt area reduces pain — touch signals "close the gate" on pain signals. This is why distraction during painful procedures reduces pain — attentional resources gates pain processing.

## Key Terms
- **Sensation:** Detection of raw physical stimuli by sensory receptors
- **Perception:** Brain's active construction of meaningful experience from sensory data
- **Bottom-up processing:** Stimulus-driven perception from sensory data up
- **Top-down processing:** Knowledge-driven perception — prior expectations shape sensory interpretation
- **Gestalt principles:** Rules of perceptual organization (proximity, similarity, closure, etc.)
- **Perceptual constancy:** Perceiving stable object properties despite changing retinal images
- **Visual capture:** Vision's dominance over other senses in multimodal conflict
- **Perceptual learning:** Improved domain-specific perception through experience
