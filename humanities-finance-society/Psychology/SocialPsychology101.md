# Social Psychology 101

> *Put a good person in a bad situation — and what wins? More often than you'd like to admit: the situation.*

---

## The Big Picture

You probably believe your behavior reflects your character — that honest people tell the truth, kind people help others, and brave people resist authority. Social psychology's most disturbing and important finding: **situational factors routinely override character**.

This is called the **Fundamental Attribution Error** — and once you see it, you can't unsee how much the world's behavior makes sense not because of who people are, but because of where they are.

Social psychology is the science of how we think about, influence, and relate to each other. Its experiments are some of the most dramatic in all of science — and its findings are some of the most practically important.

---

## The Fundamental Attribution Error

**Definition:** The tendency to overestimate the role of personality/character and underestimate the role of situation when explaining others' behavior.

**Classic demonstration (Ross, Amabile, Steinmetz, 1977):** Participants watched a quiz show where one person (randomly assigned as "Questioner") asked difficult questions and another (randomly assigned as "Contestant") tried to answer. Observers rated the Questioner as significantly more knowledgeable — even knowing the roles were randomly assigned.

The questioner role gave a structural advantage (you pick questions on your own areas of knowledge), but observers attributed performance to the person, not the role.

**Real-world applications:**
- We assume homeless people are lazy rather than considering structural factors
- We assume successful people are talented rather than considering circumstance and luck
- We assume someone who snapped in anger has a "bad temper" rather than considering what they've been dealing with

**The actor-observer asymmetry:** We explain our own behavior situationally ("I was late because of traffic") but others' behavior dispositionally ("she's always late — she's disorganized"). We're both wrong.

## Conformity: The Power of Others' Behavior

### Asch's Conformity Experiments (1951)

**Solomon Asch** showed participants a simple line-length comparison task — one line vs. three lines, obvious which matched. Then placed them in groups where confederates unanimously gave the wrong answer.

**Result:** 75% of participants conformed to the clearly wrong answer at least once. On average, participants conformed 37% of the time.

**What this shows:** The need to belong and not appear deviant is powerful enough to make people deny the evidence of their own eyes.

**Conditions that reduce conformity:**
- Having even one ally (unanimity matters enormously — even one dissenter dramatically reduces conformity)
- Being able to respond privately rather than publicly
- Having expertise in the domain

### Normative vs. Informational Social Influence
- **Normative influence:** Conforming to fit in, avoid rejection — even if you don't privately believe the group
- **Informational influence:** Conforming because you genuinely believe the group has better information than you

Both are real; informational influence is actually adaptive in situations of genuine uncertainty.

## Obedience to Authority: The Milgram Experiments

**Stanley Milgram's** obedience studies (1961–62) remain the most disturbing experiments in psychology's history.

**Setup:** Participants ("Teachers") were told to administer electric shocks to a "Learner" (a confederate) for incorrect answers. The shock machine went from 15V to 450V ("XXX — Danger: Severe Shock"). The Learner screamed, protested, complained of heart problems, then went silent. An experimenter in a white coat told the Teacher to "please continue."

**Result:** **65%** of participants delivered the maximum 450V shock.

**What Milgram concluded:** Ordinary people, under the right situational pressures, will perform acts of great harm — not because they're monsters, but because they're in a structure that makes harmful behavior seem like following instructions.

**Factors that increased obedience:**
- Authority figure physically present
- Institution's perceived legitimacy (Yale vs. a seedy building)
- Victim remote (in another room vs. same room)

**Factors that decreased obedience:**
- Victim made physically visible/present
- Two researchers visibly disagreed (authority conflict)
- One peer refused first (rebel ally effect)

> **Aha! moment:** The guards at Abu Ghraib, the administrators who signed deportation orders, the ordinary Germans who followed Nazi orders — Milgram's experiments explain this. Not because humans are uniquely evil, but because humans are systematically vulnerable to authority structures.

## The Stanford Prison Experiment: Situational Power

**Philip Zimbardo's** 1971 Stanford Prison Experiment is infamous: college students randomly assigned as "guards" or "prisoners" in a mock prison. Guards became abusive within days; prisoners broke down. The experiment was stopped after 6 days (planned for 2 weeks).

**The conclusion Zimbardo drew:** Situations create roles, and roles create behavior — even sadistic behavior in ordinary people.

**Current reassessment:** The SPE has been heavily criticized — Zimbardo coached the guards, prisoners who didn't break down were excluded from analysis, the study had no proper controls. The findings were real but the interpretation was overstated.

**What holds up:** Social roles and institutional structures genuinely shape behavior — but so do individual character. The person-situation interaction is complex.

## Group Dynamics

### Groupthink
**Irving Janis** analyzed historical policy disasters (Bay of Pigs, Challenger explosion, Iraq WMD assessment) and identified a pattern:

**Groupthink** occurs when cohesive groups prioritize consensus over realistic appraisal. Symptoms:
- Illusion of invulnerability and unanimous agreement
- Collective rationalization of warnings
- Stereotyping of outgroups as weak or evil
- Self-censorship of dissent
- Pressure on dissenters to conform
- Mindguards who filter information to maintain consensus

**Prevention:** Designate a "devil's advocate," invite dissent explicitly, allow anonymous input, invite outside experts who disagree.

### Social Loafing
People **exert less effort** in groups than individually — especially when individual contribution is not identifiable. The bigger the group, the less each person tries.

**Cause:** Diffusion of responsibility + reduced accountability.

**Prevention:** Make individual contributions identifiable; keep group size manageable; build cohesion and shared goals.

### Deindividuation
In large groups, anonymity, and high arousal situations, people lose their individual sense of self and become more likely to follow group behavior — including destructive behavior. This explains mob violence, crowd behavior, and why online anonymity amplifies aggression.

## The Bystander Effect

**Kitty Genovese** was murdered outside her New York apartment in 1964. Newspapers reported that 38 neighbors watched and did nothing.

The Genovese story was embellished — but the psychological phenomenon it inspired is real.

**Darley and Latané's bystander experiments:** When people believe they're the only witness to an emergency, they help ~85% of the time. When they believe five others are also witnessing it: ~31%.

**Two mechanisms:**
1. **Diffusion of responsibility:** "Someone else will help."
2. **Pluralistic ignorance:** "Others don't seem alarmed, so maybe it's not an emergency."

**What this means for you:** If you're in distress and need help in a crowd, don't shout "help!" — point at a specific person and say "You, in the red jacket — call 911." Specificity eliminates diffusion of responsibility.

## Persuasion and Influence

**Robert Cialdini's** six principles of influence (from *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*):

| Principle | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Reciprocity** | We feel obligated to return favors | Free samples increase purchases |
| **Commitment/Consistency** | We want to act consistently with past commitments | Foot-in-the-door: small yes leads to big yes |
| **Social proof** | We look to others in uncertain situations | "Most popular" labels; reviews |
| **Authority** | We defer to experts and credentials | Doctor's white coat; expert endorsements |
| **Liking** | We're more influenced by people we like | Salespeople mirror our body language |
| **Scarcity** | We want what's rare or diminishing | "Only 3 left!"; limited-time offers |

These principles are not tricks — they're heuristics that work correctly most of the time. They're exploitable because they normally point toward good decisions.

## Prejudice and Intergroup Relations

### In-Group vs. Out-Group
Humans categorize others automatically — and favor in-group members. **Tajfel's Minimal Group Paradigm** showed that even arbitrary group assignment (based on coin flip or art preference) produces in-group favoritism.

**Realistic Group Conflict Theory (Sherif's Robbers Cave):** Prejudice and hostility between groups increases when groups compete for limited resources. Hostility decreases when groups must cooperate toward superordinate goals.

### Stereotype Threat
**Claude Steele and Joshua Aronson:** When people are aware of a negative stereotype about their group's performance in a domain, and the task makes that identity salient — their performance measurably drops.

This explains part of test-score gaps across groups: the performance decrement produced by being in a situation where you're aware you're being stereotyped.

**Reduction:** Making stereotypes non-salient; affirmations of non-threatened identities; belonging interventions.

---

## Key Terms
- **Fundamental Attribution Error:** Overweighting personality and underweighting situation when explaining others' behavior
- **Conformity:** Changing behavior or beliefs to match group norms
- **Obedience:** Complying with direct authority commands
- **Groupthink:** Group cohesion at the expense of realistic appraisal
- **Bystander effect:** Reduced helping when others are present; diffusion of responsibility
- **Social loafing:** Reduced individual effort in groups when contributions aren't identifiable
- **Deindividuation:** Loss of individual identity in group contexts; increased likelihood of following group behavior
- **Stereotype threat:** Performance impairment from awareness of negative group stereotypes
