# Attention 101

> *You have about 11 million bits of information entering your senses every second. You're consciously aware of maybe 50. Attention is the editor that decides which 50.*

---

## The Big Picture

Attention is the gateway to all cognition. You cannot perceive, remember, learn, or decide without attention. Yet it's limited, easily hijacked, and operating on mechanisms mostly invisible to your conscious mind.

Understanding attention is understanding why multitasking is a myth, why your phone is engineered to be irresistible, and why focus is becoming one of the most valuable cognitive skills in the modern world.

---

## What Is Attention?

William James wrote in 1890: "Everyone knows what attention is. It is the taking possession by the mind, in clear and vivid form, of one out of what seem several simultaneously possible objects or trains of thought."

He was right that everyone *thinks* they know. The reality is more complex — "attention" is not one thing but a family of related but distinct processes.

---

## Types of Attention

### Selective Attention
The ability to focus on one thing while ignoring others.

**The Cocktail Party Effect (Cherry, 1953):** You can follow one conversation in a noisy room — your brain filters out competing voices. Yet if someone across the room says your name, you hear it — even though you weren't consciously monitoring that stream. The brain is *selectively* processing at multiple levels simultaneously.

**Inattentional blindness:** When attention is fully engaged on one thing, obvious objects in the visual field go unnoticed. **Simons & Chabris' "invisible gorilla" experiment:** participants watching a basketball passing video and counting passes missed a person in a gorilla suit walking through the scene (~50% of participants). You don't see what you're not attending to.

### Sustained Attention (Vigilance)
Maintaining focus over an extended period. Human sustained attention is fundamentally limited — vigilance declines sharply after about 20 minutes on monotonous tasks. This is why air traffic controllers work in short shifts.

### Divided Attention
Attempting to attend to multiple things simultaneously. **True multitasking for cognitive tasks is essentially impossible.** What looks like multitasking is actually rapid *task-switching* — and every switch carries a cost.

### Executive Attention
Top-down, goal-directed control of attention — the ability to deliberately focus on what matters, resist distractions, and shift as needed. Located primarily in the **anterior cingulate cortex** and **prefrontal cortex**. This is what meditation trains.

## The Spotlight Model

Anne Treisman and others developed the **spotlight metaphor**: attention is like a spotlight illuminating a region of the perceptual field. Within the spotlight: rich, detailed processing. Outside: crude, automatic processing.

The spotlight can be **narrow** (focused, high resolution, small area) or **broad** (diffuse, lower resolution, large area). Expert performers in sport, music, and surgery learn to control this flexibly.

**Feature Integration Theory (Treisman):** Basic features (color, shape, orientation) are detected in parallel — automatically, across the whole field. Combining features into objects requires focused attention. This is why a red letter among green letters "pops out" effortlessly, but finding a specific letter-shape-color combination requires deliberate search.

## The Attentional Bottleneck

Cognitive psychology identified that somewhere in the processing chain, information funnels through a bottleneck — a stage where only one thing can be fully processed at a time. This is why:

- You can't follow two conversations simultaneously
- Phone calls impair driving (even hands-free — the conversation demands cognitive bandwidth)
- Reading while listening to a podcast means you process neither fully

**Task-switching cost:** Every time you switch tasks, there's a "resumption lag" — it takes time and cognitive effort to re-engage with the previous task. Estimates suggest it takes ~23 minutes to fully regain deep focus after an interruption.

## Attention and Memory: The Gatekeeper Role

Memory encoding requires attention. **Unattended information is rarely stored.** This is why:

- You can "read" a page and remember nothing (absent-minded reading)
- You forget where you put your keys (didn't attend to the action)
- You can't remember someone's name five seconds after being introduced (attention was on managing the social interaction, not encoding the name)

**The encoding implication:** Depth of processing → strength of memory. Deep attentional processing (semantic, meaningful, self-referential) creates stronger memories than shallow (phonological, visual). You remember what you truly attend to.

## Attention in the Digital Age: The Hijack

Every app, platform, and notification is engineered by teams whose job is to capture and hold your attention — because attention is the product being sold to advertisers.

**Variable reward schedules** (the same mechanism as slot machines): unpredictable rewards (a like, a funny post, a new message) produce compulsive checking behavior because the brain's dopamine system is highly responsive to uncertain, intermittent rewards.

**The consequences:**
- Average attention span in task-switching contexts has compressed
- Deep focus becomes harder with habitual device checking
- Notification culture creates a state of chronic partial attention

**What helps:**
- **Phone in another room** during focused work (not just silent — out of sight)
- **Pomodoro technique** — 25-minute focused blocks with scheduled breaks
- **Single-tasking** — one task, one window, one input
- **Attention training** — mindfulness meditation directly builds the executive attention network

## ADHD: When Attention Is Dysregulated

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder is a neurodevelopmental condition characterized by:
- **Inattention:** Difficulty sustaining attention, easily distracted, forgetful
- **Hyperactivity-Impulsivity:** Fidgeting, excessive talking, difficulty waiting
- **ADHD-Inattentive type:** Primarily attention deficits, less hyperactivity

ADHD is not a deficit of attention per se — people with ADHD can hyperfocus intensely on things they find intrinsically interesting. It's a deficit in **voluntary attention regulation** — difficulty directing attention toward less immediately rewarding activities.

Neurologically: reduced activity in prefrontal cortex and anterior cingulate (the executive attention network); dysregulation of dopamine and norepinephrine systems.

## Mindfulness and Attention Training

**Mindfulness meditation** is essentially **attention training** — specifically training:
1. Sustaining focus on a chosen object (breath, sensation)
2. Noticing when the mind has wandered (meta-awareness)
3. Returning attention without judgment

Research shows 8 weeks of MBSR (Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction) produces measurable changes in:
- Anterior cingulate cortex thickness and activity
- Reduced amygdala reactivity
- Improved scores on sustained attention and executive attention tasks

> **Aha! moment:** Every time your mind wanders during meditation and you return your attention — that's the rep. The wandering isn't failure; it's the training stimulus.

---

## Key Terms
- **Selective attention:** Focusing on one stimulus while filtering others
- **Inattentional blindness:** Failing to notice clearly visible stimuli when attention is elsewhere
- **Task-switching cost:** Time and cognitive effort required to re-engage after interruption
- **Executive attention:** Goal-directed top-down control of attention; PFC/ACC-based
- **Attentional bottleneck:** The processing limit that makes true multitasking impossible
- **Variable reward schedule:** Unpredictable intermittent rewards that drive compulsive behavior — the mechanism apps exploit
