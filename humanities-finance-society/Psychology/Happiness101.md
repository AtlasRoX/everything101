# Happiness 101

> *Happiness is not something that happens to you. It's something you practice — and the things you think will make you happy usually won't.*

---

## The Big Picture

**Affective forecasting** — predicting how future events will make you feel — is something humans do constantly and do badly. Research by **Daniel Gilbert** and **Timothy Wilson** shows we systematically overestimate:
- How happy major positive events will make us (promotion, new relationship, winning money)
- How unhappy major negative events will make us (breakup, job loss, injury)

This is called **impact bias** — we exaggerate the emotional impact of future events.

Understanding happiness scientifically means accepting that your intuitions about what will make you happy are often wrong — and that counterintuitive things actually work.

---

## What Is Happiness? Two Types

Psychologists distinguish two kinds:

**Hedonic well-being:** Positive emotion — feeling good, experiencing pleasure, absence of pain. The day-to-day balance of positive vs. negative affect. "Are you feeling good?"

**Eudaimonic well-being:** Flourishing — living meaningfully, engaging your strengths, growing, contributing to something beyond yourself. "Are you living well?"

These often but don't always go together. A person can have high hedonic well-being (lots of pleasure) but low eudaimonia (no purpose). High eudaimonia often co-occurs with lower hedonic pleasure — meaningful work is often difficult.

**Martin Seligman's PERMA model** (from Positive Psychology) captures both:
- **P**ositive emotion
- **E**ngagement (flow)
- **R**elationships
- **M**eaning
- **A**ccomplishment

A complete life requires more than just feeling good.

## The Hedonic Treadmill

One of the most important findings in happiness research: **we adapt to almost everything**.

**Lottery winners** are not significantly happier one year after winning than matched controls.
**Accident victims** who become paraplegic are not significantly less happy one to three years after their accident than matched controls.

This is **hedonic adaptation** — we rapidly return to a baseline level of happiness after positive or negative events. The treadmill: you keep running (pursuing happiness), but your position (baseline well-being) barely changes.

**What doesn't adapt as much:**
- Chronic noise and commuting (persistent irritants that never fully adapt)
- Social relationships (quality keeps mattering)
- Meaningful engagement (flow states don't fully adapt)
- Health (when health declines significantly, adaptation is incomplete)

**What adapts quickly:**
- Income above a comfort threshold
- Material possessions
- Relationship status (initial boost then adaptation)
- Physical appearance

## The Happiness Set Point

**David Lykken and Auke Tellegen's** twin studies found that ~50% of variation in people's happiness is explained by genetic factors — what they called the **happiness set point**.

This doesn't mean your happiness is fixed. It means there's a range within which environmental factors operate. The set point represents the baseline you return to; it doesn't determine the ceiling or floor.

**Good news:** The non-genetic 50% is substantial. And research identifies specific practices that move people within (and potentially shift) their range.

## What Actually Predicts Happiness

### What Doesn't Work (Much)

**Income above a threshold:** **Ed Diener's** research established that basic poverty correlates with unhappiness (unmet needs cause suffering), but above a moderate income threshold (~$75,000/year in early 2010s US data — recently contested with newer estimates suggesting higher thresholds for some measures), additional income has diminishing returns on emotional well-being.

**The exception:** Higher income continues to predict better *life evaluation* (reflective assessment of your life) even at high incomes — just not day-to-day emotional experience.

**Stuff:** Buying material goods produces a brief pleasure spike followed by quick adaptation. The exception: buying experiences.

### What Actually Works

**1. Experiences over Things (Van Boven & Gilovich)**
Experiential purchases (travel, concerts, learning) produce more lasting happiness than material ones. Why?
- Experiences are harder to compare (you can't Google "was my trip to Nepal better than yours?")
- Experiences become identity (you are someone who traveled to Nepal)
- Experiences are better remembered (adaptation is slower)
- Experiences are more social (shared stories)

**2. Social Relationships — The #1 Predictor**
The **Harvard Study of Adult Development** — the longest running study of adult life (75+ years, 700+ participants) — found that **the quality of close relationships** is the single most important predictor of health and happiness in later life.

Not wealth. Not fame. Not achievement. The warmth and depth of your relationships.

This is the most replicated finding in happiness research. Loneliness is as damaging to health as smoking 15 cigarettes per day (Holt-Lunstad meta-analysis).

**3. Flow and Engagement**
See [Motivation101](./Motivation101.md) — being absorbed in challenging, meaningful work produces high well-being even more reliably than relaxation.

**4. Meaning and Purpose**
Victor Frankl's observation from concentration camp survival: people who found meaning in their suffering survived better than those who didn't. Eudaimonic well-being — having a sense that your life matters, that you contribute, that you're living according to your values — is one of the strongest predictors of life satisfaction.

**5. Gratitude Practice**
**Robert Emmons' research:** Writing down 3 things you're grateful for daily produces measurable increases in happiness and decreases in depression over time — especially if the entries are specific and novel (not the same three things every day).

Why it works: We have a negativity bias — threats and losses are more salient than good things. Gratitude practice counteracts this by deliberately attending to positive experiences.

**6. Acts of Kindness and Generosity**
**Elizabeth Dunn's** research: spending money on others produces more happiness than spending it on yourself — even for small amounts. Volunteering, generosity, and prosocial behavior consistently predict higher well-being.

**7. Mindfulness and Present-Moment Attention**
**Killingsworth and Gilbert's** smartphone study: people's minds wander about 47% of waking hours, and they're less happy when their minds wander, regardless of the activity. Being present — engaged with what you're actually doing — predicts happiness more than what you're doing.

**8. Physical health behaviors**
Sleep, exercise, and nutrition have massive effects on mood. These aren't nice-to-haves — they're the biological substrate of emotional life.

## Positive vs. Negative Emotions: The Ratio

**Barbara Fredrickson's Broaden-and-Build Theory:** Positive emotions don't just feel good — they build resources.
- They broaden attention and thinking (make you more creative, open, and resilient)
- They build lasting resources: social connections, knowledge, psychological resilience
- They "undo" the physiological narrowing of negative emotions (positive emotions speed cardiovascular recovery after stress)

**The negativity bias:** Negative events impact us more than positive ones of equal magnitude (roughly 3:1 ratio). This means positive experiences need to be actively cultivated — they don't carry themselves.

**Fredrickson's 3:1 ratio** (positive to negative emotions for flourishing) has been statistically challenged, but the general principle holds: you need more positive experiences than negative ones because negative ones weigh heavier.

## Happiness Across the Lifespan: The U-Curve

Reported well-being in developed countries follows a U-shaped curve across the lifespan:
- High in youth
- Bottoming out in middle age (late 40s in most countries)
- Rising through later life — peaking in the 70s–80s

**Why are older people happier?** Several mechanisms:
- **Socioemotional selectivity theory (Laura Carstensen):** As people perceive less time remaining, they prioritize emotionally meaningful goals and relationships over achievement and novelty
- Older people show a **positivity bias** in memory (better recall of positive events)
- Reduced social comparison (less competition, more acceptance)
- Greater emotional regulation and equanimity

## The Paradox of Choice

**Barry Schwartz's** research: more choice doesn't increase happiness — beyond a moderate number of options, it decreases it. More options → more imagined alternatives → more regret and second-guessing of actual choices.

**Maximizers** (people who want the best possible option) are less happy than **satisficers** (people who pick something "good enough"). Maximizers spend more time deciding, feel worse about their choices, and experience more regret.

**Practical implication:** Limit your options deliberately. Choose your priorities and commit to them rather than keeping all possibilities open indefinitely.

---

## Key Terms
- **Hedonic adaptation:** The process of returning to a happiness baseline after positive or negative events
- **Affective forecasting:** Predicting how future events will make you feel — systematically inaccurate
- **Impact bias:** Overestimating the emotional impact of future events
- **Set point:** The genetically influenced baseline happiness level people tend to return to
- **Eudaimonia:** Happiness as flourishing and meaning, not just pleasure
- **Hedonic:** Happiness as pleasure and positive emotion
- **Broaden-and-Build:** Fredrickson's theory that positive emotions expand thinking and build lasting resources
- **Socioemotional selectivity:** Older people prioritizing emotionally meaningful goals as time horizon shrinks
