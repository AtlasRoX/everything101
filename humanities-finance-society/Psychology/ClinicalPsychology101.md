# Clinical Psychology 101

> *Mental disorders are not personal failures, character defects, or spiritual problems. They are conditions — with biology, phenomenology, and treatment — like any other medical condition.*

---

## The Big Picture

**Clinical psychology** is the branch of psychology focused on assessing, diagnosing, and treating mental disorders and psychological distress. It's where psychology meets medicine, and where theory meets the suffering of real people.

Understanding clinical psychology helps you: make sense of your own mental health; understand how diagnosis works (and its limitations); know what actually happens in therapy; and reduce the stigma that prevents people from seeking help.

---

## What Is a Mental Disorder?

The **DSM-5** (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th edition) — the primary diagnostic reference in North America — defines a mental disorder as a syndrome characterized by:

1. Clinically significant disturbance in cognition, emotion regulation, or behavior
2. That reflects dysfunction in biological, psychological, or developmental processes
3. That is associated with significant distress or disability in important areas of functioning

**What a mental disorder is NOT:**
- An expectable or culturally approved response to a common stressor (grief, understandable fear)
- Deviance from social or political norms
- A conflict between the individual and society

---

## Diagnosis: The DSM System and Its Limits

The DSM uses **categorical diagnosis** — you either meet criteria for a disorder or you don't (like pregnancy). In reality, most mental health conditions are **dimensional** — they exist on spectra of severity.

**The reliability problem:** The same person presented to different clinicians may receive different diagnoses. Reliability has improved with each DSM edition but remains imperfect.

**The comorbidity problem:** Most people with one mental disorder have at least one more. Major depression + anxiety disorders is the most common combination. This suggests current categories may not carve nature at its joints.

**The cultural limitation:** DSM categories were developed primarily in Western, educated, industrialized, rich, democratic (WEIRD) populations. Some disorders (like anorexia, ADHD) show dramatically different prevalence across cultures; some culture-bound syndromes don't fit existing categories.

Despite its limitations, the DSM provides the shared language clinicians need to communicate, research needs to progress, and insurance systems need to function.

## Major Mental Disorders: A Primer

### Mood Disorders

**Major Depressive Disorder (MDD):** See [Depression101](./Depression101.md) for the full treatment.

**Bipolar Disorder:** Characterized by episodes of **mania** (elevated/expansive mood, decreased need for sleep, grandiosity, racing thoughts, impulsive behavior, goal-directed activity) alternating with depressive episodes.

- **Bipolar I:** Full manic episodes (often requiring hospitalization)
- **Bipolar II:** Hypomanic episodes (less severe) + full depressive episodes
- **Cyclothymia:** Milder mood cycling

Bipolar disorder has ~60–80% heritability — among the highest of any psychiatric condition. Treatment centers on **mood stabilizers** (lithium remains the gold standard), antipsychotics, and therapy.

### Anxiety Disorders

See [Anxiety101](./Anxiety101.md) for the full treatment: GAD, Panic Disorder, Social Anxiety Disorder, Specific Phobias.

### Schizophrenia Spectrum

**Schizophrenia** is one of the most serious and least understood psychiatric conditions.

**Positive symptoms** (additions to normal experience):
- **Hallucinations:** Most commonly auditory (hearing voices) — voices often comment on behavior, give commands, or argue with each other
- **Delusions:** False beliefs held with certainty despite contrary evidence (persecutory delusions; grandiose delusions; thought insertion; thought broadcasting)
- **Disorganized thinking/speech:** Loose associations, derailment

**Negative symptoms** (absences of normal experience):
- Flat affect (reduced emotional expression)
- Alogia (poverty of speech)
- Avolition (lack of motivation)
- Anhedonia

Schizophrenia affects ~1% of the world's population; lifetime. It's highly heritable (~80%). The dopamine hypothesis (overactive D2 receptors in mesolimbic system) was the original theory — partially supported by the fact that D2 blockers (antipsychotics) reduce positive symptoms. Current models involve glutamate dysregulation and neurodevelopmental abnormalities.

### OCD and Related Disorders

**Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder:** Intrusive, unwanted thoughts (obsessions) + repetitive behaviors performed to reduce obsessional anxiety (compulsions). The compulsions provide short-term relief but maintain long-term anxiety by preventing habituation.

**Body Dysmorphic Disorder:** Preoccupation with an imagined or slight defect in appearance, causing significant distress.

**Hoarding Disorder:** Persistent difficulty discarding possessions, regardless of value.

### Trauma-Related Disorders

**PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder):** Following exposure to trauma, characterized by:
- **Re-experiencing:** Flashbacks, nightmares — intrusive trauma memories
- **Avoidance:** Of trauma reminders (places, people, thoughts, feelings)
- **Negative cognitions and mood:** Shame, guilt, emotional numbing, detachment
- **Hyperarousal:** Exaggerated startle response, irritability, sleep disturbance, hypervigilance

The neurobiology: trauma over-consolidates in the amygdala (creating highly reactive fear memories) while disrupting hippocampal contextualization (the memory lacks its time-place context — it feels like the past is happening now).

### Neurodevelopmental Disorders

**ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder):** See [Attention101](./Attention101.md) — dysregulation of voluntary attention and impulse control. Strongly heritable; dopamine and norepinephrine systems involved.

**Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD):** Characterized by persistent deficits in social communication/interaction and restricted, repetitive behavior patterns. Enormous heterogeneity — from minimally verbal to highly verbal individuals. Strong genetic component; multiple converging causes.

### Personality Disorders

Enduring, inflexible patterns of inner experience and behavior that deviate markedly from cultural expectations and cause significant distress or impairment. Organized into three clusters:

- **Cluster A (Odd/Eccentric):** Paranoid, Schizoid, Schizotypal
- **Cluster B (Dramatic/Erratic):** Antisocial, Borderline, Histrionic, Narcissistic
- **Cluster C (Anxious/Fearful):** Avoidant, Dependent, Obsessive-Compulsive

**Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD):** Fear of abandonment, unstable relationships (idealization → devaluation), unstable self-image, impulsivity, self-harm, emotional dysregulation. Highly treatable with DBT.

## Psychological Assessment

Clinical psychologists use multiple methods:

**Clinical interview:** Structured or unstructured; the primary tool. The clinical interview alone outperforms most tests for most conditions.

**Structured diagnostic interviews:** SCID (Structured Clinical Interview for DSM) — standardized questions for each diagnostic criterion.

**Psychometric tests:**
- **MMPI (Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory):** 567-item self-report assessing personality and psychopathology
- **BDI (Beck Depression Inventory):** 21-item self-report measuring depression severity
- **Intelligence tests:** WAIS (Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale) for cognitive assessment

**Neuropsychological testing:** Assessing cognitive function after brain injury, dementia screening.

## The Major Therapy Approaches

### Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
The most extensively researched psychotherapy. Addresses the relationship between thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. Structured, time-limited (typically 12–20 sessions), present-focused.

*Most evidence-based for:* Depression, anxiety disorders, OCD, PTSD, eating disorders.

### Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)
Developed by **Marsha Linehan** specifically for BPD. Combines CBT with mindfulness and dialectical philosophy (holding opposite truths simultaneously).

Four skill modules: **Mindfulness, Distress Tolerance, Emotion Regulation, Interpersonal Effectiveness**.

*Most evidence-based for:* BPD, self-harm, suicidality, emotional dysregulation.

### EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)
Developed by **Francine Shapiro**. While watching a moving stimulus (originally finger movements), clients recall traumatic memories. Proposed mechanism: bilateral stimulation facilitates reprocessing of traumatic memories; reduces emotional charge.

*Most evidence-based for:* PTSD. Mechanism remains debated — may work through distraction/exposure rather than the specific bilateral element.

### Psychodynamic Therapy
Derived from psychoanalytic tradition. Explores unconscious patterns, early relational experiences, and how they manifest in current relationships (including the therapist-patient relationship — "transference").

*Most evidence-based for:* Personality pathology, chronic interpersonal difficulties, depression with complex etiology.

### Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)
Third-wave CBT. Not about changing thoughts — about changing your *relationship* to thoughts. Emphasizes psychological flexibility, acceptance, values-based action.

*Most evidence-based for:* Chronic pain, anxiety, depression, substance use.

### Pharmacotherapy (Medication)

Clinical psychologists in most jurisdictions cannot prescribe medication — that's the domain of psychiatrists. But collaboration is essential.

The major classes:
- **Antidepressants** (SSRIs, SNRIs, tricyclics, MAOIs) — depression, anxiety, OCD, PTSD
- **Antipsychotics** — schizophrenia, bipolar, severe depression
- **Mood stabilizers** (lithium, valproate, lamotrigine) — bipolar disorder
- **Benzodiazepines** — short-term anxiety; avoid long-term
- **Stimulants** — ADHD

## The Effectiveness of Psychotherapy

Meta-analyses consistently show psychotherapy works:
- Effect size of ~0.80 compared to no treatment — equivalent to an antidepressant for mild-to-moderate depression
- The person who receives therapy is better off than ~79% of untreated people
- **The dodo bird verdict:** Most evidence-based therapies produce similar outcomes — the specific technique matters less than the therapeutic alliance (the quality of the client-therapist relationship)

The "therapeutic alliance" — the collaborative, trusting relationship between client and therapist — accounts for more outcome variance than the specific technique used. This is consistently replicated across modalities.

## Key Terms
- **DSM-5:** Diagnostic and Statistical Manual — the categorical diagnostic system for mental disorders
- **Comorbidity:** The co-occurrence of two or more mental disorders in the same person
- **Psychosis:** A state involving lost contact with reality — hallucinations and/or delusions
- **Mania:** An abnormally elevated, expansive, or irritable mood with increased energy — characteristic of bipolar disorder
- **Therapeutic alliance:** The quality of the collaborative relationship between client and therapist — the most powerful predictor of outcome
- **DBT:** Dialectical Behavior Therapy — evidence-based treatment for BPD and emotional dysregulation
- **EMDR:** Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing — evidence-based trauma treatment
