# Evolutionary Psychology 101

> *Your brain was shaped by selection pressures that operated over millions of years in environments radically different from the one you live in now. Understanding this gap explains a lot of modern suffering.*

---

## The Big Picture

**Evolutionary psychology** applies Darwinian principles to the mind. The premise: just as the eye evolved to see and the heart evolved to pump blood, the brain's information-processing mechanisms evolved to solve specific adaptive problems — survival, reproduction, kin cooperation, coalition-building.

Your fears, desires, social emotions, cognitive biases, and even mental disorders make more sense when viewed through this lens.

**The central argument:** The brain is not a general-purpose reasoning machine. It contains **evolved psychological mechanisms** — specialized, domain-specific processes that evolved to solve specific problems faced by ancestral humans.

---

## The Core Logic: Natural Selection and the Mind

**Natural selection** operates when:
1. There is **variation** in a trait
2. The variation is **heritable**
3. The variation affects **reproductive success**

Any psychological trait that consistently caused its carriers to survive and reproduce more would spread. Traits that caused harm to fitness would diminish.

**But selection operates on ancestral environments.** The environment in which most human evolution occurred — the **Environment of Evolutionary Adaptedness (EEA)** — is roughly the Pleistocene African savanna (approximately 2.5 million to ~12,000 years ago): small hunter-gatherer bands, no agriculture, face-to-face social groups of ~150, constant caloric uncertainty, predators.

Our brains were shaped for that world. We live in this one.

## Evolved Psychological Mechanisms: Examples

### Fear and Phobias

We are **biologically prepared** to acquire fears of ancestral threats faster and more durably than fears of modern threats.

**Seligman's preparedness theory:** Snakes, spiders, heights, and open spaces generate fear responses far more easily than cars, electrical outlets, and firearms — which are statistically far more dangerous.

The ancestral logic is clear: fear of snakes and spiders was adaptive for millions of years. Fear of cars has had no evolutionary time to be selected for.

This explains why phobias cluster around evolutionarily relevant stimuli — and why fear of public speaking is so common and so intense (public humiliation and social rejection in small groups was life-threatening).

### Food Preferences

**Preference for sweet, fatty, and salty foods** — the "unhealthy" preferences — were adaptive in the EEA where these nutrients were scarce and caloric reserves were survival-critical.

In the EEA, you ate sugar when you found it (rare). Now you can eat it indefinitely. The mechanism that said "eat more sweet things" is unchanged, but the environment has made it pathological.

This is the "mismatch hypothesis" — the gap between evolved mechanisms and modern environments.

### Kin Selection and Altruism

**Hamilton's rule:** Altruism evolves when the cost to the altruist is less than the benefit to the recipient, weighted by genetic relatedness: *rB > C*.

We are disproportionately altruistic toward genetic relatives. We sacrifice more for our children than our nephews; more for our nephews than neighbors; more for neighbors than strangers — roughly tracking degree of genetic relatedness.

**Inclusive fitness:** Selection doesn't maximize personal reproductive success — it maximizes the spread of shared genes. Helping your siblings reproduce spreads your genes even without your own reproduction.

### Reciprocal Altruism

**Robert Trivers** (1971): Altruism toward non-kin can evolve if there are opportunities for return favors — if you help me today and I help you tomorrow.

This requires: repeated interaction; ability to recognize individuals; ability to detect and punish cheaters.

Humans are extraordinarily equipped for all three. We have **dedicated cheater detection circuits** — we're much better at logical problems framed as "detect who is violating a social contract" than the same logic problem framed neutrally (Cosmides & Tooby's Wason selection task results).

We feel powerful emotions when we detect cheaters: **moral outrage** — which motivates costly punishment even when it doesn't benefit the punisher. This is what enforces reciprocal altruism in large societies.

### Mate Preferences: Parental Investment Theory

**Robert Trivers' Parental Investment Theory:** The sex that invests more in offspring will be choosier about mates; the sex that invests less will compete more intensely for access to the high-investing sex.

In humans, females have higher obligatory parental investment (internal fertilization, 9-month gestation, nursing) — so the theory predicts females will be choosier about long-term mates and males will compete more intensely.

**Cross-cultural evidence (Buss, 1989):** Survey of 37 cultures found:
- **Men** universally place higher value on female **physical attractiveness** and **youth** (cues to fertility) in long-term partners
- **Women** universally place higher value on male **resource acquisition** and **status** (cues to ability to provision offspring) in long-term partners

Both sexes prioritize **intelligence and kindness** above appearance for long-term relationships — features relevant to parenting quality.

**Caveats:** These are average tendencies across populations; individual variation is enormous; cultural context modifies expression significantly; homosexual mate preferences present a complex picture.

## The Mismatch Hypothesis: Why We're Unhappy

The single most powerful practical takeaway from evolutionary psychology: **many features of modern life systematically mismatch our evolved psychology**.

| Modern Feature | Ancestral Design | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Caloric abundance | Eat when available (scarcity) | Obesity, metabolic syndrome |
| Social media status | Small-group status (150 people) | Social anxiety, endless comparison |
| Pornography | Actual mating opportunities | Desensitization, relationship harm |
| 24-hour news | Threat monitoring (local events) | Chronic anxiety, tribalism |
| Sedentary work | High-activity lifestyle | Depression, cardiovascular disease |
| Nuclear family isolation | Alloparental community | Parental burnout, loneliness |
| Artificial light/late hours | Daylight-governed sleep | Disrupted circadian rhythms, disease |
| Chronic stress without resolution | Short-term acute threats | HPA dysregulation, chronic disease |

The mismatch hypothesis doesn't tell you to "live like a caveman." It helps explain *why* certain modern conditions cause harm — and points toward interventions that address root mismatches.

## Status, Hierarchy, and the Social Brain

Human ancestors lived in hierarchical social groups. Status mattered enormously — for resource access, mate access, and survival. As a result:

- We are **obsessively sensitive** to status signals
- Social **status threats** activate the same brain circuits as physical threats
- We track relative standing in social hierarchies automatically and continuously
- **Serotonin levels** are affected by perceived social standing — dominants have higher serotonin; subordinates have lower

The social brain hypothesis (Dunbar): Human intelligence evolved primarily to manage complex social relationships, not for tool use or other ecological challenges. **Dunbar's Number** (~150) is the cognitive limit of stable social relationships our brains can maintain.

Modern environments give us nominal "relationships" with thousands — but the emotional circuitry for deep trust, reciprocity, and connection can only manage ~150. Social media creates a mismatch between social visibility and social connectedness.

## Evolutionary Psychology Controversies

Evolutionary psychology is a contentious field. Legitimate criticisms:

**The "just-so story" problem:** It's easy to construct evolutionary narratives after the fact for any observed behavior. Without rigorous testing, this becomes unfalsifiable storytelling.

**The fallacy of naturalism:** "It evolved this way" does not imply "it should be this way." Evolved tendencies toward tribalism, status hierarchies, and jealousy don't justify them.

**Cultural variation:** Cross-cultural data shows significant variation in behavior that evolutionary psychology sometimes downplays. The universality of some findings is contested.

**Legitimate responses:** Good evolutionary psychology uses comparative methods, cross-cultural data, developmental evidence, and genetic studies to test specific predictions. It does not use evolution to justify existing social arrangements.

## What Evolutionary Psychology Explains Well

- Clustering of phobias around ancestral threats
- Universal facial expressions for basic emotions (Ekman's cross-cultural work)
- Kin-based favoritism in altruism
- Reciprocal altruism and cheater detection
- General patterns in mate preferences across cultures
- Why sugar, fat, and salt taste good
- Social anxiety and sensitivity to rejection
- Status sensitivity and competitive behavior
- Tribalism and in-group/out-group psychology

---

## Key Terms
- **Environment of Evolutionary Adaptedness (EEA):** The ancestral environment our psychology evolved in (~Pleistocene Africa)
- **Evolved psychological mechanism:** A domain-specific cognitive process shaped by natural selection to solve an adaptive problem
- **Mismatch hypothesis:** Modern suffering often results from evolved mechanisms operating in environments they weren't designed for
- **Inclusive fitness:** Maximizing the spread of shared genes, including through helping genetic relatives
- **Reciprocal altruism:** Helping non-kin when future reciprocation is likely
- **Parental investment theory:** Higher-investing sex is choosier about mates; lower-investing sex competes more
- **Preparedness:** Biological predisposition to acquire certain fears more easily than others
- **Dunbar's Number:** ~150 — the cognitive limit of stable social relationships
