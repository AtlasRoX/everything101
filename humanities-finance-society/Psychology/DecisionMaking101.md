# Decision Making 101

> *Every day you make thousands of decisions. Most of them feel rational. Most of them aren't — and understanding why is one of the most practically valuable things psychology offers.*

---

## The Big Picture

Classical economics assumed humans are **rational agents** — consistently choosing options that maximize their expected utility, with complete information, unbiased calculation, and stable preferences.

Every single one of those assumptions is wrong.

**Behavioral economics** — built largely on the work of Kahneman and Tversky — documents the systematic, predictable ways human decision-making departs from rationality. This isn't just academic. These biases affect medical decisions, financial choices, legal judgments, and every domain where stakes are high.

---

## The Two-System Model

See [Bias101](./Bias101.md) for the full treatment — the core framework:

**System 1:** Fast, automatic, emotional, unconscious. Generates intuitive judgments effortlessly.

**System 2:** Slow, deliberate, logical, effortful. Can override System 1 — but doesn't always bother.

Most decisions are made by System 1. System 2 often serves as a **rationalization machine** — generating reasons for decisions that System 1 already made.

> **Aha! moment:** Jonathan Haidt's "social intuitionist model" suggests that moral decisions are made intuitively and instantly — then reasoned about afterward. The reasoning doesn't produce the judgment; it justifies it. We feel we're making rational decisions; we're largely narrating emotional ones.

## Prospect Theory: How We Actually Value Outcomes

**Kahneman and Tversky's Prospect Theory** (1979) is the most important descriptive model of decision-making under risk.

### Loss Aversion

The central finding: **losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good** — by roughly a factor of 2.

The pain of losing $100 feels about twice as strong as the pleasure of gaining $100. This asymmetry explains:

- Why people hold losing stocks (selling feels like realizing the loss)
- Why people take unnecessary risks to avoid losses ("I can't lose this")
- Why insurance is purchased even at negative expected value (loss avoidance)
- Why the pain of a price increase matters more than the joy of an equivalent discount

### The S-Shaped Value Function

Our subjective experience of value is not linear:

```
Subjective
 Value (↑)
      |          /──────────────
      |        /
      |      /
      |    /
 ─────|──/──────────────────── Objective Value (→)
      |/ ←── Reference point (current state)
      |\
       \
        \───────────────────
```

- **Reference-dependence:** We evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point (usually the status quo), not in absolute terms
- **Diminishing sensitivity:** Each additional unit of gain or loss produces less additional subjective impact (the difference between winning $10 and $20 feels bigger than between $1,000 and $1,010)
- **Loss aversion:** The loss curve is steeper than the gain curve

### Risk Attitudes Under Prospect Theory

- **For gains:** We are **risk-averse** — prefer a certain $50 to a 50% chance of $100
- **For losses:** We are **risk-seeking** — prefer a 50% chance of losing $100 to a certain loss of $50

This explains the "gambler's fallacy" behavior of people who've already lost significantly — they take bigger risks to "get back to zero" rather than accepting the sure (smaller) loss.

## Framing Effects in Decisions

The same choice presented differently produces different decisions.

**Asian disease problem (Kahneman & Tversky):**
> Program A: 200 people saved (of 600). Program B: 1/3 probability all 600 saved, 2/3 probability nobody saved.

When framed as **gains** (lives saved): 72% prefer the certain Program A.

> Program C: 400 people will die. Program D: 1/3 probability nobody dies, 2/3 probability all 600 die.

When framed as **losses** (lives lost): 78% prefer the risky Program D.

**Same outcomes. Different frames. Opposite decisions.** Rational choice theory says this shouldn't happen — preferences should be independent of presentation.

## Mental Accounting

**Richard Thaler's** concept: people divide money into separate "mental accounts" and apply different rules to each.

- Money won gambling is spent more readily than money earned from salary (same purchasing power; different mental source)
- The sunk cost fallacy: "I've already paid for the ticket" — the money is in a "sunk" account that feels like it must be "used"
- Windfall money is spent differently than income (though both are fungible)
- Losses in one domain are less painful when "offset" by gains in another (portfolio managers evaluate assets together, not separately)

## Choice Architecture and Nudges

**Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein's nudge theory:** Small changes in how choices are presented (the "choice architecture") predictably influence decisions — without restricting options.

**Defaults are powerful.** Because of status quo bias, the default option gets chosen disproportionately:
- Organ donation: opt-out countries have ~90%+ donation rates vs. opt-in countries at ~10-15%
- 401(k) enrollment: auto-enrollment → far higher participation
- Cafeteria layout: placing healthier foods first → more healthy choices

**Nudges are now standard government policy** in the UK (Behavioural Insights Team), the US (White House Social and Behavioral Sciences Team), and many other countries — using behavioral science to improve public health, savings, and energy use without legislation.

## The Paradox of Choice

**Barry Schwartz's** paradox: more options don't produce better decisions or more satisfaction. Above a moderate number of options, more choice:
- Increases decision difficulty
- Increases post-decision regret ("maybe I should have picked the other one")
- Decreases satisfaction with the chosen option
- Increases choice avoidance (not deciding at all)

**Jam study (Iyengar & Lepper):** A grocery store offering 24 jam varieties attracted more tasters but produced 1/10th the sales of a table offering 6 varieties.

## Temporal Discounting: The Present Bias

We systematically prefer immediate rewards over delayed ones — even when the delayed reward is objectively larger. This is **hyperbolic discounting**.

"Would you rather have $50 now or $100 in a year?" Most people take $50. "Would you rather have $50 in 5 years or $100 in 6 years?" Most people wait for $100.

Same gap (1 year), different time frame — different choice. This is irrational by standard economic theory.

**Why this matters:**
- Retirement savings: the future feels abstract; spending feels immediate
- Health behaviors: exercise costs effort now; benefits accrue later
- Climate change: present costs vs. future benefits
- Addiction: immediate reward vs. long-term harm

**Commitment devices** address this: mechanisms that bind your future self to a decision your present self makes. Ulysses ordering himself tied to the mast before hearing the Sirens. A retirement plan that automatically deducts before you see the money. A friend you've told your goal to.

## Naturalistic Decision Making

**Gary Klein's** research on how experts actually decide in high-stakes, time-pressured situations (fireground commanders, ICU nurses, military commanders) found they rarely use analysis:

**Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) model:** Experts don't compare options. They recognize the situation as similar to previous experiences, mentally simulate whether the obvious action would work, and if yes — act.

Expert decision-making is fast and pattern-based, not slow and analytical. The expertise is in the quality of pattern recognition, not the ability to run analyses.

**Implication:** Decision quality depends heavily on the quality of experience — and the quality of feedback from that experience. Experts who receive delayed or ambiguous feedback (psychiatrists predicting recidivism, stock pickers) don't develop reliable intuition. Experts with immediate, clear feedback (chess players, firefighters) do.

---

## When to Trust Intuition vs. Analysis

| Situation | Use | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Expert in familiar domain, good feedback, time-pressured | Intuition | Pattern recognition is reliable and fast |
| Novel problem, no feedback, high stakes | Analysis | Intuition has no reliable patterns to draw from |
| Emotionally loaded (loss aversion, fear) | Analysis | Emotions distort intuition systematically |
| Choosing between few options | Deliberate comparison | Analysis adds value |
| Choosing between many complex options | Intuitive synthesis | "Sleep on it" — unconscious integration often beats analysis |

## Key Terms
- **Prospect theory:** The descriptive model showing we evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point, with loss aversion and diminishing sensitivity
- **Loss aversion:** Losses hurt ~2x more than equivalent gains feel good
- **Framing effect:** Identical information presented differently produces different decisions
- **Mental accounting:** Treating money differently based on its source or intended category
- **Nudge:** A change in choice architecture that predictably influences behavior without restricting options
- **Temporal discounting / present bias:** Preferring smaller immediate rewards over larger delayed ones
- **Commitment device:** A mechanism that binds future behavior to a current decision
- **Recognition-primed decision:** Expert decision-making via pattern recognition, not analysis
