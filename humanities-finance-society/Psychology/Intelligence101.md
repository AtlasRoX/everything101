# Intelligence 101

> *IQ tests measure something real. But "intelligence" is far stranger, more plural, and more malleable than any single number can capture.*

---

## The Big Picture

Intelligence is one of the most studied, most debated, and most politically charged topics in psychology.

On one hand, the research is clear: cognitive ability measured by standardized tests predicts real-world outcomes — academic achievement, career success, health behaviors, even longevity — better than almost any other measurable trait.

On the other hand, what we call "intelligence" may be a family of distinct abilities that we've somewhat arbitrarily bundled together. And the question of what drives differences in measured intelligence — genes, environment, culture, testing bias — is deeply contested.

This article gives you the science honestly: what we know, what we don't, and what the debates are actually about.

---

## What Is Intelligence?

In 1994, 52 leading intelligence researchers signed a statement in *The Wall Street Journal* with this definition:

> "Intelligence is a very general mental capability that, among other things, involves the ability to reason, plan, solve problems, think abstractly, comprehend complex ideas, learn quickly, and learn from experience."

Key features of this definition:
- **General** — not specific to one domain
- **Mental capability** — cognitive, not physical or social
- **Involves learning** — adapting to new situations, not just recalling stored knowledge

## A Brief History of Intelligence Testing

### Francis Galton: The Wrong Start

**Francis Galton** (1822–1911) — Darwin's half-cousin — believed intelligence was largely hereditary and measurable through simple sensory tests (reaction time, grip strength). He invented the concept of measuring intelligence quantitatively. He also founded **eugenics** — the catastrophically harmful movement to "improve" humanity through selective breeding. His science was often sloppy; his ideology was vicious.

### Binet and the Practical Test

**Alfred Binet** (1857–1911) took a different approach. Asked by the French government to identify schoolchildren who needed extra help, he and Théodore Simon created the **Binet-Simon Scale** (1905) — the first practical intelligence test.

Binet explicitly warned against using his test to rank people or make permanent judgments. He designed it as a practical educational tool. His warnings were ignored.

### The IQ Score

**William Stern** introduced the **Intelligence Quotient (IQ)** in 1912:

**IQ = (Mental Age / Chronological Age) × 100**

If a 10-year-old performed like a typical 12-year-old, their IQ was 120.

Modern IQ tests use a **deviation IQ** — your score relative to your age group, standardized so that 100 is always the mean and 15 is one standard deviation:

| IQ Range | Description | % of Population |
|---|---|---|
| 130+ | Very Superior | 2.2% |
| 120–129 | Superior | 6.7% |
| 110–119 | High Average | 16.1% |
| 90–109 | Average | 50% |
| 80–89 | Low Average | 16.1% |
| 70–79 | Borderline | 6.7% |
| <70 | Extremely Low | 2.2% |

## The g Factor: General Intelligence

In 1904, statistician **Charles Spearman** made an important observation: performance on different cognitive tests tends to correlate. If you score high on verbal tests, you tend to score high on math tests, spatial tests, and memory tests too.

This led him to propose a general factor — **g** — that underlies performance across all cognitive domains.

**The g factor is the most replicated finding in psychology.** It's not controversial among researchers that g exists as a statistical reality — something that cognitive tests share variance over.

What's debated: what g actually *is* biologically, and how much of human variation it explains.

### Modern Intelligence Structure: CHC Theory

The most widely accepted current model is **Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) Theory**, which proposes a hierarchical structure:

```
                    ┌────────────────┐
                    │       g        │  ← General Intelligence
                    └────────────────┘
                           │
       ┌───────────────────┼───────────────────┐
       │                   │                   │
   ┌───┴───┐           ┌───┴───┐           ┌───┴───┐
   │  Gf   │           │  Gc   │           │  Gs   │ ...more
   └───────┘           └───────┘           └───────┘
  Fluid             Crystallized          Processing
  Intelligence      Intelligence          Speed
```

**Fluid Intelligence (Gf):** The ability to reason and solve novel problems without relying on prior knowledge. "Pure" reasoning ability. Peaks in early adulthood, declines with age.

**Crystallized Intelligence (Gc):** Accumulated knowledge and verbal ability built through education and experience. The "library" in your head. Increases through middle age, relatively stable into older adulthood.

**Working Memory Capacity:** The ability to hold and manipulate information in mind simultaneously. Strongly predictive of academic and professional success.

**Processing Speed:** How quickly you can process simple information. Declines markedly with age.

## Does IQ Predict Anything?

Yes — substantially. This is one of the most robust findings in psychology:

| Outcome | Correlation with IQ |
|---|---|
| Academic achievement | ~0.50–0.60 |
| Job performance (complex jobs) | ~0.50–0.60 |
| Job performance (simple jobs) | ~0.20–0.30 |
| Income | ~0.30–0.40 |
| Educational attainment | ~0.55 |
| Occupational status | ~0.50 |
| Crime (negative correlation) | ~-0.20 |
| Health outcomes | ~0.20–0.30 |

**Note on correlations:** A correlation of 0.50 means IQ explains about 25% of the variation in that outcome. This is large by social science standards but leaves enormous room for other factors — motivation, personality, opportunity, luck, specific skills.

> **Aha! moment:** IQ is a significant predictor of outcomes — not because smart people deserve more, but because the modern economy heavily rewards the specific cognitive abilities these tests measure. This is a fact about societies, not about inherent human worth.

## The Nature-Nurture Debate on Intelligence

This is where the science gets politically charged. Here's what the evidence actually shows:

### Heritability

**Twin studies** (comparing identical twins raised together vs. apart, vs. fraternal twins) consistently find that IQ heritability in adults is approximately **50–80%** in developed countries.

**What heritability means:** Not that 50–80% of your IQ comes from genes. It means that in the *population studied*, 50–80% of the *variation* in IQ scores can be attributed to genetic differences.

**What heritability does NOT mean:**
- That IQ is fixed or unchangeable
- That environmental interventions can't matter
- That group differences in IQ are genetic

> **The heritability fallacy:** "IQ is 70% heritable" does NOT mean genes determine 70% of your intelligence. A population of plants grown in a greenhouse in identical conditions might show 100% heritability for height (all variation is genetic) — but the same plants given better soil all grow taller. Heritability describes a population in specific conditions, not an absolute limit.

### The Flynn Effect

**James Flynn** discovered in 1984 that **IQ scores have risen substantially over time** — roughly 3 IQ points per decade in developed countries since testing began.

In one generation, average IQ in developing countries can rise 10–20 points. This cannot be genetic — genes don't change that fast.

Proposed causes:
- Better nutrition (especially iodine and iron)
- More formal schooling (tests measure school-like thinking)
- Reduced exposure to neurotoxins (lead removal from paint and gasoline)
- Increased cognitive complexity of daily life

The Flynn Effect proves IQ is significantly plastic — responsive to environmental conditions. It also complicates comparisons between groups in different environmental conditions.

### Group Differences in IQ

This is the most politically sensitive area of intelligence research. The honest summary:

**Observed differences:** Average test scores differ across demographic groups in the U.S. and globally. The Black-white gap in the U.S. has been about 10–15 IQ points on average (though it has narrowed substantially since the 1970s). Gaps also exist between socioeconomic groups within every racial category.

**What explains these gaps?**

The scientific consensus is that **observed group differences are substantially or entirely environmental** in origin:
- Historical exclusion from quality education
- Socioeconomic deprivation (poverty affects brain development)
- Stereotype threat (awareness of negative stereotypes measurably reduces test performance)
- Nutritional deficits
- Environmental toxin exposure (lead, air pollution are more prevalent in low-income areas)
- Test bias (tests developed by and normed on particular cultural groups)

**The argument for genetic causes** — most prominently made by Richard Herrnstein and Charles Murray in *The Bell Curve* (1994) — is rejected by mainstream behavioral genetics for multiple reasons, including that the Flynn Effect alone demonstrates environmental factors can drive the magnitude of observed gaps.

The American Psychological Association's official position: the causes of group differences in IQ are **not known** but environmental explanations are well-supported and genetic explanations lack evidence.

## Multiple Intelligences: A Useful Myth?

In 1983, developmental psychologist **Howard Gardner** proposed **Theory of Multiple Intelligences (MI)** — arguing there are at least 8 distinct intelligences:

1. Linguistic
2. Logical-Mathematical
3. Spatial
4. Musical
5. Bodily-Kinesthetic
6. Interpersonal
7. Intrapersonal
8. Naturalistic

MI theory became enormously popular in education — validating students who weren't strong in traditional academic subjects.

**The scientific critique:** Most intelligence researchers don't accept MI as a theory of intelligence. Gardner's "intelligences" correlate with each other (suggesting a common underlying g factor), and they include things traditionally called "talents" or "skills" rather than intelligence. There's little empirical evidence that teaching to different "intelligences" improves learning.

**What's valid about MI:** The idea that cognitive abilities are plural and different people have different strength profiles is true. But this doesn't require abandoning the g factor — it's captured by models like CHC theory.

## Emotional Intelligence (EQ)

**Daniel Goleman**'s 1995 book *Emotional Intelligence* proposed that EQ — the ability to recognize, understand, and manage emotions in self and others — was "more important than IQ" for life success.

**The research reality:**
- Emotional intelligence is real and measurable
- The ability to perceive and manage emotions predicts important outcomes, especially in social and leadership contexts
- But EQ does not "matter more than IQ" as a global claim — the evidence doesn't support Goleman's strong version
- EQ and IQ are partially independent — having both is better than either alone

## What Intelligence Is NOT

**Intelligence is NOT:**
- Wisdom (good judgment about important matters)
- Creativity (though correlated above a threshold)
- Character (honesty, courage, compassion)
- Emotional regulation
- Practical skill (knowing how to actually do things)
- Motivation or conscientiousness

High intelligence without these other qualities produces people who are very clever and sometimes very harmful. The history of atrocities is full of intelligent people making brilliant arguments for monstrous policies.

## Can You Increase Intelligence?

### Working Memory Training

Several programs claim that training working memory increases general intelligence. Meta-analyses are skeptical — training improves performance on the trained tasks but **does not generalize** to broad intelligence improvement.

### Physical Exercise

Aerobic exercise robustly improves **executive function** and working memory, promotes neurogenesis, and is associated with better cognitive outcomes over a lifetime. This is not a modest effect — exercise is one of the most powerful cognitive enhancers known.

### Sleep

Sleep deprivation directly impairs IQ-equivalent performance. Adequate sleep (7–9 hours for adults) is essential for cognitive function, memory consolidation, and insight.

### Nutrition (Especially Early Life)

Severe malnutrition, iodine deficiency, and iron deficiency in early childhood (especially prenatal and first 3 years) cause measurable, lasting IQ reductions. Adequate nutrition during development is the most important intelligence intervention available.

### Education

Formal schooling genuinely increases IQ scores — by approximately **1–5 IQ points per year of schooling** in some estimates. This is not just learning the content of tests — it's developing the abstract reasoning skills that tests measure.

### Lead and Environmental Toxins

Lead exposure, particularly in early childhood, measurably reduces IQ. The removal of lead from gasoline and paint in developed countries from the 1970s onward is estimated to have raised average IQ by 4–5 points — and may explain part of the Flynn Effect.

## Intelligence and Creativity: The Threshold Hypothesis

The relationship between intelligence and creativity is non-linear:

- **Below ~IQ 120:** Higher IQ is associated with higher creativity
- **Above ~IQ 120:** The relationship weakens or disappears

Above a threshold of general cognitive ability, other factors — personality openness, intrinsic motivation, domain expertise, tolerance for ambiguity — matter more for creative achievement than raw IQ.

This explains why many of history's most creative people were highly intelligent but not necessarily the most intelligent people in their fields.

## Key Terms

- **g factor:** General intelligence — the statistical factor underlying performance across cognitive domains
- **Fluid intelligence (Gf):** Reasoning ability with novel problems, independent of prior knowledge
- **Crystallized intelligence (Gc):** Accumulated knowledge and verbal ability from experience
- **Flynn Effect:** The observed rise in IQ scores across generations — proof of substantial environmental influence on intelligence
- **Heritability:** The proportion of variation in a trait attributable to genetic differences in a specific population — NOT the genetic "ceiling" of an individual
- **Working memory:** The ability to hold and manipulate information in mind simultaneously — one of the best predictors of g
- **Threshold hypothesis:** Above a moderate IQ level, personality and motivation matter more than raw intelligence for creative achievement
